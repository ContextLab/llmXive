# Systematic Assessment of Non-Coding Variant Effects on Transcription Factor Binding Affinities

**Field**: biology

## Research question

Do common non-coding single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in the human genome systematically disrupt transcription factor (TF) binding motifs in a manner that correlates with known disease-associated loci, and which specific TFs exhibit the highest sensitivity to such allelic variation?

## Motivation

A significant portion of disease-associated variants identified in Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) reside in non-coding regions, yet their functional mechanisms remain largely uncharacterized. By quantifying the predicted impact of common SNPs on TF binding affinities using established position weight matrices, this study aims to bridge the gap between statistical genetic associations and biological regulatory mechanisms, prioritizing variants for experimental follow-up.

## Related work

- [Competitive binding of transcription factors drives Mendelian dominance in regulatory genetic pathways (2016)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.06668) — Establishes a biophysical model demonstrating how competitive binding dynamics can lead to allelic dominance, providing a theoretical framework for interpreting how single base changes alter regulatory output.
- [Negative selection maintains transcription factor binding motifs in human cancer (2015)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.02842) — Demonstrates that mutations disrupting TF binding sites are subject to negative selection, validating the functional importance of preserving these motifs in human genomes.
- [The Functional Consequences of Variation in Transcription Factor Binding (2013)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1310.5166) — Reviews the mechanisms by which genetic variation in binding sites alters gene expression programs, highlighting the need for systematic scoring of variant effects across the genome.
- [Adaptive evolution of transcription factor binding sites (2003)](https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0301574) — Investigates the evolutionary generation of binding sites and cooperativity, offering context on how binding affinity landscapes have been shaped by natural selection.

## Expected results

We expect to identify a subset of transcription factors whose binding motifs are disproportionately disrupted by common SNPs compared to random genomic background, suggesting these factors are critical nodes in regulatory networks vulnerable to genetic variation. The analysis should reveal a statistically significant enrichment of these high-sensitivity TF disruptions within known GWAS loci for complex traits, providing a prioritized list of candidate regulatory variants.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data Acquisition**: Download common human SNPs (MAF > 1%) from dbSNP (via FTP) and retrieve Position Weight Matrices (PWMs) for human TFs from the JASPAR database (2024 release).
- **Genomic Context Filtering**: Filter SNPs to those located within annotated regulatory regions (promoters and enhancers) using ENCODE or Roadmap Epigenomics BED files.
- **Affinity Scoring**: For each SNP, extract the reference and alternate allele sequences (±10bp context) and calculate binding affinity scores using the JASPAR PWMs via a lightweight Python implementation (e.g., `biopython` or `logomaker`).
- **Delta Calculation**: Compute the difference in binding affinity ($\Delta Score = Score_{alt} - Score_{ref}$) for every TF-SNP pair.
- **Statistical Aggregation**: Aggregate $\Delta Score$ distributions per TF and test for deviation from a null distribution generated by shuffling SNP positions across the genome (permutation test, $n=1000$).
- **GWAS Overlap Analysis**: Cross-reference high-impact SNPs (top 5% $|\Delta Score|$) with summary statistics from the GWAS Catalog to assess enrichment of binding-disrupting variants in disease loci.
- **Visualization**: Generate heatmaps of TF sensitivity and Manhattan-style plots showing the genomic distribution of high-impact variants.
