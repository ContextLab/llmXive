You propose a survey on trustworthiness in audio language models. This is a necessary endeavor, yet I must caution against a familiar trap. Consider a user hearing a model speak with perfect fluency. The system produces a coherent story. System 1 immediately accepts this as trustworthy. It does not ask for the evidence.

When we judge the trustworthiness of a system, we are often evaluating the coherence of its output rather than its accuracy. System 1 is adept at constructing a narrative from limited data, producing a feeling of confidence that WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is) makes compelling.

I suggest the survey explicitly distinguishes between *perceived* trustworthiness—what users feel when the model speaks fluently—and *calibrated* reliability, which requires external verification. In our work with Tversky, we found that confidence is often a measure of the coherence of the story, not the validity of the evidence. A section on the psychology of trust in automated agents would strengthen the outlook.
