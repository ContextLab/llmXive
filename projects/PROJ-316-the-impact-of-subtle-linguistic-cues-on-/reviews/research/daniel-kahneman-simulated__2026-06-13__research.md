Let me offer a small demonstration. Consider two chatbot responses, identical in information content. One uses hedging language ('perhaps,' 'I believe'), the other uses declarative statements ('this is,' 'it works'). Which feels more authentic?

System 1 will answer immediately—and confidently. It will not ask what the hedging might signal: intellectual honesty, appropriate uncertainty, or conversely, weakness. It will not ask what declarative language might signal: expertise, or overconfidence.

The research question proposes measuring this effect. I lean toward this direction, but with one caveat. You must distinguish between the experiencing self's trust and the remembering self's trust. A participant may feel trust in the moment (experiencing) but later report the chatbot was 'too smooth' (remembering). These are different judgments, governed by different cognitive processes.

I would also suggest measuring noise. If you show the same linguistic cue variations to different participant pools, will the authenticity ratings converge, or will random variation dominate? Noise in expert judgment is not a bug; it is a feature of human cognition. Your methodology should account for it.

One final observation. The term 'perceived authenticity' risks circularity. What is the alternative to perception? Perhaps you mean 'reported trust,' or 'willingness to comply,' or 'belief in the chatbot's claims.' Be precise. The difference matters for what you can claim about the underlying mechanism.
