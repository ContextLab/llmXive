# Beyond Scalar Rewards by Internalizing Reasoning into Score Distributions

**Field**: computer science

## Research question

*How does conditioning a score‑distribution reward model on teacher‑generated reasoning traces affect the calibration of predicted visual‑preference scores compared to a model trained without reasoning?*

## Motivation

Current reward models for text‑to‑image generation treat human preference as a single scalar, ignoring the inherent uncertainty and the reasoning that annotators use when assigning rubric scores. By internalizing reasoning into the predicted distribution we may obtain reward signals that are better calibrated to human judgments, enabling safer and more reliable downstream fine‑tuning. Demonstrating a measurable calibration benefit would justify moving beyond scalar rewards.

## Related work

- [Beyond Scalar Rewards by Internalizing Reasoning into Score Distributions (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09076) — Introduces a teacher‑student framework that generates reasoning traces and learns a distribution over rubric scores, but does not isolate the calibration impact of the reasoning component.  
- [Burning RED: Unlocking Subtask‑Driven Reinforcement Learning and Risk‑Awareness in Average‑Reward Markov Decision Processes (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.10578) — Provides a formal foundation for reward modelling under uncertainty and risk‑awareness, motivating the need for richer than scalar reward representations in sequential decision‑making.

## Expected results

We expect the reasoning‑conditioned model to exhibit lower calibration error (e.g., Expected Calibration Error < baseline by ≥ 5 percentage points) and a smaller Brier score on a held‑out human‑annotated test set. A paired bootstrap test (10 000 resamples) should show the improvement is statistically significant (p < 0.01). If no improvement is observed, the result will still clarify that reasoning traces do not by themselves enhance calibration, guiding future model‑design choices.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**  
  1. Download the *Aesthetic Image Preference* dataset from HuggingFace (`datasets/aesthetic_image_dataset`) – contains ~30 k image‑prompt pairs with 5‑point rubric scores and optional free‑text comments.  
  2. Split into 70 % training, 15 % validation, 15 % test (stratified by score).  

- **Model preparation**  
  3. Use a publicly available CLIP ViT‑B/32 checkpoint as the visual encoder (download from `https://github.com/openai/CLIP`).  
  4. Fine‑tune a lightweight transformer decoder (≈ 2 M parameters) to generate:  
     - a) a reasoning trace (short natural‑language explanation)  
     - b) a categorical distribution over the 5 rubric bins.  

- **Teacher‑student distillation**  
  5. Train the *teacher* model on the training split with a joint loss: cross‑entropy on the distribution + language modeling loss on the reasoning trace.  
  6. Distill to a *student* model (ViT‑B/16 encoder + 1 M‑parameter head) under two conditions:  
     - **Condition R** – student receives the teacher’s reasoning trace as an additional input (concatenated token embeddings).  
     - **Condition S** – student receives only the teacher’s scalar expectation (no reasoning).  

- **Evaluation**  
  7. On the held‑out test set, compute:  
     - Expected Calibration Error (ECE) across the 5 bins.  
     - Brier score for the predicted distribution.  
  8. Perform a paired bootstrap test (10 000 resamples, identical random seeds for both conditions) to assess significance of the ECE/Brier differences.  
  9. Run three random seeds for each condition to verify robustness; report mean ± standard error.  

- **Ablation & analysis**  
  10. Remove the reasoning loss from the teacher (train a scalar‑only teacher) and repeat steps 5‑9 to confirm that any observed gain stems from reasoning‑conditioned supervision rather than teacher quality alone.  

All steps rely on CPU‑friendly models and the public datasets above; the full pipeline (download ≈ 1 GB, training ≈ 4 h, evaluation ≈ 30 min) fits within a 6‑hour GitHub Actions runner.
