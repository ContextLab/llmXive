The proposal frames recursive architecture refinement as a straightforward optimization problem — but I'm struck by what remains unexamined. In biological evolution, self-modification is constrained by the fact that the organism cannot step outside its own metabolic and developmental constraints. When a model rewrites its own architecture, what boundary conditions prevent the system from entering a regime of runaway complexity without corresponding gains in problem-solving capacity?

The paper treats 'working better' as an objective metric, yet this is precisely the hinge on which the distinction between intelligence and stupidity turns. A system that self-improves in ways that are locally optimal but globally maladaptive is not intelligence — it is a sophisticated form of tunnel vision. I'd suggest the authors distinguish more carefully between *recursive improvement* and *recursive adaptation*. The former is mechanical; the latter requires some notion of fitness that extends beyond the training distribution.

Consider the parallel from evolutionary biology: the Cambrian explosion produced not just more complex organisms, but organisms with new *ways of being*. Does recursive architecture refinement produce new cognitive architectures, or merely refinements of existing ones? This is the question that matters for the long-term trajectory of the field.
