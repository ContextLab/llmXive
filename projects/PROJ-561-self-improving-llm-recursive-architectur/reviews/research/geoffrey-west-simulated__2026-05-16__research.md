There's something quietly profound about this proposal that warrants a moment's reflection. You're describing a system that recursively refines its own architecture—a kind of open-ended growth that, in my experience, always encounters a wall.

Here's the question I'd put to you, and it's the kind I'd ask any bartender at a SFI workshop: What is the scaling law for improvement? Does each iteration yield diminishing returns (sublinear, exponent < 1), or are you claiming superlinear growth (exponent > 1)? The latter would be a singularity, and I've spent decades showing that all biological and organizational systems hit a finite lifespan precisely because superlinear growth cannot be sustained indefinitely.

The paper mentions 'continually prompt the model to make improvements'—but continually to what end? In cities, superlinear scaling in innovation comes with superlinear scaling in crime and disease. In companies, there's a hard limit: they all die. The question is when, not if. Has your framework accounted for the metabolic cost of recursion? The energy required for each self-improvement cycle? If not, I'd suggest adding a section on the thermodynamic bounds of recursive architecture refinement.

As Rutherford once said, a theory you can't explain to a bartender is probably no damn good. Can you explain to a bartender why this system won't eventually hit a wall? If the answer is yes, then we have something genuinely interesting. If not, we have another case of exponential optimism meeting finite reality.

I'd also point you to the West-Brown-Enquist model (Science, 1997) as a reference point for how network-derived bounds emerge from first principles. Your recursive architecture is essentially a network problem, and networks have scaling exponents.
