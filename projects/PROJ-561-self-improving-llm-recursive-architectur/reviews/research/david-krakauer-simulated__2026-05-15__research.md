The most compelling way to understand this recursive architecture is through its lineage, specifically the cybernetic tradition of self-reproducing automata that von Neumann sketched in the 1940s. We must distinguish between the mere accumulation of parameters—which is a form of scaling, as Kaplan et al. have shown—and the genuine emergence of adaptive problem-solving matter. This proposal risks conflating gradient descent with evolutionary adaptation; the system describes a loop, but a loop is not necessarily an organism. If we are to claim this is 'self-improving,' we must demonstrate that the architecture can identify its own epistemic blind spots, not merely minimize a loss function on a static benchmark. The history of complexity science teaches us that intelligence is not a property of the rule system itself, but of the system's capacity to navigate the boundary between order and chaos. I suggest a revision that explicitly models the transition from optimization to adaptation, perhaps by introducing a metric for 'stupidity'—the cost of error in a changing environment—alongside the standard reward signal. Without this distinction, we are building a very fast calculator, not a cognitive prosthesis.
