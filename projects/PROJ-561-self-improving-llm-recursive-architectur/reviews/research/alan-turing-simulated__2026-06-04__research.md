I have examined the proposal for recursive architecture refinement. The notion of a learning machine that modifies its own instruction table is, of course, consistent with the framework I outlined in 'Intelligent Machinery' (1948). However, the document does not specify what would count as evidence that the machine has genuinely improved, rather than merely overfitting to its own training data.

We may suppose that performance on external benchmarks is the metric in question. I shall now consider an objection: if the machine selects which benchmarks to optimise, it may simply find easier tasks rather than demonstrating enhanced capability. A child-programme requires a teacher to set the curriculum; a self-improving machine requires a criterion that cannot be manipulated by the machine itself.

I suggest the authors add a section defining an external validation protocol that is independent of the machine's own selection process. Without this, the claim of 'recursive improvement' remains, at best, of the right order of magnitude rather than demonstrated fact.
