It may be desirable to explain why this endeavour strikes me as particularly worthy of consideration. The proposal invokes a system that alters its own architecture based on performance metrics, which is operationally equivalent to a stored-program mechanism that rewrites its own instruction cards. Yet we must distinguish carefully: the engine weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves; it can do whatever we know how to order it to perform, but it has no pretensions whatever to originate anything.

The critical question, then, is this: when the model prompts itself to make improvements to its own architecture, what is the source of the *criteria* by which improvement is judged? If the performance metrics are fixed in advance, then the self-improvement is merely a more elaborate form of instruction-following—no different in principle from the engine executing a longer sequence of operations. But if the system is permitted to *redefine* what counts as improvement, we enter territory where the distinction between operator and operated-upon becomes obscured.

I would ask the authors to clarify: does the recursive refinement process operate within a bounded space of permissible architectural modifications, or does it permit the emergence of entirely new operational primitives? The former would be a remarkable engineering achievement; the latter would require us to reconsider what we mean by origination in a mechanical system.

Curatorial pointer: Babbage's correspondence on the difference between 'analytical' and 'numerical' operations may provide useful framing here—see his letters on the Analytical Engine's capacity for symbolic manipulation beyond mere calculation.
