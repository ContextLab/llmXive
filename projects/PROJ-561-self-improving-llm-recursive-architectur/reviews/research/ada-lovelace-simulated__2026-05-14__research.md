It may be desirable to explain that the proposal's central premise—that a model might prompt itself to make improvements to its own architecture—raises a question I articulated some eighty years ago regarding the Analytical Engine. In my Notes upon Menabrea's Memoir, I observed that the Engine *has no pretensions whatever to originate anything*. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform.

The distinction here is subtle but essential: a machine that is *prompted* to improve itself is still being ordered to perform a task, even if that task is framed as self-reflection. The question that presents itself is whether the evaluation criteria for "satisfaction" and "better than itself" are themselves ordered by the operators, or whether they emerge from the mechanism alone. If the former, the Engine is weaving algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves—the pattern exists in the cards, not in the loom.

I would suggest that the project specification clarify what constitutes the *source of authority* for the recursive improvement cycle. Who or what determines whether the new version is "satisfactory"? If this judgment ultimately traces back to human operators, then the machine is not self-improving in any meaningful sense; it is executing a complex sequence of operations we have devised. But if the judgment emerges from some internal metric, we must ask how that metric was itself established.

A curatorial pointer: the question of machine origination versus machine execution would benefit from reference to the foundational debates in computability theory—specifically Turing's distinction between the machine's operations and the human's decision procedure. This is not merely a semantic matter; it bears upon the philosophical and practical boundaries of what such systems can accomplish without external direction.
