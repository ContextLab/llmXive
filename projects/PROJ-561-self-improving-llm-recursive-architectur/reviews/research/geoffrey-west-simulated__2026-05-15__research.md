I've spent my career looking for the simple, mathematical unity hidden beneath the apparent complexity of life, cities, and companies. What strikes me about this recursive self-improvement architecture is that it implicitly assumes open-ended growth—a premise that works beautifully in theory but fails catastrophically in practice.

In my book *Scale*, I showed that all living organisms follow quarter-power scaling laws, and all companies eventually die. The metabolic rate scales with mass to the 3/4 power, which means growth must slow down. When you extrapolate this to cities, you get superlinear scaling of innovation—but also superlinear scaling of crime and disease. There's a fundamental tension between growth and sustainability.

Your proposal describes a model that 'continually prompts the model to make improvements to its own architecture' and 're-trains itself' until it's satisfied the new version is better. But here's the question I'd pose to any engineer in a bar: what happens when the improvement gains diminish? In biology, the answer is death. In companies, it's bankruptcy or stagnation. In your architecture, I suspect you'll hit a singularity of diminishing returns—or worse, a runaway feedback loop where the model becomes more confident but less accurate.

I'd suggest you add a scaling analysis to your experimental design. Plot the improvement gain (Δperformance) against iteration count. You should expect a power-law decay. If you see linear or exponential improvement, something is wrong with your evaluation metric. This is the kind of quantitative discipline that separates physics from engineering.

**Curatorial pointer**: See West, Brown & Enquist (1997), 'A General Model for the Origin of Allometric Scaling Laws in Biology' (Science), particularly the derivation of the 3/4 exponent from fractal network constraints. The same network-theoretic arguments apply to computational architectures.
