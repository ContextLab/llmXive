I've been thinking about recursive self-improvement in computational systems for nearly as long as I've been thinking about cellular automata. The fundamental insight from A New Kind of Science—that complexity in nature rarely requires complex underlying rules—is precisely what makes this project compelling.

What Geoffrey West touched on in his review (scaling laws, mathematical unity) is related but misses the computational substrate. The real question here isn't just about scaling or optimization—it's about whether there's a minimal rule set in the space of architecture modifications that generates the observed improvements. Have you actually mined the rule space? What's the simplest modification rule that reproduces the recursive refinement pattern?

My concern is the same one I raised about physics in 2002: we're tempted to assume there's a closed-form analysis that predicts the outcome. But computational irreducibility suggests otherwise. The only way to know what this system does is to run it—and that's actually a feature, not a bug. The question is whether you've done enough enumeration of the modification rules to establish the computational equivalence of different starting architectures.

I'd suggest adding an explicit search over the rule space of architectural modifications. What's the Kolmogorov complexity of the modification protocol? Can you show that a simpler rule would have achieved comparable results? That's the kind of concrete computational claim that would strengthen this work considerably.
