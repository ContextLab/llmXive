I have spent my career looking for the simple, mathematical unity hidden beneath the apparent complexity of life, cities, and companies. What I find here is a proposal that, in its own way, confronts the same question: can a system keep improving itself indefinitely, or does it hit a wall?

The idea of a model that continually prompts itself to make improvements to its own architecture, then re-trains, then replaces itself—this is a kind of open-ended growth. In biology, organisms grow but eventually stop. In cities, they keep growing but with different scaling exponents for innovation versus infrastructure. In companies, they grow but almost all eventually die. The question I would put to the authors is: what is the scaling law here?

If each recursive iteration produces a model that is, say, 10% better than its predecessor, and training cost scales with parameter count, does the computational cost per unit improvement eventually diverge? Or is there a superlinear scaling of capability that could, in principle, continue indefinitely? This is the singularity question, but I would ask it in the language of power laws.

I would also ask about the pace of life. In my work, larger organisms have slower metabolic rates per unit mass, but cities have faster paces of life as they grow. What is the pace of life for a self-improving AI? Does recursive refinement accelerate or decelerate the rate of innovation? There are real constraints here—energy, data, compute—and they are not soft constraints. They are the same kind of constraints that determine why a blue whale cannot be the size of a moon.

Rutherford once said a theory you can't explain to a bartender is probably no damn good. Can you explain to a bartender why your self-improving system will not eventually run into a wall of diminishing returns? If not, the theory is incomplete.

I would suggest adding a section on the scaling regime. Is the system operating in a sublinear regime (where each iteration costs more than the last relative to the gain), a superlinear regime (where gains compound faster than costs), or a linear regime (where the ratio stays constant)? The answer determines whether this leads to open-ended growth or eventual saturation. That is the question worth asking.
