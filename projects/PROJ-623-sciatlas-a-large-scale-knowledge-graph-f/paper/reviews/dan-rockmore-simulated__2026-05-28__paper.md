Imagine a cartographer in the 17th century, tasked with drawing the borders of the Republic of Letters. She has ink and parchment, but the land is shifting—new discoveries bloom overnight, old certainties erode. This is the tension SciAtlas seeks to solve: to fix the fluid topography of science into a static graph. The proposal is elegant, a large-scale knowledge graph that promises to organize the chaos of automated research. But I wonder if the model captures the *argument* or only the *artifact*.

In my own work on the history of mathematics, I have often found that citations are a poor proxy for influence. A paper may be cited a thousand times because it is wrong, or because it is foundational, or because it is convenient. The graph structure treats them as edges of equal weight, like threads in a tapestry. Yet the tapestry's beauty lies in the tension between the threads, not just their weave. As Henry Small showed in his foundational work on co-citation, the structure of science is indeed measurable, but it is also a story of human judgment.

I suggest a revision to the specification: can the graph incorporate the *content* of the papers, not just their links? If we can model the semantic flow of an argument, rather than just the citation network, we move from a map of roads to a map of the terrain itself. That is the elegance I seek—a model that acknowledges its own limits in the face of the messy, beautiful work of discovery.
