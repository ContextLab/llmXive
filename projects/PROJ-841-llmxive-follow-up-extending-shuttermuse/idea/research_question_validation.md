## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check
**Verdict**: concern
The question is framed around a specific implementation constraint ("lightweight, vision-agnostic classifier") and a comparative performance metric ("comparable to the full MLLM") rather than a fundamental inquiry into the nature of photography guidance. While the underlying phenomenon (the sufficiency of textual rationales for visual decision-making) is valid, the current phrasing prioritizes the engineering outcome of model distillation over the scientific understanding of how visual advice is encoded.

### Circularity check
**Verdict**: concern
The predictor (textual rationales) is generated by the same model (ShutterMuse) that produces the ground truth labels (decisions) used for training the lightweight classifier. Since the rationales are essentially the model's own internal justification for its decision, training a classifier to map rationales to decisions risks measuring the consistency of the original model's self-explanation rather than the independent sufficiency of the text. The data sources are not fully independent; the "signal" in the text is derived from the same process that generated the target.

### Triviality check
**Verdict**: concern
If the result is positive, it confirms that the MLLM's text contains the decision logic, which is somewhat tautological given the text was generated to explain that logic. If the result is negative, it suggests the visual features were essential, which is expected for a multimodal task. Neither outcome offers a deep insight into photography or human perception; the result is largely a measure of the MLLM's own coherence rather than a discovery about the domain.

### Question-narrowing check
**Verdict**: fail
The question explicitly names implementation constraints (lightweight, CPU-tractable, vision-agnostic) and a specific benchmark comparison (accuracy vs. full MLLM) as the core inquiry. This frames the project as a system optimization study ("Can we build a smaller model?") rather than a domain science question ("What information is necessary for photography guidance?").

### Overall verdict
**Verdict**: validator_revise
The core issue is the circularity of using the model's own output as the sole predictor for the model's own decision, combined with an over-focus on the engineering feasibility of the classifier. The project needs to reframe to ask about the *information content* of the rationales relative to the visual task, independent of the specific distillation pipeline.
[REVISED]
To what extent do the textual rationales generated by multimodal models capture the critical visual features required for photography guidance, and can a text-only model trained on these rationales generalize to new images better than a model trained solely on the raw visual input?
[/REVISED]
This reframing shifts the focus from "can we make a small model?" to "what is the information theoretic value of the text vs. the image," breaking the circularity by testing generalization on held-out images rather than just reproducing the original model's decisions.
