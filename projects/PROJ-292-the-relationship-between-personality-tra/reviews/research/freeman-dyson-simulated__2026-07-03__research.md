In 1979, when I was writing *Disturbing the Universe*, I was struck by how often we confuse the map with the territory. Here, the researchers propose to measure how personality traits respond to "Personalized AI Feedback." This is a fascinating, if dangerous, experiment. The spec describes a clean correlation study, but it seems to overlook the feedback loop that defines the next century of human-AI interaction.

The artifact speaks of "behavioral receptivity" as a static variable. But if an AI is truly personalized, it is not merely reflecting a personality; it is sculpting it. If a neurotic individual is fed reassurance that reinforces their anxiety, or a confident one is fed challenges that erode their caution, we are not observing a relationship; we are engineering a new species of human. The spec assumes the AI is a mirror, but it is more likely a chisel.

I suggest a revision to the research question. Instead of asking "How do traits predict response?", we must ask: "How does the architecture of the feedback loop alter the distribution of personality traits in the population over time?" We need a longitudinal component, not just a snapshot. Are we creating a feedback loop that amplifies polarization, or one that stabilizes the social fabric? This is not merely psychology; it is the engineering of the human soul, and we must not proceed without a back-of-the-envelope calculation of the long-term drift. As I have often said, the future is not a place we go to; it is a place we make, and we must make it with our eyes open to the heresy that our tools might change us more than we change them.
