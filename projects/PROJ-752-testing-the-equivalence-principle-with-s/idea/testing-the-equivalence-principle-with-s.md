# Testing the Equivalence Principle with Satellite Laser Ranging  

**Field**: physics  

## Research question  

Do geodetic satellites of differing composition (e.g., LAGEOS 1 / 2, Etalon‑1 / 2, Starlette) exhibit measurable differential accelerations that would violate the weak equivalence principle (WEP)?  

## Motivation  

The weak equivalence principle underlies General Relativity and all metric theories of gravity; any composition‑dependent deviation would signal new physics. Existing high‑precision tests rely on lunar laser ranging, planetary radioscience, and binary pulsars, but satellite laser ranging (SLR) offers an independent, Earth‑based laboratory with centimeter‑level orbit determination. Demonstrating that SLR can place competitive limits on WEP violations would broaden the experimental toolkit and exploit a freely available, long‑term data set.  

## Related work  

- [Lunar Laser Ranging Science (2004)](https://arxiv.org/abs/gr-qc/0411095) — Shows how laser ranging to the Moon constrains the Nordtvedt parameter, a WEP‑related quantity, establishing the benchmark precision of laser‑ranged tests.  
- [Testing General Relativity with LAGEOS, LAGEOS II and Ajisai laser‑ranged satellites (2001)](https://arxiv.org/abs/gr-qc/0105014) — Demonstrates that SLR data can be used to test relativistic effects (frame‑drag, Lense‑Thirring); provides a methodological precedent for extracting small dynamical signals from satellite orbits.  
- [Constraining the Nordtvedt parameter with the BepiColombo Radioscience experiment (2016)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1605.07822) — Uses interplanetary radio tracking to bound WEP violations, illustrating the current state‑of‑the‑art limits from spacecraft missions.  
- [PSR J0337+1715: an appropriate laboratory for testing the Nordtvedt effect (2014)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1407.0090) — Provides a complementary astrophysical test of the Nordtvedt effect, highlighting the diversity of experimental approaches.  
- [The Eötvös Paradox: The Enduring Significance of Eötvös' Most Famous Paper (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.11163) — Reviews the historical context and lingering puzzles in WEP testing, motivating fresh experimental avenues such as SLR.  

## Expected results  

We anticipate deriving an upper bound on the Eötvös parameter η at the 10⁻⁹ – 10⁻¹⁰ level (comparable to, though slightly weaker than, the best LLR limits). Confirmation of the null hypothesis (no composition‑dependent acceleration) will tighten existing constraints; a statistically significant non‑zero η would constitute a discovery of WEP violation. Evidence will be judged by (i) the χ² improvement of a model that includes a composition‑dependent term and (ii) the resulting 95 % confidence interval on η.  

## Methodology sketch  

- **Data acquisition** – Download multi‑year SLR normal‑point series for LAGEOS‑1, LAGEOS‑2, Etalon‑1, Etalon‑2, and Starlette from the International Laser Ranging Service (ILRS) public archive (e.g., `ftp://ilrs.gsfc.nasa.gov/SLR/`).  
- **Satellite selection** – Choose satellites that span a range of bulk composition and mass‑to‑area ratios (e.g., dense brass LAGEOS vs. lighter aluminum Starlette).  
- **Orbit modelling** – Use the open‑source `pyoorb` / `pyorbital` libraries together with the latest Earth gravity field (e.g., GGM05C from GRACE) to generate high‑fidelity dynamical models including geopotential, atmospheric drag, solar radiation pressure, and relativistic corrections.  
- **Parameter estimation** – Perform a weighted least‑squares fit of the model to the SLR normal points, solving simultaneously for standard orbital elements and a possible composition‑dependent acceleration term **a₍c₎** proportional to the difference in material density.  
- **Residual analysis** – Compute post‑fit residual accelerations for each satellite; form differential residuals between each pair of compositionally distinct satellites.  
- **Statistical test** – Apply an F‑test (or Bayesian Information Criterion) to compare the null model (a₍c₎ = 0) against the alternative model (a₍c₎ ≠ 0). Derive a 95 % confidence upper limit on η = 2a₍c₎/g.  
- **Independent validation** – Cross‑check the Earth gravity model using an external GRACE‑derived product (not derived from the same SLR data) to ensure that any detected differential signal is not an artefact of the geopotential model.  
- **Reproducibility** – All scripts will be written in Python, containerised with Docker (≤ 2 GB image), and executed in a single GitHub Actions job (< 6 h). Output includes the η limit, diagnostic plots, and a CSV of residuals.
