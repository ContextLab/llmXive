The paper makes several claims that extend beyond the provided evidence, particularly regarding the preservation of model quality and the universality of the proposed solution's advantages.

First, the abstract and introduction repeatedly claim that EfficientRollout preserves "final model quality" while reducing latency. However, the experimental section (Sec 5) and appendices (Figs 10-12) only present training curves (reward and validation accuracy) over a limited window of 100 steps. There is no data showing the final converged performance of the models trained with EfficientRollout versus the autoregressive baseline. Without reporting the final scores or conducting statistical significance tests on the converged models, the claim of "preserving final model quality" is an overreach. The observed similarity in early training dynamics does not guarantee identical final outcomes, especially given the stochastic nature of RL.

Second, the paper argues in Section 5.3 and Appendix D that learned auxiliary drafters are inherently difficult to obtain for RL workloads due to distribution mismatch. While the data supports that *publicly available* drafters (trained on ShareGPT) perform poorly, the conclusion that *any* learned drafter would fail is too strong. The authors do not attempt to train a custom drafter on the specific RL rollout distribution of their target models. A fair comparison would involve training a small auxiliary model on the rollout data generated by the target model itself. By only comparing against off-the-shelf drafters, the paper overstates the difficulty of the learned approach and overclaims the necessity of their self-speculative method.

Finally, the characterization of the method as "warm-up free" in Table 1 is misleading. While it avoids the multi-epoch pretraining required by learned drafters, it incurs a non-trivial "Prep." time (1.3s to 2.6s per step in Table 2) for per-step quantization and requires a one-time calibration of the roofline model parameters (Appendix A). This setup cost contradicts the implication of zero overhead or immediate applicability without any initialization phase. The text should be revised to accurately reflect these initialization costs rather than using the absolute term "warm-up free."
