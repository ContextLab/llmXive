## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a fundamental biological relationship between the variance of epigenetic marks and the variance of gene expression under environmental stress. It is framed as an inquiry into how these two biological phenomena interact, rather than testing the performance of a specific algorithm or computational constraint.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (epigenetic variance derived from methylation or chromatin accessibility data) and the predicted variable (gene expression variance derived from RNA-seq) represent distinct molecular layers. While measured in the same samples, they are generated by separate biochemical assays (e.g., bisulfite sequencing vs. transcriptome sequencing), ensuring the correlation is empirical rather than mechanically guaranteed by data construction.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: concern

While a positive correlation is biologically plausible, a null result (no correlation) might be difficult to interpret definitively as "non-functional" without controlling for the specific timescales of drift versus transcriptional response. However, demonstrating that epigenetic variance *fails* to predict expression variance under fluctuating conditions would still be informative, suggesting that other buffering mechanisms dominate, so the question is not entirely trivial.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question explicitly names a domain relationship (the correlation between epigenetic variance and expression variability) within a specific biological context (fluctuating environments). It does not frame the inquiry around the ability of a specific model to fit the data or meet a computational budget.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

The research question is well-formed, focusing on a substantive biological mechanism rather than methodological constraints or circular data definitions. While the triviality check raises a minor point about interpreting null results, the core question is scientifically valid and the proposed methodology (correlating independent omics layers) is sound for testing the hypothesis.
