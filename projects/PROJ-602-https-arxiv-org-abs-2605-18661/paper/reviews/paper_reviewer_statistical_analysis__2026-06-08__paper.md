As a survey paper, this manuscript does not present primary statistical analysis or experimental data generated by the authors. Consequently, standard statistical review criteria (e.g., test appropriateness, model assumptions, power analysis) are not directly applicable to the authors' methodology. However, the paper makes numerous quantitative claims aggregating results from cited works (e.g., '17.5% of CS papers AI-modified', '89% of cases improved').

The primary statistical concern is the reporting of point estimates without uncertainty measures. For instance, Section 5 reports 'detectable AI modification in up to 17.5% of computer science abstracts' without citing the confidence interval or sample size of the underlying study (Liang et al., 2024). Similarly, Section 6 states LLM feedback improved quality in '89% of cases' without reporting variance or statistical significance of this improvement.

When aggregating metrics across benchmarks (e.g., SWE-bench scores in Section 3.3), the paper treats disparate benchmarks as comparable without normalization or meta-analytic correction. This risks 'cherry-picking' high-performing benchmarks. Additionally, claims of statistical significance (e.g., 'p<0.05' in Section 3.1) rely entirely on cited p-values; the authors should explicitly state that these are secondary analyses and verify if multiple comparisons were handled in the original studies.

Furthermore, reproducibility of cited statistics is hindered by the lack of a unified data appendix. While the bibliography is extensive, the specific datasets or extraction methods used to derive aggregated percentages are not detailed. To strengthen statistical rigor, the authors should: (1) clarify that all statistics are secondary citations; (2) include confidence intervals or sample sizes for key aggregated metrics where available; (3) avoid implying statistical significance for trends unless the underlying studies explicitly support it with corrected tests; and (4) consider a meta-analytic approach for benchmark comparisons to account for heterogeneity.
