# llmXive follow-up: extending "Learning from the Self-future: On-policy Self-distillation for dLLMs"

## Summary of the prior work
The paper introduces d-OPSD, the first on-policy self-distillation framework specifically designed for diffusion Large Language Models (dLLMs), which overcomes the incompatibility of existing autoregressive-centric methods by utilizing self-generated answers as suffix conditioning ("self-future") and shifting supervision from token-level to step-level. By leveraging the bidirectional generation capabilities of dLLMs, this approach allows a single model to act as both teacher and student, achieving superior reasoning performance and sample efficiency compared to Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) baselines.

## Proposed extension
How does the *density* of privileged suffix information (the ratio of revealed tokens in the self-generated future) impact the stability and convergence speed of d-OPSD, and can an adaptive scheduling strategy for this density outperform fixed ratios without requiring expensive GPU-based training loops? This question matters because the current method relies on a fixed retaining ratio ($\rho_{\text{teacher}}$), which may be suboptimal across different reasoning tasks or training stages, and identifying a CPU-tractable proxy metric for this optimization could significantly reduce the cost of hyperparameter tuning in future dLLM post-training pipelines.

## Methodology sketch
We will utilize a small-scale synthetic reasoning dataset (e.g., 1,000 samples of multi-step arithmetic or logical deduction tasks) generated by a lightweight pre-trained dLLM (such as a distilled 100M parameter model) to simulate the "student" and "teacher" trajectories. The procedure involves running a series of CPU-only simulation experiments where we vary the $\rho_{\text{teacher}}$ parameter (from 0.1 to 0.9) and compare a fixed schedule against an adaptive schedule that increases revealed tokens based on a simple heuristic (e.g., current step confidence score calculated via entropy on the CPU). The expected result is a quantifiable "stability-efficiency curve" demonstrating that an adaptive density schedule converges to a higher-quality policy in fewer iterations than any fixed ratio, validated by measuring the reduction in "hallucination rate" (mismatch between generated and ground-truth steps) on a held-out CPU-evaluated test set.
