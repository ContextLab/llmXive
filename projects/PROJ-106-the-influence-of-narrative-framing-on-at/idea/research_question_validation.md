## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a psychological relationship between narrative framing and user attitudes toward AI, independent of any specific ML method or computational technique. The framing manipulation (partner vs. tool language) is the independent variable, and the outcomes (attitudes, usefulness, trust) are measured through established psychological scales.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (narrative framing in vignettes) and the predicted variables (attitude, usefulness, trust scales) are measured through entirely independent sources: one is a stimulus condition assigned by the experimenter, the other is self-reported survey data from participants. There is no mechanical relationship between the framing text and the survey responses.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result (partner framing increases trust) would inform AI communication design and policy. A null result (framing has no effect) would be equally informative, suggesting that users evaluate AI based on substantive performance factors rather than narrative framing. Both outcomes are publishable in psychology/technology journals.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a clear domain relationship (narrative framing → attitudes toward AI assistance) rather than implementation constraints. It does not fixate on specific method performance, budget limits, or computational resources, but instead asks a substantive question about human-AI interaction psychology.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass without concern. The research question is well-framed as a psychological phenomenon question with independent predictors and outcomes, publishable regardless of the direction of results, and focused on the domain relationship rather than implementation details. The proposed experimental design appropriately supports the question.
