The paper presents a compelling ablation study design, particularly in Table `tab:ablation_anyflow`, which effectively isolates the contributions of flow map training and backward simulation. The comparison across bidirectional and causal architectures (1.3B to 14B parameters) strengthens the claim of generalizability. However, the scientific evidence regarding statistical significance is incomplete.

The quantitative improvements reported in Table `tab:t2v_comparison` are often marginal (e.g., AnyFlow 14B T2V Total 84.04 vs. rCM 14B 83.73). Without reported variance, confidence intervals, or p-values, it is difficult to determine if these gains are statistically significant or within the noise of the evaluation metric (VBench). Section 5.1 states a synthetic dataset of 256K pairs was used for training, but Section 5.2 does not explicitly state the number of prompts sampled for the VBench evaluation. Standard video generation benchmarks often use 100-1000 prompts; knowing this sample size is critical for interpreting the precision of the reported scores.

Additionally, while the authors re-evaluate key baselines using official VBench augmented prompts, they note that "Results for all other methods are taken directly from their respective papers" (Section 5.2). This introduces a potential confounding variable where differences in evaluation protocols between the re-evaluated AnyFlow and the cited baselines could account for performance gaps. Finally, the reliance on synthetic data generated by the teacher model (Section 5.1) is acknowledged in the Limitations, but the potential for distribution shift affecting the "any-step" generalization claim warrants further discussion on how this impacts the robustness of the evidence compared to training on the base model's original dataset.
