# llmXive follow-up: extending "A Stylometric Application of Large Language Models"

## Summary of the prior work
The paper introduces "predictive comparison," a stylometric method where a GPT-2 model is trained from scratch on a single author's corpus to capture their unique writing style, then evaluated by measuring the cross-entropy loss on held-out texts from other authors. The core finding is that a model trained on Author A consistently predicts Author A's text with lower loss than text from any other author, achieving 100% classification accuracy across eight classic authors and successfully confirming the disputed authorship of the 15th *Oz* book. The study further decomposes these signatures, finding that while content words, function words, and part-of-speech sequences all contribute, the full text provides the most robust stylistic signal.

## Proposed extension
**Research Question:** Can a CPU-tractable, "predictive comparison" framework based on character-level n-gram language models (rather than transformer-based LLMs) robustly distinguish authorial style in non-fiction, highly structured technical writing (e.g., scientific abstracts or legal briefs), where stylistic variance is hypothesized to be lower than in literary fiction?

This extension matters because the original study focused on literary fiction with high narrative variance; applying the concept to constrained, formulaic non-fiction tests the limits of the "stylometric distance" metric and determines if the approach scales to domains where traditional function-word analysis often fails. Using character-level n-grams ensures the experiment remains feasible on standard CPU hardware without the GPU overhead of training GPT-2, while still testing the fundamental hypothesis that an autoregressive model trained on one author's distribution will fail to predict another's.

## Methodology sketch
**Data:** Compile a corpus of 500+ short, anonymized scientific abstracts (approx. 250 words each) from 10 distinct fields (e.g., condensed matter physics, immunology) written by 20 unique lead authors (10 abstracts per author), plus a set of "contested" abstracts generated by swapping sentences between authors to create synthetic "hybrid" texts.

**Procedure:**
1. Preprocess text by lowercasing and removing punctuation, then tokenize at the character level.
2. For each of the 20 authors, train a simple character-level n-gram language model (order $n=4$ to $6$) using a CPU-efficient implementation (e.g., `scikit-learn` or a custom Python script using hash maps) on 8 of their abstracts.
3. Compute the perplexity (exponential of cross-entropy loss) of each held-out abstract (the 9th and 10th) and the synthetic hybrid abstracts under every author's model.
4. Apply the "predictive comparison" logic: assign the held-out text to the author whose model yields the minimum perplexity.
5. Compare the classification accuracy of this n-gram approach against a baseline of traditional function-word frequency analysis (chi-square test) and a baseline using the original paper's GPT-2 method (if GPU resources are available for a subset, otherwise rely on the CPU n-gram comparison).

**Expected Result:** We hypothesize that character-level n-gram models will achieve high accuracy (>85%) in distinguishing authors in non-fiction, proving that the "predictive comparison" principle holds even without deep semantic understanding, though accuracy may drop significantly for the synthetic hybrid texts (indicating the models are sensitive to subtle, consistent structural habits). This would demonstrate that the method is computationally lightweight and applicable to domains where data volume is too small for fine-tuning large transformers.
