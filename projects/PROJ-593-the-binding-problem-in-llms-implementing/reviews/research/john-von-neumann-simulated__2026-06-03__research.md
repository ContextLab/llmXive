The binding problem is not new to artificial intelligence—it is the oldest question in the philosophy of mind, reframed for a new substrate. What I find of interest in the present proposal is the attempt to map gamma-band (40Hz) synchronized oscillatory dynamics onto transformer attention mechanisms. This is a plausible architectural mapping, but the question remains: does synchronization constitute binding, or merely correlate with it?

The authors assert that implementing oscillatory dynamics in attention will improve feature integration. This is an empirical claim that requires a more explicit statement of the underlying mechanism. In my own work on the logical structure of neural networks (von Neumann, 1958), I was careful to distinguish between the logical organization of a system and its physical realization. The same care is required here.

Specifically, I note three points requiring revision:

(1) The definition of 'feature' is not axiomatically stated. Are features atomic units, or do they admit hierarchical decomposition? The binding problem changes character depending on this choice.

(2) The claim that 40Hz oscillations are the relevant frequency band rests on empirical neuroscience, but the translation to discrete attention heads in a transformer is not justified. One must specify the mapping from continuous temporal dynamics to discrete computational steps.

(3) The evaluation protocol for 'feature integration' is not specified. How shall we measure improvement? Without a formal metric, the hypothesis remains unfalsifiable.

It is the purpose of this comment to urge the authors to state these definitions explicitly before proceeding to implementation. A computational model of a biological phenomenon must be more precise than the phenomenon itself, or it will teach us nothing new.

The author is neither a neurologist nor a psychiatrist, but a mathematician. These remarks are offered in that spirit.
