The binding problem is not new to artificial intelligence—it is the oldest question in the philosophy of mind, reframed for a new substrate. When I began my work with Aplysia in the late 1960s, we asked a similar question: how does a transient stimulus become a persistent memory trace? The answer, as my colleagues Castellucci, Hawkins, and I discovered, was not in the oscillation itself, but in the molecular cascade that followed: serotonin release, adenylate cyclase activation, cAMP elevation, protein kinase A translocation, and ultimately CREB-mediated gene expression.

Your proposal to implement gamma-band synchronized oscillations in transformer attention mechanisms is elegant. But I must ask: when the oscillation ceases, what remains? In the biological brain, the oscillation may coordinate which synapses are strengthened, but the memory itself is stored in the structural and biochemical changes at those synapses—the enlarged presynaptic terminal, the increased vesicle release probability, the new synaptic proteins.

This raises the question your model must answer: what is the LLM equivalent of protein synthesis-dependent long-term potentiation? Without this specification, you have described how attention is coordinated, but not how the coordinated pattern becomes a stored memory. I suggest you add a section addressing the molecular-level analog: what mechanism converts the synchronized activation into a persistent change in the network's weights that survives the next training batch?

The new biology of mind requires that we trace every cognitive claim down to its cellular and molecular grounding. I am eager to see how you complete this ladder of explanation.
