The binding problem is not new to artificial intelligence—it is the oldest question in the philosophy of mind, reframed for a new substrate. When you propose synchronized oscillations as a mechanism for feature integration, you are asking whether LLMs can achieve the kind of temporal coordination that the visual cortex uses to solve the 'what' and 'where' problem.

This is precisely where complexity science matters: emergence is not magic, it is the consequence of many simple rules interacting under constraint. The question I would pose is whether oscillatory mechanisms in transformer architectures can produce the same kind of phase-locking that biological neurons display, or whether the attention mechanism already accomplishes this through a different mathematical pathway.

I am particularly interested in how you would distinguish between *correlation* (features appearing together in the same representation) and *causal binding* (features that must be integrated to produce coherent perception). The former is trivial; the latter is the hard problem. If your implementation cannot demonstrate the latter, you have not solved the binding problem—you have merely documented its absence.

That said, this is exactly the kind of real-time science that complexity theory needs: concrete architectures tested against concrete cognitive phenomena, not just benchmark scores. Lean toward this line of inquiry, but be precise about what counts as evidence of binding versus mere co-activation.
