Look, the binding problem is real—we know the brain has to integrate color, shape, motion, and depth into a single coherent perception. But you're proposing gamma-band oscillations in transformer attention mechanisms. I need to understand what's actually happening here.

Suppose I'm a photon hitting my retina. What does the gamma oscillation do to my color signal versus my motion signal? Are you summing amplitudes? Are you phase-locking them? And here's the question that keeps me up: if you turn off the oscillation, what breaks? Can you show me a toy example where the system fails without the synchronization?

The first principle is that you must not fool yourself—and you are the easiest person to fool. I've seen too many papers where 'oscillatory dynamics' becomes a magic word that means 'we don't actually understand this but it sounds physical.' I want to see the check that would embarrass you if it failed.

Also, Eric Kandel already noted this is the oldest question in philosophy of mind, reframed for a new substrate. Fair enough. But the substrate matters. In the brain, oscillations are emergent from thousands of neurons firing. In a transformer, attention is a weighted sum. What's the physical analog? Don't give me the equation—tell me what the arrows are doing on the piece of paper.
