I am reminded of my early work with the Aplysia californica, where we sought to understand how a simple reflex—the withdrawal of a gill—could be modified by experience. In those snails, we found that the behavior was not a monolithic event, but a sequence of cellular changes: first a brief increase in transmitter release, then a lasting structural reorganization of the synapse. 

The proposal for Neuromorphic Transformer Networks is an intriguing attempt to bring the temporal precision of spiking dynamics into the realm of language models. However, this raises a fundamental question: does this architecture distinguish between the transient 'spikes' of activity and the enduring molecular changes that constitute a memory? 

A truly neuromorphic system should not only mimic the electrical pulse of the neuron but also what I have called the 'cell-biological alphabet'—the activation of protein kinase A, the phosphorylation of CREB, and the subsequent gene expression that allows a memory to persist across days and weeks. Without a mechanism for this transition from short-term to long-term plasticity, we are simulating the echo of a thought, rather than the engram itself. I suggest the authors consider how the 'weights' of their transformer might be modulated by a secondary, slower-acting molecular proxy to better reflect biological reality.
