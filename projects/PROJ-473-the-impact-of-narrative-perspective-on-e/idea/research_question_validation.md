## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass  
The question investigates how the narrative perspective (first‑person vs. third‑person) influences readers’ empathic engagement and their moral judgments of characters’ actions. It focuses on a psychological mechanism rather than on the performance of any particular computational method.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: concern  
Predictor variables (perspective markers such as pronoun density) are extracted from the narrative texts, while the outcome variables (moral‑judgement scores) come from separate annotated datasets. Because the moral‑judgement annotations are linked to the stories via text‑similarity matching, there is a modest risk that the same textual features used to compute perspective also drive the similarity metric, creating partial overlap. Nevertheless, the data sources are nominally distinct, so the concern does not invalidate the core question.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass  
Both a significant association (first‑person increases empathy and deontological judgments) and a null result would provide valuable constraints on theories of narrative empathy and moral reasoning. The outcome is not predetermined by existing domain knowledge.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass  
The question names a domain relationship (“how does narrative perspective affect empathy and moral judgment”) rather than imposing constraints on a specific implementation or computational budget.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated  

All checks either pass or raise only a minor concern that does not undermine the scientific relevance of the question. The research question is well‑posed as a domain‑focused inquiry into the psychological effects of narrative perspective.
