The manuscript presents a safety-positive contribution by prioritizing faithfulness and calibrated abstention, which reduces hallucination risks common in general-purpose LLMs. The training data sources (Wikipedia, standard benchmarks like HotpotQA and MuSiQue) are public and do not raise immediate privacy or IRB concerns (sections `sections/synth.tex`, `tables/benchmarks.tex`). The use of synthetic data generated by other LLMs (`gpt-oss-120B`, `Qwen3.5-27B`) is standard but warrants a note on ensuring the synthetic traces themselves do not encode harmful biases.

However, significant safety gaps remain in the discussion of deployment risks. The core design principle of "faithfulness" creates a dual-use vulnerability: the model is explicitly trained to prioritize provided context over parametric knowledge, even when the context is factually incorrect (see `tables/demo.tex` where the model answers "Charles de Gaulle" based on false context). While this demonstrates robustness against memory interference, it implies the model can be weaponized to amplify misinformation if the retrieval pipeline is compromised (context poisoning). The paper currently lacks a discussion on this specific failure mode.

Additionally, the release of model checkpoints (`OCC-RAG-0.6B` and `OCC-RAG-1.7B`) is mentioned without corresponding licensing or acceptable use policies. Given the model's specialized optimization for reasoning and refusal, clear guidelines on prohibited use cases (e.g., automated disinformation generation) are necessary. Finally, the evaluation does not include adversarial robustness testing against prompt injection via context, a standard safety requirement for RAG systems. Addressing these points will ensure the safety implications of the technology are fully transparent to users and regulators.
