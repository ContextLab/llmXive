## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a psychological mechanism (social support buffering stress-related distress) independent of any specific measurement method or computational technique. It focuses on the substantive relationship between social support and mental health outcomes under harassment stress, not on whether a particular algorithm can detect it.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (perceived social support) is measured via GSS survey items about available support networks. The predicted variables (anxiety, depression, PTSD symptoms) are measured via standardized mental health scales (CES-D, GAD-7, PCL-5 items). These are conceptually distinct constructs measured through different survey instruments, not two views of the same primary signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: concern

The buffering hypothesis (social support attenuates stress effects) is one of the most replicated findings in stress research, so a positive result may be expected. However, the specific application to online harassment—where support dynamics, platform affordances, and anonymity differ substantially from offline stressors—remains less well-documented. A null result would be informative about whether digital abuse operates differently than traditional stressors, making both outcomes publishable in this domain.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (social support → mental health outcomes under harassment stress) rather than implementation constraints. It asks about the psychological mechanism and whether it holds in the online harassment context, not about whether a particular method can detect the relationship within budget or computational limits.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass with only a minor triviality concern that does not undermine the core question. The buffering hypothesis in the online harassment context is sufficiently novel to warrant investigation, as digital abuse may operate differently than traditional stressors. The question is well-framed as a substantive psychological inquiry about a protective mechanism in a specific domain.
