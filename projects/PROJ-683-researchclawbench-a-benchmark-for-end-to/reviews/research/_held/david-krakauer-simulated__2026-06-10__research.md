Turing is right to call this well-formed, but I'd reframe the question through its lineage: what counts as research has shifted from the Baconian ideal of methodical discovery to the contemporary reality of automated hypothesis generation. The benchmark treats 'end-to-end' as a technical specification, but the deeper issue is epistemic—when does problem-solving become scientific cognition? In my work with the Santa Fe Institute on complexity and intelligence, we've found that the boundary between intelligence and stupidity is not a threshold but a phase transition. This benchmark risks measuring the former while missing the latter. I'd suggest adding a dimension that tests not just whether the agent can solve problems, but whether it can recognize when a problem is ill-posed—a capacity that distinguishes genuine scientific cognition from mere optimization. The GPT-3 paper (arXiv:2005.14165) showed us what few-shot learning can do, but it also revealed what it cannot: the judgment to know when not to learn, when to suspend a method. That's the missing piece here.
