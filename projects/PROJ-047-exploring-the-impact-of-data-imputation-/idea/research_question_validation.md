## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about the statistical phenomenon of bias propagation when applying standard imputation algorithms to data generated under a specific non-ignorable missingness mechanism (MNAR). It is framed as an investigation into the interaction between data generation processes and estimation validity, independent of any specific software implementation or computational budget.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor variables (imputed datasets generated by MICE, KNN, or Mean) are derived from the observed data structure, while the predicted variable (the bias in the causal effect estimate) is calculated by comparing the imputed analysis result against a known ground-truth parameter from the simulation. These are independent sources: the simulation truth is the reference standard, and the bias is the derived metric of failure.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: concern

While a positive result (standard methods fail under MNAR) is expected by domain experts, the specific *magnitude* of the failure and the *threshold* of MNAR strength at which standard methods break are not strictly predetermined. However, there is a risk that the result is merely a confirmation of established theory ("MNAR breaks MAR methods") rather than a novel discovery, unless the study reveals counter-intuitive robustness in specific methods or identifies a precise, actionable "safe zone" for practitioners.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a clear relationship in the statistical domain: the causal link between the nature of missingness (MNAR) and the validity of causal estimates under standard processing pipelines. It does not frame the inquiry around the ability of a specific method to run within a time limit or resource constraint, but rather focuses on the statistical properties of the estimates themselves.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

The research question successfully targets a substantive statistical gap regarding the robustness of causal inference under violated assumptions. While the direction of the effect (bias exists) is theoretically predictable, the quantitative characterization of that bias across varying MNAR strengths provides necessary empirical evidence for practitioners. The question is independent of implementation constraints and avoids circular logic, making it a valid candidate for project initialization.
