## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive relationship between cosmic ray arrival-direction anisotropy and solar activity phase, which is a physics phenomenon question. The methodology (IceCube/Auger data, statistical correlation) serves as the means to answer the question rather than being the question itself.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (solar cycle phase from sunspot number and solar-wind speed) is sourced from NOAA/NGDC ground and satellite observations. The predicted variable (TeV–PeV cosmic ray anisotropy) is measured independently by IceCube and Pierre Auger particle detectors. These are separate measurement modalities with no shared primary signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive correlation would bridge high-energy cosmic-ray propagation and heliospheric physics, constraining particle transport models. A null result would falsify the hypothesis that heliospheric modulation affects large-scale anisotropy at TeV–PeV energies, suggesting galactic origins independent of solar effects. Either outcome is scientifically informative and not predetermined by existing domain knowledge.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (anisotropy modulation by solar cycle phase) rather than implementation constraints. The budget, dataset size, and computational limits appear in the methodology sketch but do not define the research question itself.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four validation checks pass. The research question is a substantive physics question about cosmic ray propagation and heliospheric modulation, with independent data sources for predictor and outcome, and both positive and null outcomes would be publishable contributions to the field.
