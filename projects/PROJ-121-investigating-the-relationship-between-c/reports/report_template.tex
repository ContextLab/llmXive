\begin{abstract}
This report presents the results of an automated investigation into the relationship between cosmic ray anisotropy and solar cycle modulation. Using data from the IceCube and Pierre Auger observatories, combined with solar proxy indices from NOAA, we analyze the temporal correlation between cosmic ray dipole amplitudes and solar activity. The study employs Lomb-Scargle periodograms, block-bootstrap resampling, and Monte-Carlo shuffle tests to assess statistical significance, applying Bonferroni corrections for multiple hypothesis testing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Cosmic rays exhibit anisotropies in their arrival directions, with a dipole component that may be modulated by the solar cycle. This work investigates the hypothesis that the amplitude and phase of the cosmic ray dipole correlate with solar activity indices such as sunspot numbers and interplanetary magnetic field strength.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Data Acquisition}
Cosmic ray event data were obtained from the IceCube and Pierre Auger observatories. Solar proxy data (sunspot number, solar wind speed, IMF magnitude) were fetched from the NOAA National Geophysical Data Center (NGDC) with exponential backoff retry logic.

\subsection{Temporal Binning}
Events were binned into intervals of configurable duration (default 27 days). Each interval was processed to compute the dipole amplitude and phase using HEALPix maps with $N_{\text{side}}=64$ and spherical harmonic decomposition up to $\ell=2$.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
 \item \textbf{Lomb-Scargle Periodogram:} Used to detect periodicities in the anisotropy time series.
 \item \textbf{Block-Bootstrap Resampling:} Applied to estimate confidence intervals, with adaptive block lengths based on the number of independent blocks.
 \item \textbf{Monte-Carlo Shuffle Test:} Performed to assess the significance of the correlation by shuffling solar proxy series relative to anisotropy data.
 \item \textbf{Bonferroni Correction:} Applied to control the family-wise error rate at $\alpha=0.0017$.
\end{itemize}
