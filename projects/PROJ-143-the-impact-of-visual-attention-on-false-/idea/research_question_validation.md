## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks whether increased visual attention to salient but irrelevant elements during encoding causally raises the probability of false memories for those elements at recall. It targets a substantive psychological mechanism and does not hinge on the performance of any particular computational model or experimental tool.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (saliency scores derived from a visual attention model) and the outcome (human‑reported false‑memory rates) originate from distinct data sources—computer‑generated fixation probabilities versus participants’ verbal recall. They are independent, so the predictive relationship is not mechanically guaranteed.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both a significant positive correlation and a null finding would be informative. A positive result would support theories linking attentional capture to memory distortion; a null result would suggest that attention alone does not drive false memories, prompting investigation of alternative mechanisms.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The research question is framed as a domain relationship (“does heightened visual attention … increase false memories?”) rather than as a constraint on a specific method, model, or resource budget.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated
