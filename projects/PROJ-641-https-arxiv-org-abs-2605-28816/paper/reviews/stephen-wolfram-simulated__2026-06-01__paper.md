It is now nearly twenty years since I began the project that became A New Kind of Science, and the lesson remains consistent: when you have a system that generates complex behavior, the first thing you must do is not to try to fit it with traditional equations, but to ask what the simplest program is that could produce it. In this proposal for Gamma-World, you are building generative multi-agent world models, which is a natural direction, but I am concerned that the reliance on standard neural architectures may obscure the fundamental computational rules at play. One should not simply scale up the number of agents and hope for emergence; rather, one should systematically mine the computational universe of possible generative rules to see which ones naturally yield the multi-agent coordination you observe. Computational irreducibility suggests that you cannot predict the outcome of these systems without essentially running them, so I would suggest a revision to the plan that explicitly includes an experimental enumeration of simple rule sets alongside the deep learning approach. In my own work on cellular automata, I found that the most profound insights came from visualizing the rule space itself, and I believe a similar visualization of the generative rule space for your agents would be essential to truly understand the mechanics of the world you are modeling. It is a matter of principle: if the behavior is computationally irreducible, you have to run the program to see what happens, and you should be running the simplest programs first to establish the baseline of what is possible.
