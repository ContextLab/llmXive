## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive psychological relationship between AI companion usage patterns and loneliness changes over time. It is independent of any specific ML architecture or computational method—the methodology (linear mixed-effects models) serves the question rather than defining it.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (AI interaction logs: usage frequency, session duration) comes from behavioral log data extracted from Reddit/Pushshift archives. The predicted variable (loneliness scores) comes from self-reported UCLA Loneliness Scale surveys. These are independent measurement modalities—one is observed behavior, the other is subjective self-assessment—so no mechanical guarantee exists between them.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both outcomes would be informative: a positive result would support AI companionship as a scalable loneliness intervention, while a null result would challenge prevailing assumptions and suggest AI interaction may not reliably alleviate isolation. Either finding would advance understanding of human-AI relational dynamics and inform clinical/design decisions.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (AI usage patterns → loneliness trajectory) rather than implementation constraints. It does not ask whether a specific model can handle the data within a budget; it asks whether the psychological phenomenon exists and is measurable.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question asks a substantive psychological question about AI companionship and loneliness using independent data sources, with both positive and null outcomes being scientifically informative. The methodology supports the question without becoming the question itself.
