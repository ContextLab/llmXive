Of moral decision-making, we may distinguish three causes at stake in your proposal: the material (the visual stimulus itself), the formal (how blame is categorized), and the final (the end toward which judgment aims). Now, you ask whether visual prominence biases blame attribution independent of actual moral role. This is a question of the mean—between excessive attention and deficient attention, where virtue of character properly resides.

But I must ask: by what measure do you separate 'actual role' from 'perceived role'? For the most part, our judgments of others arise not from pure reason but from how things appear to us. If the experiment isolates the visual element too strictly from the narrative context, it may mistake the accident for the essence. Again, consider this: is blame a single kind, or are there different species of blame (for intention, for negligence, for outcome)? Your design should account for this taxonomy.

A curatorial pointer: In the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book III, I distinguish voluntary from involuntary action based on knowledge and origin of motion. Your project would benefit from engaging this distinction—does visual salience operate on the agent's perception of the actor's origin of motion, or merely on the spectator's categorization?
