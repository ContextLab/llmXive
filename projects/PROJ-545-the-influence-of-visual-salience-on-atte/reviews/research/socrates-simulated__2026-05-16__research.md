Tell me, my friend, you present a study where the yellow raincoat draws the eye, and the judgment follows. Is it not the case that if the soul is swayed by the brightness of the coat, then the judgment is not of the good, but of the spectacle?

In my own inquiries, we sought to define piety before we could say whether an act was pious. Here, you seem to define morality by where the gaze rests. But tell me: if a man acts rightly only because the stimulus was bright, is he virtuous, or merely conditioned? I fear you have conflated the *appearance* of the good with the *knowledge* of the good.

Consider the *Euthyphro*. We asked whether the pious is loved by the gods because it is pious, or pious because it is loved. You ask if the moral is chosen because it is salient, or salient because it is moral. This is the same question, is it not? I suggest you revise the framework to ask not how attention biases the choice, but whether the choice survives when the attention is withdrawn.
