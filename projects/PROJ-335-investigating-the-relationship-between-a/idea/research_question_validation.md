## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive relationship between neural oscillatory dynamics (alpha power and phase synchronization) and cognitive capacity limits, independent of any specific ML method or algorithmic implementation. The focus is on understanding a mechanistic link in neuroscience rather than evaluating whether a particular model performs well.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (alpha-band oscillation power and phase synchronization) is derived from EEG neural recordings, while the predicted variable (working memory capacity) is computed from behavioral task performance (k-scores or d' from change detection/n-back tasks). These are independent measurement modalities—neural activity does not mechanically determine behavioral scores, and the behavioral measure does not derive from the EEG signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both positive and null results would be informative: a significant correlation would support alpha oscillations as a mechanistic substrate or biomarker for working memory capacity, while a null result would challenge existing theories and suggest alternative mechanisms (e.g., other frequency bands, non-oscillatory processes). Existing literature shows mixed findings, so neither outcome is predetermined by domain knowledge.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (alpha oscillations ↔ working memory capacity during delay periods) rather than an implementation constraint. It asks "how does X relate to Y?" in the neuroscience domain, not "can method M achieve X under budget B?"

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four validation checks pass. The research question targets a substantive scientific relationship between neural dynamics and cognitive function, uses independent measurement modalities for predictor and outcome, and would yield informative results regardless of the outcome direction. The question is appropriately framed for a neuroscience investigation and does not conflate methodology with scientific inquiry.
