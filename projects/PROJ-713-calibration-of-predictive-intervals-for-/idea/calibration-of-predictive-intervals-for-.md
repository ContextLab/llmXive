# Calibration of Predictive Intervals for Time‑Series Forecasts

**Field**: statistics

## Research question

Do predictive intervals produced by widely used time‑series forecasting methods (ARIMA, Prophet, and a lightweight LSTM) achieve nominal coverage (e.g., 80 % and 95 %) on standard benchmark datasets such as M4 and UCI Electricity?

## Motivation

Forecast users rely on interval coverage to assess risk, yet most published evaluations focus only on point‑forecast accuracy. Without calibrated intervals, decisions based on forecast uncertainty can be systematically over‑ or under‑confident. Quantifying the calibration of existing, off‑the‑shelf methods on public benchmarks will reveal whether current practice already provides reliable uncertainty estimates or whether additional post‑hoc calibration is needed.

## Related work

- [Probabilistic wind speed forecasting on a grid based on ensemble model output statistics (2015)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.02001) — Demonstrates ensemble‑based probabilistic forecasting and evaluates interval calibration for a physical time‑series (wind speed), providing a methodological precedent for assessing coverage on real‑world data.  
- [Self‑Calibrating Conformal Prediction (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.07307) — Introduces a conformal prediction framework that automatically adjusts predictive intervals to achieve exact marginal coverage, directly relevant as a potential calibration baseline for the methods studied.  
- [Adaptive Conformal Predictions for Time Series (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.07282) — Extends conformal prediction to non‑exchangeable time‑series data, offering techniques and evaluation metrics (e.g., coverage, PIT histograms) that can be applied to ARIMA, Prophet, and LSTM forecasts.

## Expected results

We expect to observe systematic mis‑calibration for at least one of the three baseline models (e.g., ARIMA intervals being too narrow). Calibration will be quantified by (1) empirical coverage deviation from nominal levels, (2) PIT histogram uniformity tests, and (3) proper scoring rules such as the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS). A statistically significant deviation (p < 0.05) will falsify the hypothesis that the intervals are calibrated; otherwise, the hypothesis will be supported.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**: `wget` the M4 dataset (https://github.com/M4Competition/m4-methodology) and the UCI Electricity Load Diagrams dataset (https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/ElectricityLoadDiagrams20112014).  
- **Pre‑processing**: Split each series into a training window (first 80 %) and a hold‑out test window (last 20 %). Standardize each series to zero mean and unit variance.  
- **Model fitting**:
  1. Fit an ARIMA model using `statsmodels.tsa.arima.model.ARIMA`.
  2. Fit a Prophet model via `prophet.Prophet`.
  3. Train a lightweight LSTM (single hidden layer, 32 units) with `tensorflow.keras`, using teacher‑forcing for one‑step ahead predictions.
- **Predictive‑interval generation**:
  - ARIMA: use the model’s conditional variance to construct Gaussian intervals.
  - Prophet: employ its built‑in `uncertainty_samples` to obtain empirical quantiles.
  - LSTM: obtain residuals on a validation slice, assume Gaussian noise, and build intervals; also evaluate a conformal‑prediction wrapper (using the method from *Self‑Calibrating Conformal Prediction*).  
- **Calibration assessment**:
  1. Compute empirical coverage for 80 % and 95 % intervals.
  2. Generate probability‑integral‑transform (PIT) values and test uniformity with the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test.
  3. Calculate CRPS for each forecast using `properscoring.crps_ensemble`.
  4. Perform paired bootstrap tests to compare calibration metrics across models.  
- **Reporting**: Produce tables of coverage deviations, PIT histograms, and CRPS values; summarize statistical significance; discuss whether post‑hoc conformal adjustment improves calibration.
