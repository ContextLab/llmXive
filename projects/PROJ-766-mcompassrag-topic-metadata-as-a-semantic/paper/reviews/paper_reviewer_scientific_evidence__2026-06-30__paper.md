The scientific evidence supporting the central claims of MCompassRAG is generally robust in terms of experimental breadth (six benchmarks) and the inclusion of strong baselines. However, the statistical rigor regarding efficiency claims and the validity of the synthetic training data require clarification.

First, the claim of "over 5x lower latency" (Abstract, Section 2) is not supported by the provided statistical evidence. Table 2 presents single-point latency measurements (e.g., 174ms for MCompassRAG vs. 925ms for SAKI-RAG) without reporting standard deviations, confidence intervals, or the number of runs performed. In LLM-based systems, latency can vary significantly due to GPU load, network jitter, and token generation stochasticity. A single mean value is insufficient to claim a definitive 5x improvement. The authors must provide error bars or statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) across multiple independent runs to validate that the observed latency reduction is not due to random variance.

Second, the training methodology relies entirely on synthetic data generated by GPT-4o (Section 5.1, "Training data construction"). The paper states that GPT-4o generates queries and assigns relevance labels (0/1) to chunk pairs. There is no mention of human evaluation, inter-annotator agreement (IAA), or a validation set with human-annotated ground truth to verify the quality of these synthetic labels. If the LLM teacher has specific biases in how it defines "relevance" or generates queries, the student model may simply be overfitting to these artifacts rather than learning generalizable retrieval skills. The "Information Efficiency" gains could be an artifact of the teacher's specific prompting style. The authors should include a small-scale human evaluation of the synthetic dataset or report IAA scores to demonstrate that the training signal is reliable.

Finally, while the ablation studies (Section 6) effectively demonstrate the contribution of the selection and abstraction modules, the comparison to the "LLM + 10 Topics" oracle (Table 1) lacks statistical depth. The performance gap between MCompassRAG and this oracle is very small (e.g., 94.13 vs. 94.67 on SCI-DOCS). Without statistical testing, it is unclear if this difference is significant or within the margin of error. The claim that MCompassRAG "closely approaches" the oracle is plausible but requires statistical confirmation to be scientifically rigorous.
