# llmXive follow-up: extending "ResearchClawBench: A Benchmark for End-to-End Autonomous Scientific Re"

## Summary of the prior work
ResearchClawBench introduces a benchmark for evaluating end-to-end autonomous scientific research across 40 tasks in 10 domains, revealing that current AI agents struggle significantly with experimental protocol alignment and evidence synthesis, achieving scores far below the threshold for reliable re-discovery. The study utilizes expert-curated multimodal rubrics to score agents on their ability to reconstruct target papers from raw data and literature, identifying "experimental protocol mismatch" and "missing scientific core" as primary failure modes.

## Proposed extension
**Research Question:** Can a "Modular Protocol Injection" framework, where the agent is explicitly provided with a validated, step-by-step experimental scaffold derived from a subset of the training literature (rather than the target paper), significantly reduce the "experimental protocol mismatch" error rate compared to fully autonomous planning? This matters because the original benchmark's error analysis suggests that agents fail not due to a lack of reasoning capability, but due to the combinatorial explosion of correctly sequencing lab procedures, and isolating this variable is CPU-tractable.

## Methodology sketch
**Data:** Select 10 tasks from ResearchClawBench where "experimental protocol mismatch" was the dominant failure mode; for each, construct a "Scaffolded" variant containing a generic, domain-specific protocol template (e.g., "For chemical synthesis, always include purification step X") derived from the provided *related* literature, distinct from the hidden target paper.
**Procedure:** Re-run the seven autonomous agents on both the original "Zero-Shot" tasks and the new "Scaffolded" tasks using the existing ResearchHarness on CPU-only nodes; measure the change in the "Protocol Alignment" sub-score of the expert rubric while keeping the "Scientific Core" score constant to isolate the effect.
**Expected Result:** We hypothesize that the Scaffolded condition will yield a statistically significant increase in the Protocol Alignment sub-score (e.g., from ~15 to ~35 out of 50) without a corresponding drop in the Scientific Core score, demonstrating that the bottleneck is procedural retrieval rather than hypothesis generation.
