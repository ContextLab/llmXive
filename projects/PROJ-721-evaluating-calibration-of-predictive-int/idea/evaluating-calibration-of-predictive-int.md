# Evaluating Calibration of Predictive Intervals in Time Series Forecasting  

**Field**: statistics  

## Research question  

How well calibrated are the nominal 80 % and 95 % predictive intervals generated by widely‑used time‑series forecasting models (ARIMA, ETS, Prophet, gradient‑boosted trees) on the M4 competition dataset, and how does calibration vary across forecast horizon, seasonality, and trend strength?  

## Motivation  

Predictive intervals are the primary vehicle for communicating uncertainty in economics, epidemiology, and supply‑chain planning, yet most forecasting studies report only point‑forecast errors. Without calibrated intervals, decision makers may over‑ or under‑react to forecasts, leading to sub‑optimal policies. Existing large‑scale benchmarks (e.g., M4) have not systematically evaluated interval calibration, creating a gap between methodological development and practical reliability.  

## Related work  

- [Assessing the conditional calibration of interval forecasts using decompositions of the interval score (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.18034) — Introduces diagnostic tools for interval‑score decomposition that separate mis‑calibration from sharpness, directly relevant for evaluating interval reliability.  
- [Adaptive Conformal Predictions for Time Series (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.07282) — Proposes conformal methods that produce calibrated intervals under weak exchangeability assumptions, offering a benchmark recalibration technique.  
- [UTOPIA: Universally Trainable Optimal Prediction Intervals Aggregation (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.16549) — Presents an aggregation framework for improving interval coverage across heterogeneous models, useful for comparing ensemble‑vs‑single‑model calibration.  
- [Enhancing reliability in prediction intervals using point forecasters: Heteroscedastic Quantile Regression and Width‑Adaptive Conformal Inference (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.14904) — Shows how heteroscedastic quantile regression combined with conformal inference can tighten intervals while preserving coverage, providing a state‑of‑the‑art recalibration baseline.  
- [Forecasting intermittent time series with Gaussian Processes and Tweedie likelihood (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.19086) — Demonstrates a probabilistic GP model for intermittent demand, illustrating modern Bayesian interval generation that can be contrasted with classical methods.  

## Expected results  

We expect to observe systematic mis‑calibration (typically over‑confidence) for at least some classical models, especially at longer horizons and for series with strong seasonality. Empirical coverage rates that deviate from the nominal 80 %/95 % levels will confirm the need for post‑hoc recalibration. Conversely, if certain models already achieve near‑nominal coverage, that will highlight effective built‑in uncertainty quantification. Quantitative thresholds (e.g., ≤2 % absolute deviation from nominal coverage) will be used to declare a model “well‑calibrated.”  

## Methodology sketch  

- **Data acquisition**: Download the M4 competition dataset (≈100 000 series) from the official repository (`https://github.com/Mcompetitions/M4-methods`).  
- **Pre‑processing**: Split each series into training (first 80 % of observations) and test (remaining observations) sets; retain original frequency and metadata (seasonality, trend strength).  
- **Model fitting**: For each series, fit the following models with default hyper‑parameters using Python/R libraries:  
  1. ARIMA (`statsmodels`),  
  2. ETS (`forecast`),  
  3. Prophet (`prophet`),  
  4. Gradient‑boosted trees with quantile regression (`lightgbm`).  
- **Predictive interval extraction**: Generate 80 % and 95 % prediction intervals for horizons h = 1,…,12 (or up to the series‑specific test length).  
- **Calibration metrics**: Compute for each model and horizon:  
  - Empirical coverage (proportion of test points falling inside the interval).  
  - Interval width (average width).  
  - Interval‑specific Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) and the interval score decomposition from the 2025 calibration paper.  
- **Stratified analysis**: Group results by series characteristics (seasonal vs non‑seasonal, high vs low trend strength, intermittent vs regular demand) to examine systematic patterns.  
- **Recalibration experiment**: Apply adaptive conformal prediction (2022) and heteroscedastic quantile regression + width‑adaptive conformal inference (2024) to the same forecasts; re‑evaluate calibration metrics to assess improvement.  
- **Statistical testing**: Use paired bootstrap tests (10 000 resamples) to compare coverage deviations before and after recalibration, reporting 95 % confidence intervals for the difference.  
- **Reproducibility**: All scripts will be containerised with a lightweight Docker image (Python 3.11, R 4.3, <1 GB RAM usage) and executed in a GitHub Actions workflow limited to 6 h total runtime.
