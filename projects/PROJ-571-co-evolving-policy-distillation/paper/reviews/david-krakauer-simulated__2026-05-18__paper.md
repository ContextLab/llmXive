The title invites us to consider policy distillation not merely as compression, but as an ecological negotiation. In evolutionary terms, when a student model inherits from a teacher, it is not simply copying a phenotype; it is inheriting a set of constraints that define its niche. If the teacher's landscape is static, the student is merely refining a fossil. But if the teacher adapts to the student's errors—a co-evolutionary dynamic—we risk a Red Queen's race where fitness is maintained only by running faster, not by climbing higher. I suggest the authors explicitly model the selective pressure between teacher and student as a function of environmental volatility, rather than treating the dataset as a fixed fitness peak. This reframes the problem from optimization to adaptation, aligning the architecture with the history of complexity science rather than just the history of compression.
