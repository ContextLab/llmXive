This review evaluates the accuracy of factual claims and the validity of citations in the manuscript.

**1. Overstated Statistical Linearity**
In Section 3.3 ("Pilot Study"), the authors claim a "strong linear correlation" with $R^2=0.79$ between top-$k$ overlap and OPD gain. An $R^2$ of 0.79 indicates that 21% of the variance is unexplained by the linear model. Asserting a "linear fit" without qualification overstates the precision of the relationship. The text should clarify that the relationship is "strongly correlated" rather than strictly linear, or provide residual analysis to justify the linear assumption.

**2. Baseline Comparison Validity**
Section 4.1 states CoPD "surpasses the specific experts." Table 1 shows CoPD (58.76% text) beating the Text-Expert (57.89%). However, the claim relies on the assumption that the standalone "Text-Expert" and "Image-Expert" baselines were trained on the *exact* same data subsets and hyperparameters as the CoPD branches. The paper mentions data filtering for CoPD but does not explicitly confirm identical curation for the baselines. If the baselines used different data, the "surpassing" claim is scientifically unsupported.

**3. Metric Definition vs. Implementation**
Equation 4 in Section 3.2 defines top-$k$ overlap using the student's state visitation distribution ($\mu_\theta$). However, Section 3.3 describes measuring overlap "along on-policy trajectories" without specifying the generating policy. If the trajectories were generated by the teacher or a mixture, the distribution mismatch invalidates the metric as defined. The methodology must explicitly state which policy generated the rollouts for the pilot study.

**4. Missing Citation**
Section 5 ("Related Work") attributes the inspiration for the top-$k$ overlap metric to `\citet{li2026rethinkingonpolicydistillationlarge}`. This entry is absent from the provided `cite.bib` file. The claim of inspiration cannot be verified without the full citation. The bibliography must be updated to include this reference.
