# Assessing Orbital Period Dependence of the Exoplanet Radius Gap

**Field**: physics

## Research question

Does the location of the exoplanet radius gap (the deficit of planets between ~1.5–2.0 Earth radii) shift systematically with orbital period, and if so, does the observed scaling favor photoevaporation or core-powered mass loss as the dominant atmospheric loss mechanism?

## Motivation

The radius gap is a key demographic feature in exoplanet populations, but competing theories predict different period-dependence for the gap location: photoevaporation predicts a steeper decline in gap radius with period than core-powered mass loss. Resolving which scaling behavior matches observations will constrain planetary atmospheric evolution models and inform theories of planet formation.

## Related work

- [The California-Kecker Survey. III. A Gap in the Radius Distribution of Small Planets (2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.10375) — Establishes the radius gap as a bimodal distribution feature using precise CKS radius measurements.
- [Current Population Statistics Do Not Favor Photoevaporation over Core-Powered Mass Loss as the Dominant Cause of the Exoplanet Radius Gap (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.12305) — Directly compares photoevaporation and core-powered mass loss predictions using period-dependent gap analysis.
- [Evidence that Core-Powered Mass-Loss Dominates Over Photoevaporation in Shaping the Kepler Radius Valley (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.00009) — Argues for core-powered mass loss based on period-dependent valley location measurements.
- [A fading radius valley towards M-dwarfs, a persistent density valley across stellar types (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.01967) — Examines how the radius valley varies with stellar type, relevant for period-dependent trends across different host stars.
- [Examining the Radius Valley: a Machine Learning Approach (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.12048) — Applies ML methods to characterize the radius valley, demonstrating alternative analysis approaches for gap detection.

## Expected results

We expect to detect a statistically significant slope in gap radius versus log-period, with the measured slope distinguishable between the photoevaporation and core-powered mass loss predictions. A slope consistent with photoevaporation (~-0.09 to -0.11) or core-powered mass loss (~-0.06 to -0.07) will provide evidence favoring one mechanism, while a null slope would challenge both frameworks.

## Methodology sketch

- Download Kepler DR25 catalog from MAST archive (https://archive.stsci.edu/kepler/dr25/) via HTTPS, filtering for confirmed planets with radius uncertainty <20% and period uncertainty <1%.
- Extract stellar parameters (radius, mass, effective temperature) from Kepler Input Catalog to compute incident flux and refine planet radius estimates.
- Bin planets by orbital period (log-spaced bins, 5–100 days range, minimum 30 planets per bin for statistical power).
- In each period bin, fit a two-component Gaussian mixture model to the planet radius distribution to identify the gap location (valley between peaks).
- Bootstrap resample (1000 iterations) to estimate uncertainty on gap location in each bin.
- Fit a linear regression of gap radius vs. log(period) and compute slope with 95% confidence interval.
- Compare measured slope to theoretical predictions from photoevaporation (~-0.1) and core-powered mass loss (~-0.06) using a z-test.
- Validate results by repeating analysis with alternative gap-finding methods (e.g., kernel density estimation with adaptive bandwidth).
