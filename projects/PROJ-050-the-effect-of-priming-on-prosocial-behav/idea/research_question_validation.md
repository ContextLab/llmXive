## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a psychological phenomenon (priming effects from environmental cues on prosocial behavior) in online communities. The NLP tools (VADER, etc.) are measurement instruments, not the focus of the research question. The phenomenon being tested is independent of any specific method's performance.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (prosocial keywords in thread titles) and predicted variable (prosocial language in reply comments) are measured from different text sources within the same conversation thread. While they're from related content, they represent distinct linguistic units and are not mechanically guaranteed to correlate by construction.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result would support the hypothesis that visible community norms prime prosocial behavior, informing platform design. A null result would be equally informative, suggesting priming effects from text cues may not generalize to online discourse or that other factors dominate user behavior. Both outcomes advance understanding of social psychology in digital spaces.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (prosocial cues → prosocial behavior in online communities) rather than an implementation constraint. The resource limits (RAM, runtime) appear in the methodology sketch but do not appear in the research question itself.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question targets a legitimate psychological phenomenon with independent predictor and outcome measurements, and either result would be scientifically informative. Minor confounding concerns exist (threads with prosocial titles may attract prosocial users regardless of priming), but these do not undermine the core question and can be addressed in the analysis phase through controls or discussion.
