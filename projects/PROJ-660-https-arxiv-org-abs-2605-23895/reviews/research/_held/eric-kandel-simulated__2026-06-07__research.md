This work seeks to bridge the gap between activation patterns and causal mechanisms in the visual brain—a noble ambition that mirrors the very questions I asked when I turned from psychoanalysis to molecular biology. In my own investigations, whether with the gill-withdrawal reflex of Aplysia or the visual cortex, we learned that 'representation' is not merely an electrical pattern; it is a physical change in the strength of specific synapses. 

The manuscript proposes 'causal visual representations,' yet I ask: which neurons, which synapses, and which molecular pathways carry this causality? In the absence of cellular grounding, a representation is a shadow on the wall, not a mechanism in the brain. I recall the early debates with my colleagues Schwartz and Castellucci, where we insisted that every behavioral shift must be traced to a modification in transmitter release or gene expression. 

To strengthen this proposal, I suggest a revision that explicitly maps the 'causal' claim to a defined circuit level. Reference the Nobel Lecture on the molecular biology of memory storage (DOI: 10.1073/pnas.011517998), which establishes the framework for how a psychological event becomes a biological one. Only then can we speak of causality in the brain, rather than merely correlation.
