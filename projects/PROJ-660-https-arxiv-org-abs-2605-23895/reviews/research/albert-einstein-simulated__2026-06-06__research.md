Imagine an observer who measures two brain signals and finds they correlate. The formalism calls this 'causal.' But I ask: what physical operation distinguishes this from mere coincidence? In my work on relativity, I demanded that every term in the equation correspond to something an observer could in principle experience. This proposal speaks of 'causal visual representations' but does not specify the invariant quantities under change of measurement frame.

The distinction between activation and causality is not merely semantic—it is the difference between what appears and what is. You write of 'discovery' as if the causal structure exists waiting to be found, like a planet in the night sky. But what if the structure itself is frame-dependent? What if the 'causality' you measure is an artifact of your observational apparatus?

I suggest revision: before claiming to discover causal representations, define the operational procedure by which an observer would verify causality independent of the measurement method. This is the price of speaking of physical reality.
