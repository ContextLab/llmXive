The manuscript presents an ambitious pipeline, BrainCause, that combines large‑scale generative models, language models, and an image‑to‑fMRI encoder to generate controlled stimulus sets and rank voxels by “causal” scores. While the technical contribution is clear, several claims extend beyond what the presented data and methods can reliably support.

1. **Reliance on predicted fMRI as primary evidence**  
   The core discovery results (e.g., Table 1, Fig. 3) are largely based on activation and causal scores computed on *predicted* fMRI responses. The authors filter voxels with Pearson > 0.2, but no systematic analysis is provided on how prediction error propagates to the causal scores. Consequently, statements such as “BrainCause successfully recovers known functional localizations and identifies new candidate representations across dozens of concepts” are over‑generalized; the “new” representations have little or no measured‑data validation (see Fig. 9 and the retrieval statistics, where many concepts have < 10 measured positives). The paper should explicitly limit these claims to “model‑based evidence” and discuss the uncertainty introduced by the encoder.

2. **Scope of causal inference**  
   The term “causal” is used repeatedly (e.g., “causal ranking”, “causal evaluation”, “causal representations”). The framework only tests a limited set of counterfactual edits and semantic negatives generated by current language/vision models. The authors acknowledge that failures in negative generation can leave correlated factors untested (Sec. 6), yet they still present the method as providing “causal discovery”. This overstates the strength of the inference; a more accurate phrasing would emphasize “controlled, model‑based specificity testing” rather than full causal attribution.

3. **Statistical validation**  
   The significance testing described in Appendix B (empirical p‑values vs. baseline concepts) is performed per concept but the manuscript does not correct for the 260 simultaneous tests, nor does it specify a pre‑registered p‑threshold. The claim that “causal ranking reduces false discoveries” (Fig. 4) is therefore conditional on an arbitrary threshold. Adding a multiple‑comparison correction (e.g., FDR) and reporting the number of concepts that survive would make the claim more defensible.

4. **Coverage of measured data**  
   The retrieval analysis (Fig. 8) shows that many concepts have sparse measured positives/negatives, yet the paper treats high causal scores on generated data as evidence of discovery for those concepts. This extrapolation is not justified without additional experimental validation. The authors should either restrict the “new candidate representations” claim to concepts with sufficient measured coverage or explicitly label the others as “promising hypotheses requiring follow‑up experiments”.

5. **Comparisons to baselines**  
   The activation‑based baselines achieve higher activation scores but lower causal scores, which is expected given the different objectives. However, the manuscript sometimes interprets the higher activation as “false positives” without quantifying how many of those voxels are truly non‑selective. A more balanced discussion would acknowledge that activation alone can be informative for broad categories, and that causal scores may penalize genuine but weakly selective representations.

6. **Presentation of results**  
   Several figures (e.g., Fig. 5, Fig. S2) display bright, extensive activation maps for activation‑based methods, contrasted with sparser causal maps. While visually compelling, the paper does not provide quantitative measures of map sparsity or overlap, making it difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed selectivity. Adding overlap metrics (e.g., Dice coefficient) would strengthen the argument.

In summary, the paper introduces a valuable framework for generating targeted stimulus sets and evaluating voxel specificity, but the current manuscript overstates the certainty of its discoveries, particularly where evidence relies on model predictions rather than measured brain data. Addressing the points above—especially by providing measured‑data validation for a representative subset of concepts and clarifying the limits of the causal claims—will bring the conclusions into alignment with the presented evidence.
