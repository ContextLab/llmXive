## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a relationship between social media consumption patterns (platform-switching frequency) and cognitive flexibility performance, independent of any specific analytical method. The methodology (survey data analysis, regression) is the tool for answering the question, not the subject of the question itself.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (platform-switching frequency from self-reported survey data on social media usage) and the predicted variable (cognitive flexibility from standardized tests like WCST, Trail Making, or Stroop) are measured through independent instruments. One captures behavioral patterns, the other captures cognitive performance.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both outcomes would be informative: a negative correlation would support the mechanism that task-switching specifically impairs set-shifting, while a null result would challenge prevailing assumptions about digital well-being and suggest total screen time or content type matter more. Either finding would advance intervention strategies for educators and clinicians.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (platform-switching frequency → cognitive flexibility performance) rather than an implementation constraint. It does not fixate on whether a specific model or algorithm can perform the analysis within budget constraints.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question asks about a substantive psychological phenomenon (whether platform-switching patterns impair cognitive flexibility), uses independent measurement sources for predictor and outcome, and would produce publishable results under either positive or null findings. The project can proceed to initialization.
