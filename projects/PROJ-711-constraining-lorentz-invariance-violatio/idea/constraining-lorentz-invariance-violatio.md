# Constraining Lorentz Invariance Violation with Fermi Gamma‑Ray Burst Spectra  

**Field**: physics  

## Research question  

What upper bound on the linear Lorentz‑invariance‑violation energy scale \(E_{\rm QG}\) can be derived from spectral‑lag measurements across multiple energy bands in the brightest Fermi‑GBM gamma‑ray bursts?  

## Motivation  

Quantum‑gravity models often predict an energy‑dependent photon speed, which would appear as tiny arrival‑time differences between high‑ and low‑energy photons from distant GRBs. Existing limits rely on a handful of very bright events or on very‑high‑energy instruments; a systematic analysis of the publicly available Fermi‑GBM catalog could tighten or independently confirm those bounds using modest compute resources.  

## Related work  

- [Limits on Lorentz invariance violation at the Planck energy scale from H.E.S.S. spectral analysis of the blazar Mrk 501 (2016)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.08600) — Demonstrates how energy‑dependent photon propagation can be constrained with high‑energy γ‑ray spectra, providing a benchmark for the magnitude of limits achievable with current instruments.  
- [Gamma-ray and Cosmic-ray Tests of Lorentz Invariance Violation and Quantum Gravity Models and Their Implications (2009)](https://arxiv.org/abs/0912.0500) — Reviews a variety of astrophysical tests of LIV, including spectral‑lag methods in GRBs, and summarizes the theoretical framework (linear dispersion relation) used throughout the field.  
- [Observations of Gamma-ray Bursts with ASTRO‑H and Fermi (2015)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1503.01182) — Discusses the capabilities of Fermi for GRB prompt‑emission spectroscopy and provides examples of time‑resolved spectral analysis that can be leveraged for lag measurements.  

## Expected results  

We expect to obtain a 95 % confidence upper limit on the linear LIV energy scale \(E_{\rm QG}\) that is competitive with (or improves upon) the \(\sim10^{19}\) GeV limits reported in the literature. Confirmation will be the consistency of the derived limit across the sample of ≈30 bright GRBs and the lack of a systematic bias in simulated control analyses; a significantly weaker bound would still be a valuable null result demonstrating the limits of Fermi‑GBM data for this purpose.  

## Methodology sketch  

- **Data acquisition**:  
  - Download the Fermi‑GBM burst catalog and public light‑curve files for the ~30 brightest GRBs (available via the Fermi Science Support Center).  
  - Retrieve auxiliary information (redshift, sky position) from the GRB online tables when available.  
- **Pre‑processing**:  
  - Re‑bin each light curve into several energy bands (e.g., 8–50 keV, 50–200 keV, 200–1000 keV).  
  - Apply background subtraction using the standard GBM offline tools.  
- **Spectral‑lag measurement**:  
  - Compute the cross‑correlation function (CCF) between the lowest‑energy band and each higher‑energy band.  
  - Identify the lag as the CCF peak position; estimate its statistical uncertainty via bootstrap resampling of photon arrival times.  
- **LIV constraint estimation**:  
  - Assume the linear dispersion relation \(\Delta t = \frac{E}{E_{\rm QG}} \frac{D}{c}\) (where \(D\) is the cosmological distance).  
  - Perform a weighted linear regression of measured lag versus photon energy for each burst, extracting an estimate of \(1/E_{\rm QG}\).  
  - Combine individual burst estimates using inverse‑variance weighting to produce a global upper bound.  
- **Monte‑Carlo uncertainty quantification**:  
  - Generate synthetic light curves preserving the observed temporal structure but randomizing photon energies.  
  - Repeat the lag‑measurement and regression pipeline on 10 000 simulated datasets to build the null distribution of \(E_{\rm QG}\) estimates.  
- **Systematic assessment**:  
  - Vary background‑subtraction parameters and energy‑band definitions to evaluate robustness.  
  - Compare results against the simulated null distribution to ensure no spurious detection.  
- **Reproducibility**:  
  - All analysis scripts (Python, NumPy/SciPy, Astropy) and a `requirements.txt` will be committed to the repository.  
  - Results (lag measurements, regression coefficients, final limit) will be saved as CSV files and plotted with Matplotlib for inclusion in the final report.
