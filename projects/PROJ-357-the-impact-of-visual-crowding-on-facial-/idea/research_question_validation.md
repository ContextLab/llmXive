## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about the relationship between visual crowding density and facial emotion recognition accuracy in human perception, which is a substantive phenomenon in vision science. The computational modeling approach is a tool to estimate perceptual thresholds, but the research question itself is independent of any specific method's performance.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (visual crowding density: flanker count, eccentricity, clutter metrics) is an experimental manipulation of stimulus conditions. The predicted variable (emotion recognition accuracy) is a behavioral/perceptual outcome. These are independent measurements—the crowding manipulation does not mechanically determine the recognition accuracy without the human visual system's processing.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A significant negative correlation would quantify how crowding impairs socially critical perception, constraining models of social cognition and informing environment design. A null result would be equally informative, suggesting emotion recognition is robust to visual bottlenecks—protecting threat detection and empathy mechanisms. Either outcome advances theoretical understanding.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (crowding density → recognition accuracy across emotion categories) rather than an implementation constraint. It asks "how does X affect Y" in the visual system, not "can method M achieve accuracy B within budget C."

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question addresses a clear gap in human psychophysics literature, uses independent predictor and outcome variables, and would yield informative results regardless of outcome direction. The computational modeling approach supports but does not define the scientific question.
