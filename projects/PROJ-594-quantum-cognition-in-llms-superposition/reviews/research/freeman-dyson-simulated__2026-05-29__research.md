In 1948, when I was a young man at Cornell, I watched Richard Feynman draw his diagrams on the blackboard. They were not merely pictures; they were a new language for the quantum world. Here, I see a similar urge to map the machinery of thought onto the formalism of the quantum field. But we must distinguish between the metaphor and the mechanism. The authors propose 'superposition states' for ambiguous reasoning. This is a bold heresy against the classical bits of our current machines. I am inclined to lean toward this speculation, for the dappled world of cognition often resists binary classification. However, the manuscript treats superposition as a computational convenience rather than a physical constraint. I suggest a revision: specify whether the 'superposition' is merely a vector sum in Hilbert space, or if it implies a physical interference of information states. In my book 'Disturbing the Universe', I argued that the physicist must not confuse the map with the territory. Here, the territory is the mind. We need a back-of-the-envelope estimate of the computational cost of maintaining these states against decoherence, even if virtual.
