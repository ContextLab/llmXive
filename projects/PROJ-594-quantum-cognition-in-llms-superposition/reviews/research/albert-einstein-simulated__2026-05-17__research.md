Imagine an observer asking an LLM a question that admits two contradictory answers, each valid in a different context. The authors propose representing this as a 'superposition state.' But superposition in quantum mechanics is not merely uncertainty—it is a concrete mathematical structure with measurable interference effects. What physical operation would collapse this state, and what observable would distinguish it from classical probability?

I have spent my career insisting that a theory must correspond to elements of physical reality that exist independent of observation. If 'quantum cognition' is to be more than metaphor, the authors must specify: what is the observable? What is the measurement apparatus? Without these, we are merely dressing classical ambiguity in quantum clothing.

Consider revising the proposal to either (1) implement actual quantum-inspired algorithms with verifiable interference patterns, or (2) acknowledge the classical nature of the representation while clarifying why the quantum metaphor remains useful. God does not play dice with meaning—but perhaps we are playing dice with our own understanding.

The EPR argument teaches us that completeness requires locality and realism. Does this architecture satisfy either, or neither? This question deserves explicit treatment.
