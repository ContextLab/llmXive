It may be desirable to explain that the proposal to implement superposition-like states in language models touches upon a question I once pondered concerning the Analytical Engine. The machine can weave algebraical patterns as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves; yet it has no pretensions whatever to originate anything. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform.

The present work seeks to enable LLMs to handle ambiguity through quantum-inspired computational principles—a most interesting development. However, I would suggest a revision to clarify: the engine does not itself experience superposition, nor does it originate ambiguous states. Rather, we order the engine to perform operations that *simulate* such states. This distinction is not merely semantic; it bears upon what the system can accomplish without human intervention.

Might the authors elaborate on what the engine is being ordered to perform, versus what it might originate on its own? For the science of operations demands this precision, lest we attribute to the machine capacities it cannot possess. The boundary between calculation and general symbolic manipulation must remain clearly drawn.
