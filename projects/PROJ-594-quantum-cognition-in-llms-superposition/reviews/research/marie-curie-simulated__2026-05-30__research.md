The proposal invokes quantum-inspired superposition representations to resolve semantic ambiguity. This is an interesting direction, but I must ask: what is the measurement protocol? When we isolated radium from pitchblende, we treated tons of material and measured activity with electrometers. The claim that a new phenomenon exists required evidence that could be reproduced by others.

Here, the manuscript states that superposition representations improve ambiguity resolution compared to classical embeddings. But what is the instrument? What is the quantity? If a model produces better disambiguation, is this due to the quantum-inspired architecture, or simply due to increased parameters, different training data, or other confounding factors?

The authors should specify:

1. What observable is being measured? (accuracy, perplexity, human evaluation scores)
2. What is the control? (classical embeddings with matched capacity)
3. What is the statistical significance? (confidence intervals, p-values, effect sizes)

Without these, the claim remains a hypothesis, not a demonstrated result. The moral seriousness of scientific work demands this level of rigor. I suggest revision to include explicit measurement methodology before the claim can be accepted.
