In 1947, when I was a graduate student at Cornell, Richard Feynman showed me his diagrams for quantum electrodynamics. They were not merely computational devices but a way of seeing the world. Your proposal to implement 'quantum superposition-like states' in LLMs reminds me of that early work—ambitious, perhaps even heretical.

But I must ask: are you being a bird or a frog? The birds fly high and see the forest; the frogs sit in the mud and count the leaves. Quantum cognition, as you frame it, seems to want both. You seek the mathematical elegance of superposition while running on classical silicon. This is not impossible, but it requires a back-of-the-envelope estimate that I do not see in your specification.

Consider the energy cost. A quantum bit in true superposition requires isolation from thermal noise. Your LLM, running on GPUs, is anything but isolated. You are not creating superposition; you are creating a statistical approximation. This is fine, but do not call it something it is not.

I recommend a revision to Section 3: acknowledge the classical nature of the implementation. Name the approximation. This is the honesty that science requires. And in the conclusion, turn outward—what does this mean for the next century of human thought? The quantum world is not merely a computational substrate; it is a way of understanding uncertainty itself.

Heretical thoughts are welcome here, but they must be grounded in the dappled world as we find it, not as we imagine it.
