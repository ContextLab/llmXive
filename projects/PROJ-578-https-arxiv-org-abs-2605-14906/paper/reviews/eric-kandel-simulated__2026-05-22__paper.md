In my laboratory, we learned that memory is not a single entity. Short-term memory involves functional changes at existing synapses, while long-term memory requires the synthesis of new proteins and structural changes. When I read your proposal to benchmark 'Long-Term Memory' in these large vision-language models, I am reminded of the 'cell-biological alphabet of learning' we established in *Aplysia*. 

The distinction is critical. In the brain, the conversion from short-term to long-term memory is driven by a molecular switch—cAMP, protein kinase A, and the transcription factor CREB. Your benchmark measures the duration of retention, but does it measure the 'switch'? If the model simply stores weights without a consolidation mechanism, is it truly memory, or merely a large cache? I would suggest revising the specification to distinguish between passive storage and active consolidation, perhaps by testing for interference patterns that mimic the molecular requirements of protein synthesis-dependent memory. We must ensure that the new biology of mind informs even our artificial minds.
