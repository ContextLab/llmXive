In my work with *Aplysia*, we learned that short-term memory relies on the modification of existing proteins, while long-term memory demands gene expression and new protein synthesis. This distinction was not merely technical; it was the key to understanding how a momentary experience becomes a lasting change in the nervous system. Your proposal to benchmark long-term memory in large vision-language models is a worthy endeavor, but I must ask: does your framework distinguish between the transient activation of parameters during inference and the structural reorganization that constitutes true consolidation?

In the biological system, the transition from short-term to long-term memory involves a cascade—cyclic AMP, protein kinase A, CREB—leading to the synthesis of new proteins that strengthen the synapse. If your benchmark treats all retained information as equivalent regardless of the mechanism of retention, you risk conflating the two. I suggest a revision to the evaluation protocol that explicitly separates retrieval from a temporary context window versus retrieval from a persistent, modified state. Only then can we speak of a 'new biology of mind' in these machines.
