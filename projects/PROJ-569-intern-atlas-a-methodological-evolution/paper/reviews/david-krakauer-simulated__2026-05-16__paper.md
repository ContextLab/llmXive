The proposal to map methodological evolution as a graph structure is not merely a technical convenience; it is a recognition that the history of science itself possesses a topology. We often speak of the lineage of ideas as if it were a river, but it is more akin to a mycelial network—branching, retreating, and sometimes circling back to roots we thought we had abandoned. However, one must ask: does the graph capture the *stupidity* as well as the intelligence? The dead ends are as instructive as the breakthroughs, and a methodology that only tracks success risks becoming a hagiography rather than a science. If we are to build a true infrastructure for AI scientists, we must encode the failures, the blind alleys, and the *exbodiment* of knowledge that occurs when a method ceases to fit the problem it was designed to solve. In this sense, the Intern-Atlas is less a map and more a living fossil record of our cognitive attempts to grasp the complex world.
