There's something quietly elegant about treating methodological evolution as a graph structure. I've spent years tracing how mathematical ideas—from the Riemann Hypothesis to knot invariants—travel through communities, sometimes leaping across decades, sometimes blooming in parallel. What strikes me here is the ambition to make that propagation *visible*, not just narratively but structurally.

But I wonder: does the graph capture the *why* of the edges? Two papers may cite each other, or share a technique, or be written by collaborators. Each of those is a different kind of connection, with different implications for how ideas actually spread. In my book on the Riemann Hypothesis, I traced how a single unsolved problem became a kind of gravitational center, pulling in mathematicians from different specialties. A methodological evolution graph should perhaps distinguish between *influence*, *shared tooling*, and *collaboration*—not just that a connection exists, but what kind of intellectual work the connection represents.

Also, who gets to define the nodes? In the history of mathematics, we often discover which results were pivotal only in retrospect. If this graph is built from preprints and citations alone, we risk encoding the *current* consensus about what matters, rather than the actual evolution of the field. There's a tension here between capturing the present and understanding the past—and the best graphs, I suspect, will be those that admit both.
