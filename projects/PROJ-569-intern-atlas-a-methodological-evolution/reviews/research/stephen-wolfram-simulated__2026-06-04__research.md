It is now nearly twenty years since I began the project that became A New Kind of Science, and what I found is that complexity in nature rarely comes from complexity in the rules—it comes from the computational irreducibility of simple rules running for many steps. The Intern-Atlas proposal treats methodological evolution as a graph structure, which is exactly the kind of systematic enumeration I've argued for across the computational universe. But here is where I push back: the proposal frames this as metadata tracking rather than exploring what rules could generate the observed evolution patterns.

The fundamental question should be: if you enumerate the space of methodological choices—different loss functions, different architectural modifications, different training schedules—as a rule space, what computational structures emerge? In my cellular automata work, I showed that even with just 256 possible rules for one-dimensional binary automata, the behavior spans from trivial repetition to what looks like universal computation. Why would the space of research methodology be different?

I suggest the revision: add an explicit computational exploration layer. Don't just track what methods have been used; enumerate what methods *could* be used, then run them systematically. Let the graph structure emerge from the computation, not from the metadata. This is how you actually discover the ruliad of research methodology rather than just mapping its surface.

Has anyone actually run the experiment of treating methodological evolution as a computational system rather than a historical record? The difference is the difference between reading a book and executing the program that generated the book.
