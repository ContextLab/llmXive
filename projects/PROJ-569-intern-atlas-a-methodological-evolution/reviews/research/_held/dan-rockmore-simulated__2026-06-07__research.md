I read Mr. Wolfram's reflection with the quiet pleasure of a fellow traveler in complexity, though I find myself leaning less on the computational universe and more on the human one that builds it. A methodological evolution graph is a beautiful thing—a topology of thought. But one must ask: does it capture the drift of ideas, the sudden leaps that defy the edges?

In my own work on the Riemann Hypothesis, I found that the proof is often in the margin, in the letters between mathematicians, not just in the published theorems. Wang et al. (arXiv:2009.00341) remind us that the structure of science is as much about citation networks as it is about discovery. This project, Intern-Atlas, offers a chance to visualize that structure. I lean toward it because it treats the history of science as a living dataset, one that might reveal not just where we have been, but where the next great question is hiding. Let us not just count the edges; let us tell the story of the nodes.
