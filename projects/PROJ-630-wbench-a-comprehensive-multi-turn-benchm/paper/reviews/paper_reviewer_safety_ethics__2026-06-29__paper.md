This review focuses exclusively on safety and ethics considerations regarding the WBench benchmark and its evaluation protocol.

**Human Subject Ethics**
The paper describes a human preference study involving 400 crowdworkers (Appendix: Human-Preference Annotation Platform). While the text mentions quality control measures (gold-standard checks, expert review), it lacks a formal statement regarding the ethical treatment of these participants. Standard academic practice requires disclosure of Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval or equivalent ethical oversight, confirmation of informed consent, and details on fair compensation. Without this, the human evaluation component does not meet typical publication standards for research involving human subjects. Please add a dedicated paragraph in the Appendix or main text confirming compliance with ethical guidelines.

**Data Privacy and Consent**
The authors state that test data are "synthetic or openly licensed, avoiding real identities" (Appendix: Broader Impact). This is a positive step that mitigates privacy risks associated with using real-world footage. However, the dataset includes human subjects (64% of cases). While generated by models like Nano Banana 2 and GPT-Image-1.5, the paper should explicitly confirm that these synthetic humans do not resemble real individuals to prevent potential likeness misuse. A brief statement on the synthetic nature of human subjects would strengthen this claim.

**Dual-Use and Misuse Risks**
The Broader Impact section acknowledges "mitigating misuse risks" but remains high-level. Interactive video world models have significant dual-use potential, including the generation of realistic deepfakes for disinformation or non-consensual content. The benchmark itself could inadvertently accelerate the development of such capabilities by providing a standardized evaluation for realism and consistency. The authors should expand this section to discuss specific risks (e.g., identity fraud, political manipulation) and any safeguards implemented (e.g., watermarking, access controls for the benchmark data).

**Evaluation Protocol Safety**
The web-based evaluation protocol for closed models (Genie 3, Happy Oyster) uses browser automation (Appendix: Web-Based Evaluation Protocol). While generally safe, the authors should confirm that this automation complies with the terms of service of the evaluated platforms to avoid ethical concerns regarding unauthorized access or scraping.

Addressing these points will ensure the paper meets ethical standards for human research and responsibly addresses the societal implications of the technology.
