## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: concern

The question asks about the information-theoretic capacity of translation trajectories to encode physical stability, which is a substantive domain question. However, the phrasing "To what extent... implicitly encode" is slightly ambiguous and risks being interpreted as a method-benchmark question ("can our model find it?") rather than a physics question ("does the signal exist?"). The core inquiry is valid, but the framing leans slightly toward evaluating the model's ability to extract the signal rather than the signal's existence itself.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (monocular translation trajectories) is derived from kinematic data (position over time), while the predicted variable (object stability/failure modes) is derived from physics simulation metrics (tipping angle, slippage). While the translation data is generated by the same physics engine that produces the labels, they represent distinct physical phenomena: the input is the *action* taken, and the output is the *consequence* of that action under specific physical constraints. The relationship is causal (action → outcome), not mechanically guaranteed by shared mathematical definitions.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result would be significant, proving that translation alone contains sufficient "hidden" physical cues (like friction or mass distribution effects) to predict failure, enabling sensor-free control. A null result would also be highly informative, demonstrating that rotational dynamics or explicit force feedback are strictly necessary for stability prediction in bi-manual tasks, thereby refuting the sufficiency of translation-only approaches. Neither outcome is predetermined by current domain knowledge.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a specific domain relationship (the encoding of stability information within translation trajectories) and explicitly excludes explicit force sensing to test a hypothesis about information sufficiency. While the motivation mentions resource constraints, the research question itself focuses on the *capacity* of the data modality, not the computational budget of the training process (which is a separate feasibility constraint).

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

The project investigates a genuine information-theoretic gap in robotic manipulation: whether kinematic action signals alone contain enough physical information to predict dynamic failure. While the question could be slightly sharpened to emphasize the physical phenomenon over the model's ability to find it, the core inquiry is sound, non-circular, and yields informative results regardless of the outcome. The implementation constraints (CPU, 6h) are appropriate for the scope but do not define the research question itself.
