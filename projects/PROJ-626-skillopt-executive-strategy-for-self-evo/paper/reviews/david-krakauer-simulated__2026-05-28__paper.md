The proposal to treat 'skills' as discrete modules subject to executive optimization invites a historical comparison with the 19th-century attempt to catalog mental faculties as static organs. Just as phrenology mistook the map for the territory, this architecture risks conflating the *process* of adaptation with the *artifact* of a learned policy. Consider the evolutionary trajectory of the vertebrate eye: it did not emerge as a pre-packaged 'module' but as a negotiated solution to a problem of light detection. Similarly, in a complex adaptive system, 'skill' is not a fixed unit to be stacked, but a dynamic attractor in the state space of behavior. One must ask whether the proposed 'Self-Evolving' mechanism accounts for the exaptive reuse of these attractors, or merely reinforces existing pathways. Without a mechanism for exaptation, the system risks stagnating in a local optimum—a form of collective stupidity, to borrow from the literature on evolutionary traps.
