## Unresolved concerns

- The plan proposes a repeated-measures ANOVA on ~100-200 participants rating 60 stimuli each. This design creates a massive number of observations (6,000-12,000) but only 20-60 independent experimental units (scenarios). ANOVA assumes independence of observations; here, responses are nested within scenarios. Ignoring 'Scenario' as a random effect will inflate Type I error rates (pseudoreplication). The analysis must use a Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) with random intercepts for both Participant and Scenario, not a standard ANOVA.
- The plan relies on CLIP cosine similarity (≥0.95) to validate that salience manipulation preserves semantic content. CLIP is a general-purpose vision-language model trained on internet data, not a psychometric tool for detecting subtle semantic shifts in moral ambiguity. A high CLIP score does not guarantee that the *moral* narrative or the *blameworthiness* of the actor remains constant. Relying solely on CLIP risks a construct validity failure where the 'manipulation' inadvertently changes the moral scenario, confounding the results.
- The power analysis cites a target of 100-200 participants for a medium effect size. However, with 60 stimuli per participant, the effective sample size for the fixed effect of 'Salience' is constrained by the number of *scenarios* (20), not participants. If the effect varies by scenario (which is likely in moral psychology), the study is severely underpowered to detect a generalizable effect. The plan must explicitly address the 'Scenario' as a random effect in the power calculation or reduce the number of stimuli to increase the number of unique scenarios.
- The plan cites Visual Genome as the primary dataset for 'morally ambiguous' scenarios. Visual Genome provides region-level object/attribute annotations but does not contain pre-labeled 'moral ambiguity' or 'blame' ground truth. Relying on it as the source for the experimental stimuli without a cited, verified external dataset of pre-validated moral scenarios (or a clear plan for the 'human coding' step to generate this new dataset from scratch) creates a data gap. The plan assumes the existence of 'social/conflict' tags that map directly to moral ambiguity, which is not a standard field in Visual Genome. This risks the dataset being inappropriate for the specific research question without a verified intermediate dataset or a cited source for the human-coded labels.
- The plan states 'No verified raw URL provided in the # Verified datasets block' for Visual Genome and relies on a 'canonical subset downloaded via the datasets library (if available)'. Visual Genome does not have a standard HuggingFace `datasets` loader for the full image+annotation set in the same way as smaller datasets; it typically requires manual download of large JSONs and image archives. The plan's assumption that a 'canonical subset' is available via a standard loader without a specific, verified URL or accession ID creates a feasibility risk. The dataset citation is present in name, but the access method described is ambiguous and may not be reproducible as written, violating the 'Verified Accuracy' principle if the loader fails or the subset is undefined.
- Constitution Principle II (Verified Accuracy) is marked PASS, but the plan and research.md cite 'https://visualgenome.org/' and 'https://huggingface.co/openai/clip-vit-base-patch32' without verified canonical URLs or accession IDs in a '# Verified datasets' block. The Constitution requires citations to be verified against primary sources before contributing review points. The plan claims compliance without evidence of this verification step in the design.
- FR-008 (Two-stage ambiguity identification) is mapped to '01_data_ingestion.py' + '03_semantic_validation.py'. However, the data-model.md defines 'Scenario' with 'ambiguity_score' and 'is_ambiguous' derived from 'human coding' (research.md), but the plan's script list lacks a dedicated script for the 'human coding' step or the calculation of Cohen's κ. The plan implies this is done in '01_data_ingestion.py' (metadata filtering) or '03_semantic_validation.py' (CLIP), neither of which can perform human coding or inter-rater reliability checks. This creates a gap where a critical functional requirement has no corresponding plan element to exercise it.
- The 'Project Structure' section lists 'docs/research.md' but the 'Documentation' section lists 'research.md' in the specs folder. The 'Source Code' section lists 'code/03_semantic_validation.py' but the 'Computational Task Ordering' lists '03_semantic_validation.py' as 'CLIP-based semantic similarity check'. The contracts include 'stimuli_manifest.schema.yaml' which uses 'ssim_score' (Structural Similarity Index), but the plan and data-model consistently refer to 'semantic_similarity' via CLIP cosine similarity. This naming inconsistency between the contract (SSIM) and the plan (CLIP) suggests a mismatch in the validation metric definition.
- Constitution Principle V (Versioning Discipline) is marked PASS, but the plan does not explicitly describe the mechanism for recording content hashes in 'state/projects/...yaml' or how 'updated_at' timestamps are triggered by artifact changes. The plan mentions 'Content hashes recorded' in the table but does not map a specific plan step or script to the *act* of updating the state file, which is a requirement of the Constitution.
- Constitution Check Principle VII claims 'no circularity between predictor (stimulus) and response,' but the plan's validation logic for the predictor (FR-001, SC-001) relies on CLIP cosine similarity. If the 'semantic preservation' check (CLIP score) is used to filter stimuli, and CLIP embeddings are known to correlate with human attention/salience, there is a risk of confounding the 'salience' manipulation with 'semantic distinctiveness' in the validation step. More critically, the plan treats 'semantic similarity' as a binary gate (≥0.95) but does not explicitly validate that the *manipulated* salience (contrast/brightness) is the *only* variable changing. If the manipulation inadvertently changes other visual features (texture, edge density) that CLIP also encodes, the 'independence' of the predictor is compromised. The plan must explicitly state how it isolates luminance/contrast from other visual features in the validation metric, not just rely on a global CLIP score which may be sensitive to the very changes being made.
- The plan assumes 'Predictors (salience levels) are experimentally manipulated and orthogonal; no definitional collinearity.' However, in a within-subject design with only 3 levels (Low, Medium, High) of a continuous manipulation (contrast/brightness), the 'orthogonality' is structural, not statistical in the sense of independent variables. The real risk is that the 'salience' manipulation (contrast enhancement) might not be linear or monotonic in its effect on human perception, or that the 'medium' and 'high' levels are perceptually indistinguishable from 'low' for certain image types. The plan lacks a 'manipulation check' analysis step *before* the main ANOVA to verify that the intended salience levels actually resulted in distinct perceptual salience (e.g., via a pilot eye-tracking study or a separate salience-rating task). Without this, a null result in the ANOVA could be due to failed manipulation rather than a lack of effect on blame, rendering the analysis inconclusive.
- The plan relies on 'human coding' (FR-008) to identify 'morally ambiguous' scenarios with Cohen's κ ≥ 0.6. However, the plan does not specify *what* the human coders are rating. If they are rating 'ambiguity' directly, this introduces a subjective variable that is not controlled. If they are rating 'moral relevance' or 'conflict,' the definition of 'ambiguity' is not operationalized. The plan must define the specific rating scale and instructions for the human coders to ensure that the 'ambiguity' is a consistent, measurable construct across the dataset, not just a binary 'yes/no' based on vague intuition. This is critical for the internal validity of the 'Scenario' entity.
- The plan proposes a 'Repeated-Measures ANOVA' with 'Bonferroni correction' for post-hoc tests. While standard, this approach assumes sphericity (which is checked) and normality of residuals. With a Likert scale (1-7) as the dependent variable, the assumption of interval-level data and normality is often violated in psychological research. The plan does not mention robust alternatives (e.g., Linear Mixed Models with ordinal link functions, or non-parametric repeated measures tests like the Friedman test) if assumptions are violated. Relying solely on ANOVA on ordinal data without checking robustness or providing an alternative plan is a methodological weakness that could lead to Type I or Type II errors.
