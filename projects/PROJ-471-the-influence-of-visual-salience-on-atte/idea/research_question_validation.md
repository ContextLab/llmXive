## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a psychological mechanism—whether visual salience modulates attentional allocation during moral judgment processes. This is independent of any specific experimental paradigm or measurement method, focusing on the underlying cognitive phenomenon rather than implementation details.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: concern

Visual salience and attentional bias are conceptually related constructs—attentional bias is often measured as preferential processing of salient stimuli. If both predictor (visual salience) and outcome (attentional bias) derive from the same visual fixation data, the relationship risks being partially circular. Independent measurement of the predictor (e.g., computational salience maps from stimulus images) versus the outcome (e.g., eye-tracking during moral judgment) would be required to establish independence.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Either outcome would be informative: a positive result would demonstrate that low-level perceptual features systematically bias moral reasoning (supporting embodied cognition accounts), while a null result would suggest moral judgments are insulated from visual attentional capture (supporting more abstract, rule-based accounts). Both advance theoretical understanding in moral psychology.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (visual salience → attentional bias in moral judgments) rather than implementation constraints. It does not specify a particular method (e.g., "can eye-tracking detect...") or resource budget, keeping the focus on the psychological mechanism.
