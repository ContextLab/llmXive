## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a biological relationship between gut microbiome diversity and cognitive flexibility in aging, independent of any specific ML method, architecture, or computational constraint. It targets a substantive scientific phenomenon (microbiota-gut-brain axis) rather than evaluating whether a particular technique can perform a task.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (alpha/beta diversity metrics from 16S rRNA stool sequencing) and the predicted variable (cognitive flexibility scores from behavioral assessments like NIH Toolbox or CANTAB) are derived from completely independent measurement modalities. There is no construction that would mechanically guarantee their relationship.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive correlation would provide the first quantified effect size linking microbiome diversity specifically to cognitive flexibility (not general cognition) in aging cohorts, informing biomarker development. A null result would challenge assumptions about the microbiota-gut-brain axis in this specific domain, suggesting other factors dominate. Both outcomes are scientifically informative.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a clear domain relationship (microbiome diversity → cognitive flexibility in adults 65+) rather than implementation constraints (e.g., "can method M handle X within budget B"). It is a genuine biological question about how two phenomena relate in a specific population.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question targets a substantive biological relationship between independent measurement modalities, with both positive and null outcomes being informative. The question is appropriately framed as a domain inquiry rather than an implementation benchmark, and there is no circular construction that would mechanically guarantee the answer.
