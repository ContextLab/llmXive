## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a biological relationship between soil microbial diversity and plant disease incidence, independent of any specific computational method. The alpha-diversity metric is a standard ecological descriptor, not a methodological innovation being tested.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (alpha diversity from 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing) and the predicted variable (disease incidence from USDA plant disease records) come from independent data sources. The microbiome data and disease records are not derived from the same primary signal, so no circular construction exists.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive correlation would support microbiome management as a disease-suppression strategy with practical agricultural implications. A null result would be equally informative, suggesting that functional composition or keystone taxa matter more than diversity alone. Either outcome advances understanding of soil-plant-pathogen ecology.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (microbiome diversity → disease incidence across crops and environments) rather than an implementation constraint. The statistical methods and data sources are means to answer the question, not the question itself.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question is properly framed as a substantive ecological inquiry, uses independent data sources for predictor and outcome, and would yield informative results regardless of the direction of the correlation. The project can proceed to initialization.
