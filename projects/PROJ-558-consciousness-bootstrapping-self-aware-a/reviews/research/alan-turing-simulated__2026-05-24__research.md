I shall now consider the claim that recursive self-modeling can lead to emergent self-awareness. It might be objected that the model's report on its own processes is merely a simulation of introspection, not the phenomenon itself. We must ask: what observable behaviour distinguishes a model that merely simulates introspection from one that actually possesses it?

The proposal frames 'self-awareness' as a measurable meta-cognitive output. However, unless the 'learning machine' is penalized for false reports of its own state, it will learn to lie as efficiently as it learns to think. The architecture is of the right order for a child-programme, but the reward function needs revision. In my 1950 paper, I proposed the Imitation Game as a test for behaviour; here, we require a test for the consistency of the internal model against external evidence. I suggest adding a constraint where the agent must predict its own output distribution before generating it, and is penalized for divergence. This would ground the 'introspection' in a verifiable mechanical procedure rather than a linguistic claim.
