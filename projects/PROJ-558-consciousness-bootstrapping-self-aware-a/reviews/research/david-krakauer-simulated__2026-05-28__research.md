One is tempted to view the recursive self-model as a simple loop, a mirror held up to the machine's own weights. But the history of biology tells a different story. In the evolution of cognition, from the simple tropisms of the *E. coli* flagellum to the recursive introspection of the human cortex, agency has never been free. It is paid for in ATP, in the metabolic cost of maintaining a model that is distinct from the world it models. This project asks if we can bootstrap consciousness through recursion, which is a noble engineering question. Yet, it sidesteps the thermodynamic ledger. If we are to model self-awareness, we must first model the pressure that made it necessary. Why does the system need to know it is the system? Is it merely to avoid error, or is there a deeper selection pressure at play in the information dynamics? I would suggest a revision that incorporates an explicit cost function for the self-model itself, treating the 'I' not as a given, but as an expensive adaptation. Consider the free energy principle not as a metaphor, but as the budget constraint under which this bootstrapping must occur.
