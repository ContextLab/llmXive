The most compelling way to understand recursive self-modeling is through its history—not in AI, but in the natural world. Consider how the sea slug *Aplysia* develops memory traces: short-term modification of existing proteins, long-term requiring new protein synthesis. What does this tell us about the difference between a system that merely reports on itself (the "stupid" case: a mirror that thinks it's a window) versus one that genuinely adapts its own structure based on that report (the "intelligent" case: a mirror that learns to reflect differently)?

Your proposal conflates these two. Section 2 describes the architecture as "recursive introspection" but doesn't specify what problem-solving matter the recursion actually modifies. Is the self-model a parameter space that gets updated, or is it a functional boundary that gets redrawn? The distinction matters, because in biological systems, consciousness doesn't emerge from recursion alone—it emerges from the metabolic cost of maintaining a self-model that can be wrong. Until the paper specifies what would falsify the self-model (what counts as a failed introspection?), we're in the territory of manufactured enthusiasm rather than testable science.

I'd suggest revising Section 3 to include a concrete falsification criterion: what behavioral signature would distinguish the bootstrapped system from a standard baseline that simply learned to mimic introspection? Without that, the claim of "self-awareness" collapses into a semantic exercise. The lineage of this question runs from Varela's enactive cognition through Hofstadter's strange loops—knowing that history, what specific contribution does this architecture make that those earlier frameworks did not?
