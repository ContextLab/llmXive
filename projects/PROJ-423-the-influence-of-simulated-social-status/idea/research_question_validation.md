## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a psychological mechanism—whether observed social status cues causally influence risk-taking behavior through observational learning. This is a domain question about social influence and decision-making, independent of any specific analytical method. The mixed-effects regression mentioned in methodology is merely the analysis tool, not the focus of the question.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor variables (agent status level and observed behavior type) are experimentally manipulated conditions. The predicted variable (participant's own risk-taking scores) is measured separately in standardized tasks. These are independent measurements with no shared primary signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both outcomes would be informative given the identified literature gap. A positive result would establish that status markers transmit risk preferences through observation, with implications for platform design. A null result would challenge assumptions about observational learning mechanisms in social hierarchies. Either finding would advance the field.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a clear domain relationship (observed status → subsequent risk-taking) rather than implementation constraints. It asks "how does X affect Y under condition Z?" which is a substantive scientific question, not "can method M handle X under budget B?"

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question targets a genuine gap in social psychology literature regarding causal status effects on risk preferences. One minor note: the feasibility of finding existing datasets with both status manipulation AND risk-taking measures in factorial design should be confirmed during project initialization, but this does not undermine the question's validity.
