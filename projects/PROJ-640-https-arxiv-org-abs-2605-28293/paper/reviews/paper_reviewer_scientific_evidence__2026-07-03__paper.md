The paper presents a compelling hypothesis regarding the "length shortcut" in policy gradient methods for proactive recommendation, supported by a theoretical sketch (Theorem 1) and empirical observations. The proposed rectification mechanisms (Stepwise Reward Centering and Position-Specific Advantage Estimation) are logically derived from the identified deficiencies. However, the strength of the scientific evidence is currently limited by a lack of rigorous statistical reporting and incomplete ablation data.

First, the claim of statistical significance is pervasive but unsupported by methodological detail. Tables 1, 2, and the Appendix tables mark improvements with asterisks indicating $p < 0.05$. However, the manuscript does not state the number of independent training runs (random seeds) performed, nor does it specify the statistical test employed (e.g., paired t-test, Wilcoxon signed-rank). In reinforcement learning, where variance can be high, results from a single run or a small number of seeds are insufficient to claim statistical significance. The authors must report the mean and standard deviation of metrics across at least 3-5 independent seeds and explicitly describe the statistical test used to validate the p-values.

Second, the central empirical claim that standard policy gradients suffer from a "length shortcut" leading to path collapse needs stronger quantitative backing. While Figure 2 qualitatively depicts this, the text does not provide the standard deviation of path lengths for the baseline methods (e.g., REINFORCE, GRPO) across seeds. Demonstrating that the baseline path lengths are not only longer on average but also exhibit high variance or a specific distribution shift compared to ProRL would solidify the evidence that the "shortcut" is a robust phenomenon and not an artifact of specific hyperparameter tuning.

Finally, the ablation study regarding Stepwise Reward Centering (SRC) in Table 3 shows that removing SRC leads to high CTR but poor guidance (IoI/IoR). The authors attribute this to the model exploiting the length shortcut. To fully validate this mechanism, the paper should report the average path lengths generated by the "w/o SRC" model. If the hypothesis is correct, this model should generate significantly longer paths (approaching $L_{max}$) compared to the full ProRL model. Without this specific data point, the causal link between the removed component and the observed performance drop remains partially inferential.

Addressing these points—specifically by adding seed counts, variance metrics, and explicit path length statistics for ablations—would significantly strengthen the scientific evidence supporting the paper's claims.
