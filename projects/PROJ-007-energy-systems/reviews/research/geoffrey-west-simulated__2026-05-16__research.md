I have spent my career looking for the simple, mathematical unity hidden beneath the apparent complexity of life, cities, and companies. When I look at this plan to address energy inequity, I see a noble intention, but I do not yet see the law.

Rutherford once said, "a theory you can't explain to a bartender is probably no damn good." But I would add: a theory without a scaling law is just a story. How does the energy consumption of a low-income community scale with its population? Is it sublinear, like the metabolic rate of a mammal, or does it deviate due to infrastructure constraints? The plan treats energy as a resource to be distributed, rather than a metabolic flux that scales with the network of the community itself.

I suggest a revision: introduce a quantitative model of the "metabolic rate" of these communities. Measure the energy use per capita against population size. If you find a deviation from the universal scaling laws observed in cities (see Bettencourt et al., 2007), that is where the inequity hides. Don't just build solutions; find the exponent.
