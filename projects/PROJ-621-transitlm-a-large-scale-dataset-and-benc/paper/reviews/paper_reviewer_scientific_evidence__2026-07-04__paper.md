The paper presents a compelling dataset and benchmark, but the experimental design contains significant confounds that prevent the evidence from fully supporting the central claims of "map-free" learning and "implicit spatial grounding."

First, the comparison in Table 1 between the trained TransitLM model and general-purpose LLMs (GPT-5.4, etc.) is fundamentally unfair. The general LLMs are evaluated in a zero-shot or few-shot setting without any fine-tuning on the transit domain, while the proposed method is trained on 13 million specific route records. The massive performance gap (e.g., 75.5% vs 40.2% Route Exact Match) likely reflects the benefit of domain-specific fine-tuning rather than the specific "map-free" architecture or the dataset's unique properties. To establish the claim that the *method* is superior, the authors must compare against a general LLM that has been similarly fine-tuned on the same data, or at least demonstrate that the performance gain persists when controlling for the amount of domain-specific training.

Second, the "GPS-only ablation" (Table 2) is confounded by the training setup. The SFT-only model is trained with textual station names, so removing them at test time constitutes a severe distribution shift. The CPT model, conversely, appears to be trained with GPS coordinates as part of the input (or at least exposed to them in a way that allows GPS-only inference). The observed robustness of the CPT model may simply be a result of it being trained on the specific input modality used in the test, rather than evidence of "implicit spatial grounding" that transcends the training data. A valid ablation would require training a model *only* on GPS inputs (without CPT) to see if it can learn the mapping, or ensuring the SFT-only model is also trained with GPS inputs to isolate the effect of the CPT stage.

Third, the claim of "map-free" generation is potentially circular. The dataset is derived from Amap logs, which are generated by a map-based engine. The model learns to reproduce the station sequences and routes present in these logs. While the model does not use an external map API at inference, it has effectively memorized the map structure from the training data. The paper does not provide evidence that the model can generalize to *unseen* geographic areas or handle stations not present in the training logs. A negative control testing the model on a city not included in the training set (e.g., Guangzhou) would be crucial to determine if the model truly understands spatial relationships or merely memorizes the training distribution.

Finally, the train/test split strategy is not explicitly detailed. Given that the dataset is extracted from a "single day" of logs, there is a risk of session ID overlap between the 13M training records and the 10k test set. If the test set contains routes that were part of the training data, the high accuracy scores (e.g., 73.7% REM) could be due to memorization rather than generalization. The authors must clarify the split strategy and ensure no overlap to validate the benchmark results.
