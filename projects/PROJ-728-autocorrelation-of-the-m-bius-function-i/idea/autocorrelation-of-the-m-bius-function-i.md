# Autocorrelation of the Möbius Function in Short Intervals  

**Field**: mathematics  

## Research question

How does the empirical autocorrelation of the Möbius function μ(n) behave within short intervals of length L (10³ ≤ L ≤ 10⁵), and does it agree with the zero‑correlation prediction implied by Chowla’s conjecture?

## Motivation

Understanding the sign pattern of μ(n) is central to many problems in analytic number theory, including Chowla’s conjecture and Sarnak’s Möbius disjointness conjecture. While long‑range averages are relatively well studied, quantitative evidence on short‑range dependence is sparse. Empirical autocorrelation in modest windows can reveal whether μ(n) truly mimics a random sign sequence at these scales, informing both conjectural heuristics and the design of downstream tests of Möbius randomness.

## Related work

- [Autocorrelation of the Möbius Function (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.01071) — Provides asymptotic formulas for the autocorrelation sum \(\sum_{n\le x}\mu(n)\mu(n+h)\) and discusses the expected decay, offering a theoretical benchmark for our empirical study.  
- [On the Möbius function in all short intervals (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.09076) — Shows that short‑interval sums \(\sum_{x<n\le x+x^{\theta}}\mu(n)\) are \(o(x^{\theta})\) for \(\theta>0.55\); our work complements this by examining pairwise products rather than single‑term sums.  
- [The autocorrelation of the Möbius function and Chowla's conjecture for the rational function field in characteristic 2 (2014)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1409.3694) — Demonstrates a function‑field analogue of Chowla’s conjecture, supplying methodological ideas (e.g., use of character sums) that can inspire our finite‑field‑free empirical approach.

## Expected results

We anticipate that the normalized autocorrelation \(\frac{1}{L-h}\sum_{k=1}^{L-h}\mu(k)\mu(k+h)\) will be statistically indistinguishable from zero for all lags \(h\le L/2\), consistent with Chowla’s conjecture. A significant deviation (e.g., systematic positive or negative bias) would falsify the naïve random‑sign model at the examined scales. Evidence will be quantified by permutation‑test p‑values and by confidence intervals derived from the empirical distribution of shuffled sign sequences.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data generation**: Implement a linear‑time sieve (e.g., modified Eratosthenes) to compute μ(n) for \(1\le n\le N=10^{7}\); store as a binary array (≈10 MB).  
- **Window selection**: Choose interval lengths \(L\in\{10^{3},10^{4},10^{5}\}\). For each L, slide a window across the full sequence with step size L/2 to obtain overlapping short intervals.  
- **Autocorrelation computation**: For each window and each lag \(h\in\{1,\dots,\lfloor L/2\rfloor\}\), compute the sum \(\sum_{k=1}^{L-h}\mu(k)\mu(k+h)\) and normalize by \(L-h\).  
- **Permutation testing**: Generate 1 000 random sign permutations of the μ‑values within each window (preserving the proportion of zeros); recompute autocorrelations to build a null distribution for each lag.  
- **Statistical assessment**: Calculate two‑sided p‑values and 95 % confidence intervals; flag lags where the empirical value lies outside the null envelope.  
- **Visualization**: Produce heat‑maps of autocorrelation versus lag for each L, and overlay theoretical zero lines and permutation‑based confidence bands.  
- **Comparison to theory**: Contrast empirical means with the asymptotic predictions from the 2022 paper and with the short‑interval bound of the 2019 work.  
- **Reproducibility**: All code will be written in Python 3, using only the standard library and `numpy`; data and scripts will be archived on Zenodo with a permanent DOI.
