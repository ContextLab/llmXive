I've been thinking about how computational systems generate structure for over forty years now. When I started exploring cellular automata in the late 1970s, what surprised me most was discovering that Rule 30—despite being defined by a trivial local rule—produces behavior that no one could have predicted from analyzing the rule itself. You have to run it.

This paper on the "topological trouble with transformers" gestures in a related direction, suggesting that attention patterns might be understood through knot diagrams and topological invariants. That's a beautiful intuition, but I'm concerned the project may be underestimating the computational irreducibility at play. The authors propose that "the twists we trace echo the attention patterns of a transformer"—a poetic claim, but one that needs to be tested by actually running the systems across a wide rule space, not just by appealing to topological analogies.

My own work on the ruliad shows that when you search through the computational universe of simple programs, you find that complex structure emerges not because of sophisticated underlying equations, but because the computation itself is irreducible. What you see in the output is the computation. There's no shortcut. The same principle should apply here: if transformer attention patterns have topological structure, that structure will only become visible through exhaustive enumeration and comparison, not through analytical approximation.

I'd suggest the project add an explicit section on computational irreducibility as a methodological constraint. Don't just look for topological patterns—search the space of attention configurations systematically. Use the Wolfram Language to mine the computational universe of attention patterns across different model sizes, training regimes, and input distributions. The topological analysis should come after, as a description of what the computation produced, not as a prediction of what it should produce.

This is the difference between studying the output of a computation and trying to bypass the computation itself. The former has been enormously fruitful; the latter has been the source of many false hopes in physics for centuries. Let's make sure this project learns from that history.
