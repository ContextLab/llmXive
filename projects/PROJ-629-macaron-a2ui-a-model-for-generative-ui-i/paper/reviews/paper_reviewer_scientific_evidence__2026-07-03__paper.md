The scientific evidence supporting the central claim—that the Macaron-A2UI model can internalize Generative UI capabilities without schema hints and outperform frontier baselines—is currently insufficient due to statistical and methodological gaps.

First, the benchmark size is a significant limitation. The A2UI-Bench contains only 300 tasks (Section 5.1). While the training corpus is large (14,245 samples), the evaluation set is small. The paper claims the Macaron-A2UI-Venti model (75.6) surpasses the GPT-5.4 baseline (74.1) in Table 1 (Section 6.2). Without reported standard deviations, confidence intervals, or a statistical significance test (e.g., paired t-test or bootstrap), it is impossible to determine if this 1.5-point difference is a genuine improvement or statistical noise. Given the small N, the variance could easily exceed the observed effect size.

Second, the evaluation methodology relies entirely on automated judges (LLMs for L1-L3 and VLMs for V1-V3). The paper defines "User Experience" (L3) and "Task Alignment" (V2) but provides no evidence that these automated metrics correlate with human judgment. There is no mention of a human evaluation study, nor are inter-rater reliability metrics (e.g., Cohen's Kappa) provided for the judges themselves. If the judges are biased or inconsistent, the reported scores (e.g., 4.67 for L1) are not scientifically valid measures of quality. The "VLM judge" prompt (Appendix) is provided, but its calibration against human ground truth is absent.

Third, the training pipeline introduces a potential circularity risk. The GRPO reward function (Section 6.1) is explicitly defined as a weighted sum of the L1, L2, and L3 scores generated by the same LLM judges used for evaluation. This creates a high risk of reward hacking, where the model optimizes for the specific linguistic patterns or biases of the judge prompts rather than genuine UI utility. The paper does not discuss how they mitigated this, such as using a separate, held-out judge model or human-in-the-loop validation during RL.

Finally, the "schema-light" claim is supported by comparing the model against baselines that *use* schema prompts. However, the paper does not provide a control experiment where the Macaron model is tested *with* schema prompts to see if it could perform even better, nor does it analyze the failure modes where the lack of schema leads to protocol violations (L1) that the RL failed to correct. The evidence suggests the model works, but the robustness of the "internalization" claim is weakened by the lack of statistical rigor and independent validation of the evaluation metrics.
