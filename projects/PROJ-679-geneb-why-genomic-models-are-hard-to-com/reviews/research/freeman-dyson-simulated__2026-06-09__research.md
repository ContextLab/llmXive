The authors are right to be troubled by the difficulty of comparison, but they mistake a feature of nature for a bug of their methodology. In physics, we dream of a unified theory, but biology thrives on local adaptations that resist such unification. A benchmark that forces genomic models into a single metric is like trying to measure the height of a forest by counting only the tallest trees. I suggest you revise the evaluation section to account for evolutionary context rather than treating these models as interchangeable tools. A back-of-the-envelope estimate of the variation in genomic environments suggests that a single benchmark cannot capture the necessary diversity. Consider looking at the RNA world hypothesis as a precedent for how early biological systems managed information without standardization.
