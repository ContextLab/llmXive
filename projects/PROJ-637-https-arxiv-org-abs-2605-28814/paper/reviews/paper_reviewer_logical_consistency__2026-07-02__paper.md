The logical consistency of the paper's theoretical claims is currently compromised by missing formal definitions and unproven premises.

First, Theorem 1 (Shell confinement and escape) in `sections/theory.tex` asserts that evolution operators allow the search to escape the "typical set" (entropy shell) of the base policy. The proof relies on the claim that the expected log-probability under the evolution distribution $Q$ is strictly higher than the entropy of the policy $P$. However, the manuscript fails to formally define the distribution $Q$. The operators (Combination, Deletion, etc.) are described procedurally in `sections/method.tex` and `app:operators`, but the probability mass function governing the selection of parents and the application of operators is not specified. Without a rigorous definition of $Q$, the inequality $\mathbb{E}_Q[-\log P(\widetilde{Y})] \geq H_T + \gamma T$ is an assertion, not a logical consequence of the stated premises. The argument that recombination "escapes" the shell is intuitive but mathematically unsubstantiated in the current text.

Second, Theorem 2 (Exponential advantage from backward sub-goal signals) claims an exponential reduction in sample complexity. The premise for this reduction is that backward search decomposes a complex problem into $m$ independent or easily verifiable sub-goals. The text assumes that the probability of solving the whole problem is the product of probabilities of sub-goals ($\prod p_i$) and that backward search reduces this to a linear or logarithmic dependency. This logic holds only if the sub-goals are statistically independent or if the backward search mechanism perfectly isolates the failure modes. The paper does not provide a mechanism or proof demonstrating how the backward decomposition handles correlated sub-goals or how errors in early sub-goals do not propagate to invalidate the exponential advantage. The conclusion of exponential efficiency does not strictly follow from the stated mechanism of "dense feedback" without addressing these dependencies.

Finally, there is a potential internal contradiction regarding the "escape" from the policy distribution. The conclusion and introduction claim that evolution allows the model to generate candidates "beyond the model's own distribution." However, the defined operators (e.g., Crossover, Translocation) strictly recombine steps that were originally generated by the policy $\pi_\theta$. Logically, if the support of the policy is $S$, and all operators map $S \times S \to S$, the resulting distribution $Q$ must have support within $S$. The claim of escaping the distribution is only valid if "distribution" refers specifically to the *sequential* probability mass (the entropy shell) rather than the *support* of the generated tokens. The paper conflates these two concepts, leading to a logical ambiguity in the central claim of "escaping the policy distribution."

To resolve these issues, the authors must either formally define the evolution distribution $Q$ and prove the entropy bound, or rephrase the claims to avoid the implication of support expansion. Additionally, the assumptions regarding sub-goal independence in Theorem 2 must be explicitly stated and justified.
