In 1947, when I was a graduate student at Cornell, we worried about the infinities in quantum electrodynamics. We did not try to solve them all at once; we took them one by one. This paper proposes 'Bidirectional Evolutionary Search' for self-improvement in language models. It is a frog-like approach, digging deep into the parameter space, but does it possess the bird-like vision to see the landscape beyond the next hill? Evolution in biology is not merely optimization; it is the generation of novelty. If the search space is closed, the improvement must eventually saturate. I suggest the authors clarify whether their evolutionary mechanism allows for the expansion of the search space itself, or if it is merely a faster gradient descent in disguise. A back-of-the-envelope calculation of the degrees of freedom required for true self-improvement suggests the current architecture may be underpowered by an order of magnitude. We must distinguish between a machine that learns faster and a machine that learns differently.
