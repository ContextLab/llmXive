\texttt{How many bananas have stickers on them?}

\vspace{1pt}\noindent\textbf{(a) BCQ reformulated prompt.} The teacher's correct trace and one of the student's wrong traces are teacher-compressed under a shared prompt and token cap, randomly shuffled, and inserted into a binary-discrimination template. The student then rolls out $G_{\rm S}\!=\!8$ times on the new prompt; one correct rollout is shown below.

Each of the three bananas in the bunch displays a distinct sticker, likely indicating weight or size. Close examination confirms the presence of these labels on the top, middle, and bottom bananas. ---\textbf{omitted}--- $\backslash$boxed\{3\}}

\begin{prompttemplatefig}
\textbf{Candidate~B} (anonymized; \emph{here, the teacher-derived correct trace -- not labelled as such to the student}):

\smallskip
\texttt{Visual inspection of the banana bunch identifies the front-facing bananas. A sticker is clearly visible on the top banana in the bunch. A second sticker is clearly visible on the bottom banana in the bunch. The middle banana and the side banana do not display any visible stickers. ---\textbf{omitted}--- $\backslash$boxed\{2\}}
\end{prompttemplatefig}

\begin{prompttemplatefig}
\textbf{Student's BCQ rollout} (one of $G_{\rm S}\!=\!8$; this one is judged correct):

\smallskip
\texttt{<think> The two candidates are anonymized, so I cannot tell which is teacher-correct from the label. Candidate~A asserts a uniform ``each of the three'' without per-position evidence; Candidate~B distinguishes the top and the bottom from the middle and the side. The per-position decomposition is concrete and falsifiable in a way a blanket ``three'' is not, so I commit to Candidate~B on reasoning quality alone: top and bottom stickered, middle and side bare. ---\textbf{omitted}--- Answer: 2. </think>}

\smallskip
\texttt{$\backslash$boxed\{2\}} \quad \textcolor{posgain}{\checkmark}

\vspace{1pt}\noindent\textbf{(b) NCQ reformulated prompt.} \emph{All} of the student's wrong rollouts on this question are collected, teacher-compressed under the same shared prompt and token cap, and concatenated with their parsed final answers. The teacher's correct trace is \emph{not} inserted. The student then rolls out $G_{\rm S}\!=\!8$ times on the new prompt; one correct rollout is shown below.

\textbf{Instruction to the student:} the answer is \emph{not} one of the listed values.

\begin{prompttemplatefig}
\textbf{Student's NCQ rollout} (one of $G_{\rm S}\!=\!8$; this one is judged correct):

\smallskip
\texttt{<think> All eight listed candidates report 3 with the same generic ``each banana has a sticker'' justification; none localises which banana is stickered. The prompt rules 3 out, which means the consensus over-counts a specific position rather than the count being arbitrarily off. The position most consistent with being silently absorbed by such a generic sweep is the middle one, so correcting this specific failure mode -- not eliminating from a closed answer set -- yields 2. ---\textbf{omitted}--- Answer: 2. </think>}

\texttt{$\backslash$boxed\{2\}} \quad \textcolor{posgain}{\checkmark}
