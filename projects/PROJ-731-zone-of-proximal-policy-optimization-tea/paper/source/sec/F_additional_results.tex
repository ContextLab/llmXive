\subsection{Buffer dynamics across student scales}
\label{sec:appendix:zone}

This subsection complements Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments:zone} with two views of how the prompt replay buffer behaves \emph{over training} at every student scale, all measured on ZPPO runs with the hyperparameters listed in Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:hyperparameters} (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:hyperparameters}).

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{appendix/figure_init_zppo_bin_evolve.pdf}
    \caption{Replay-buffer composition by entry rollout accuracy at admission, over training, for ZPPO at every student scale. Each panel stacks the four admission bins so the total band height is the buffer occupancy.}
    \label{fig:appendix:buffer_compose}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{(1)~The buffer saturates earlier at small scales.} In Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:buffer_compose} the buffer hits its capacity ceiling within $\sim$$70$ steps at $0.8$B and within $\sim$$100$ steps at $2$B; at $4$B and $9$B it only approaches the cap toward step $200$. The reason is simple. Weak students fail more questions, so admission outruns graduation and the buffer fills almost immediately, whereas stronger students graduate at close to the admission rate and fill it more slowly. Either way, every scale ends training near capacity, which is the trend Fig.~\ref{fig:system}b already hinted at in the main paper.

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{appendix/figure_app_zppo_grad_ratio.pdf}
    \caption{\emph{(left)} Total number of graduations per scale, broken down by entry rollout accuracy at admission ($0$\%, $12.5$\%, $25$\%, $37.5$\%). \emph{(right)} Per-scale composition of graduates. Higher bars on the leftmost colors at larger scales mean ``ZPPO graduates a larger absolute number of the hardest entries (admitted at $0$\%) at larger scales'', not that hardest entries become a smaller share of the buffer.}
    \label{fig:appendix:grad_ratio}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{(2)~Even the hardest admission bin graduates, with the absolute count growing through $4$B and the relative share rising monotonically up to $4$B before saturating.} Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:grad_ratio} (left) shows that the count of graduations from the $0$\% admission bin (the bin where ordinary RL has zero gradient signal because every rollout fails) follows $293$\,$\to$\,$432$\,$\to$\,$562$\,$\to$\,$514$ across $0.8$/$2$/$4$/$9$B, peaking at $4$B and dipping slightly at $9$B as the saturated student needs fewer hard-question graduations to begin with. The $0$\% share of the per-scale graduate composition (right panel) tracks the same shape, climbing $13$\% $\to$ $16$\% $\to$ $21$\% and then holding flat at $21$\% from $4$B to $9$B. In short, BCQ and NCQ supply usable learning signal on exactly the questions where ordinary RL stalls, which is the mechanism the main paper invokes for Fig.~\ref{fig:zone}.

\subsection{ZPPO vs.\ GRPO$^{\dagger}$ training dynamics at $2$B}
\label{sec:appendix:dynamics_2b}

This subsection isolates the source of ZPPO's advantage over GRPO$^{\dagger}$ at $2$B by tracking the optimization-side and buffer-side dynamics of the two runs side-by-side.

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{appendix/figure_app_2b_comp.pdf}
    \caption{Training dynamics of Qwen3.5-2B + ZPPO vs.\ Qwen3.5-2B + GRPO$^{\dagger}$. Left: student rollout accuracy. Center: fraction of student rollouts whose group-relative advantage is positive. Right: actor Shannon entropy of the policy distribution.}
    \label{fig:appendix:2b_comp}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{(1)~ZPPO sustains a higher fraction of positive advantages.} Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:2b_comp} (center) shows the fraction of student rollouts with positive group-relative advantage staying $\sim$$3$\,pp above GRPO$^{\dagger}$ throughout training. The mechanism is direct: BCQ turns an all-wrong group (zero gradient signal) into a mixed group as soon as one BCQ rollout is correct, and NCQ exposes the diversity inside an all-wrong group once at least two distinct failure modes appear. ZPPO therefore earns positive advantages on a subset of groups that would give none under GRPO$^{\dagger}$.

\textbf{(2)~The student stays exploratory for longer.} Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:2b_comp} (right) shows actor entropy under ZPPO climbing to $1.2$ nats by step $200$ vs.\ GRPO$^{\dagger}$'s $\sim$$0.95$, and rising rather than collapsing. We attribute this to the buffer: its continual re-admission of hard questions keeps slowing the policy's concentration onto the easy distribution that GRPO$^{\dagger}$ converges toward.

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{appendix/figure_app_grad_evict.pdf}
    \caption{Replay-buffer fate of completed entries (graduated vs.\ FIFO-evicted) at $2$B for ZPPO and GRPO$^{\dagger}$. ZPPO almost doubles the graduation rate ($26.6$\% vs.\ $14.3$\%) and proportionally cuts evictions, on the same buffer with the same admission/graduation thresholds.}
    \label{fig:appendix:grad_evict}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{(3)~ZPPO almost doubles the graduation rate at matched buffer policy.} Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:grad_evict} compares the fate of every entry that has left the buffer by the end of training, at $2$B and at matched buffer policy. ZPPO graduates $2{,}627$ entries and evicts $7{,}251$ ($26.6$\% / $73.4$\%); GRPO$^{\dagger}$ graduates $1{,}440$ and evicts $8{,}647$ ($14.3$\% / $85.7$\%). The buffer \emph{policy} (admission threshold, graduation threshold, capacity, FIFO eviction rule) is identical in both runs; the admitted cohorts themselves differ per run, since each run only admits questions on which its own plain rollouts fail. The comparison is therefore ``under matched buffer policy, what fraction of the admitted cohort ultimately graduates,'' not ``on the same set of questions, who graduates more.'' Read that way, the $\sim$$1.9\!\times$ graduation rate points squarely at the reformulation channel (BCQ/NCQ) as the thing that separates the two runs.

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{appendix/figure_app_grad_num_attempt.pdf}
    \caption{\emph{(left two panels)} Replay graduations partitioned by entry rollout accuracy at admission, for GRPO$^{\dagger}$ vs.\ ZPPO at $2$B (counts and per-model composition). \emph{(right two panels)} Per-bucket fate decomposition by number of re-sample attempts: ZPPO converts the ``improved (non-grad)'' (light green) bucket into ``graduated'' (dark green) much more aggressively than GRPO$^{\dagger}$.}

\textbf{(4)~ZPPO's extra graduations concentrate in the hardest admission bins.} Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:grad_num_attempt} (left two panels) shows that under matched buffer policy, ZPPO produces $432$ vs.\ $73$ graduations from the $0$\% admission bin and $510$ vs.\ $169$ from the $12.5$\% bin (each run measured over its own admitted cohort, as in finding~(3)). The right two panels further decompose, by number of re-sample attempts, where in the buffer entries end up: under ZPPO the ``never improved'' (gray) fraction shrinks monotonically with attempts and the ``graduated'' (dark green) fraction climbs to $\sim$$56$\% by attempt $6$+, while under GRPO$^{\dagger}$ a stubborn gray ceiling persists. So BCQ and NCQ are turning visit time into graduations rather than into wasted re-rollouts.

\subsection{Multi-metric dynamics across scales}
\label{sec:appendix:dynamics_scale}

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{appendix/figure_app_dynamics_zppo.pdf}
    \caption{ZPPO training dynamics across all four student scales. \emph{(top row)} policy gradient loss, BCQ-prompt accuracy, NCQ-prompt accuracy. \emph{(bottom row)} fraction of positive advantages, student rollout accuracy, actor Shannon entropy.}
    \label{fig:appendix:dynamics_scale}
\end{figure*}

Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:dynamics_scale} aggregates six per-step metrics for the ZPPO runs across $0.8$/$2$/$4$/$9$B student scales. Three observations link these dynamics back to the main-paper claims.

\textbf{(1)~BCQ accuracy rises with training at every scale.} BCQ accuracy starts low at every scale and climbs to a scale-dependent convergence-regime value (Sec.~\ref{sec:appendix:bcq_audit}). That rise is exactly what we would expect if the student is learning to single out the correct anonymized candidate: candidate compression matches the length and format of the two options, so obvious surface tells (preferring the longer trace, say) are gone.

\textbf{(2)~NCQ accuracy rises at $2$/$4$/$9$B but is essentially flat at $0.8$B.} On NCQ, the student must reason about \emph{why} a set of its own previous attempts were all wrong; the $0.8$B model has limited capacity to discriminate among its own failure modes and therefore gains little from being shown them collectively. This is the underlying mechanism of the NCQ-at-$0.8$B exception in Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation} (Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments:ablation}, takeaway~(iii)).

\textbf{(3)~Entropy diverges with scale.} The $0.8$B and $2$B policies climb to $\sim$$1.0$/$1.2$ nats by step $200$ -- they keep expanding their candidate distribution -- while the $4$B and $9$B policies plateau at lower entropy after a brief rise. This matches the intuition that strong students concentrate quickly on a near-optimal solution, while weak students keep gaining from the extended exploration that BCQ/NCQ opens up.

\subsection{Hint and Prefix experimental setup and results}
\label{sec:appendix:hintprefix}

This subsection details the implementation of the Hint and Prefix baselines reported in Tab.~\ref{tab:hintprefix} and discusses the numerical pattern that the inline table summarizes. Hint follows the Guide-GRPO~\citep{nath2025adaptive} / SEELE~\citep{li2025staying} family: the teacher generates an answer-free hint from a correct teacher trace, the hint is appended to the prompt inside a tag, and the student samples a fully on-policy rollout from the hint-augmented prompt. Prefix follows BREAD~\citep{zhang2025bread} (with StepHint~\citep{zhang2025stephint} as another member of the same family): a correct teacher rollout is truncated to a token-fraction prefix and forced as a shared anchor for a branched group of $G_{\rm S}$ student continuations, with group-relative advantages computed over the branched group. For implementation simplicity we omit BREAD's adaptive Episode Anchor Search (EAS) and fix the prefix fraction at $\alpha\!=\!0.4$. Per-step algorithm annotations are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:baselines} (Algorithms~\ref{alg:hint} and~\ref{alg:prefix}).

\paragraph{Setup.}
Both baselines share the prompt replay buffer (admission, graduation, and capacity identical to ZPPO), the same student initialization, the same training dataset, and the same shared training pipeline as ZPPO. Per-method compute is bounded by the GRPO$^{\dagger}$--ZPPO range reported in Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:compute} (within the same training-budget convention), rather than listed as separate rows. Each baseline differs from ZPPO only in how it uses a teacher-correct response on hard questions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Hint:} a single correct teacher rollout $y_{\rm T}^{(+)}$ is converted by the teacher into an answer-free hint and appended to the prompt inside an explicit \texttt{<hint>}\,$\cdots$\,\texttt{</hint>} tag. The student then samples its rollout from this hint-augmented prompt; the rollout itself is fully student-generated and the gradient is on-policy at the response-token level (the prompt is augmented with teacher-derived hint text). (Sec.~\ref{sec:appendix:baselines}, ``Hint baseline'' for the exact prompt template.)
    \item \textbf{Prefix:} a single correct teacher rollout $y_{\rm T}^{(+)}$ is truncated to its first $\alpha\!=\!0.4$ token-fraction and forced as a starting prefix to the student's rollout; the student then samples its continuation autoregressively. The teacher-forced prefix tokens are masked from the policy-gradient summation following BREAD (E5 in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:baselines}), so only the student-continuation tokens enter the gradient as targets. The remaining distinction from ZPPO is the \emph{content} of the training signal: Prefix supplies only a positive continuation cue (continue a correct teacher trace), without a paired wrong candidate to discriminate against (as BCQ provides) or the student's own collective wrong rollouts to avoid (as NCQ provides).
\end{itemize}
Neither baseline runs BCQ or NCQ. All other hyperparameters (Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:hyperparameters}) are identical to ZPPO.

\paragraph{Result pattern.}
Tab.~\ref{tab:hintprefix} surfaces three things at $2$B that the appendix lets us interpret precisely.

\textbf{(i)~Hint helps a moderate amount on VLM but plateaus.} GRPO$^{\dagger}\!+\!$Hint gains $+1.2$~pp on VLM over GRPO$^{\dagger}$, but is still $0.4$~pp below GRPO$^{\dagger}\!+\!$BCQ and $1.6$~pp below ZPPO. The student learns from teacher-side guidance, but the signal is one-sided: it never has to compare a correct teacher candidate against its own wrong candidate, and it never sees its own wrong rollouts collected as negatives. The gain therefore saturates below BCQ and the full BCQ+NCQ recipe.

\textbf{(ii)~Hint barely transfers to LLM or Video.} GRPO$^{\dagger}\!+\!$Hint comes in at $47.6$\,/\,$61.6$ on LLM\,/\,Video, marginal versus GRPO$^{\dagger}$'s $47.3$\,/\,$61.9$, because the hint acts as a directional shortcut that the student copies without internalizing \emph{why} the direction is correct, and exercises neither the contrastive discrimination nor the collective-negative avoidance that BCQ and NCQ supply.

\textbf{(iii)~Prefix is worse on generalization.} GRPO$^{\dagger}\!+\!$Prefix \emph{loses} $-1.0$~pp on LLM and $-0.9$~pp on Video relative to GRPO$^{\dagger}$, while gaining only $+0.3$~pp on VLM. With teacher-prefix tokens masked from the policy loss following BREAD (E5), the gradient consumes only student-generated continuation tokens, so the gap is not a gradient-side issue. The issue is what the training signal \emph{contains}: Prefix supplies only a single positive continuation cue per hard question -- a correct teacher trace the student copies and extends as a continuation shortcut -- without any paired wrong candidate to discriminate against (as BCQ provides) or any aggregation of the student's own collective wrong rollouts to avoid (as NCQ provides). The gain on LLM and Video benchmarks therefore lags BCQ/NCQ, since those held-out suites stress discrimination and self-correction behaviors rather than continuation from a known-good anchor.

It all comes down to the gap between the best prompt-injection baseline (Hint, $47.6\,/\,60.4\,/\,61.6$) and full ZPPO ($50.4\,/\,62.0\,/\,63.2$): $+2.8\,/\,+1.6\,/\,+1.6$~pp. We read that gap as the empirical price of any prompt-side method that drops (i)~the discriminative two-candidate construction of BCQ, where one teacher-correct trace and one student-wrong trace are anonymized and shuffled with no correctness label, and (ii)~the collective-negative aggregation of NCQ, where every wrong student rollout on the same question is surfaced together. Hint gives only an answer-free guidance signal with no negative side, and Prefix forces a continuation from a single teacher trace and surfaces no negatives. Neither comes close to BCQ or NCQ on either axis.

\subsection{Benchmark-selection robustness via cluster bootstrap}
\label{sec:appendix:stat_reliability}

We complement the training runs with a benchmark-level cluster bootstrap covering \emph{every} pairwise comparison reported in the main paper (Tabs.~\ref{tab:main}, \ref{tab:main_4b9b}, \ref{tab:generalization}, \ref{tab:generalization_4b9b}, \ref{tab:ablation}, \ref{tab:ablation_4b9b}, \ref{tab:ablation_gen}), not just the headline ZPPO\,$-$\,GRPO$^{\dagger}$ delta. The procedure quantifies how robust each macro-level delta is to which exact benchmarks are included in the family. For each $(\text{scale}, \text{benchmark family})$ block we resample the benchmarks with replacement $10{,}000$ times and re-compute the macro-average delta on each resample, then report the macro-average $\Delta$, its $95\%$ percentile CI, and the win-rate (number of benchmarks where ZPPO $>$ baseline). The reported $\Delta$ is this benchmark-level macro-average (the mean of the per-benchmark deltas computed from unrounded scores), so it can differ by ${\le}0.1$~pp from the Avg-row $\Delta$ printed in the main tables, which is the difference of the rounded column averages. Within each block we draw a \emph{single shared benchmark resample per iteration that is reused across all columns} and fix the RNG seed, so two columns that encode the identical comparison receive identical intervals and the cross-column deltas stay coherent. The \emph{Best non-ZPPO} baseline is the strongest non-ZPPO row selected on the full sample and then bootstrapped under the same shared resample (it may be the untrained Base, e.g.\ on $4$B/$9$B Video). Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:ci_main} reports CIs for the main-result comparisons; Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:ci_ablation} reports CIs for the ablation comparisons.

\textbf{Scope of the CIs.}~Because the resampling unit is the benchmark (not the training run or the stochastic decoding seed), these CIs quantify \emph{robustness to which exact benchmarks enter the macro-average}, not run-to-run training variance or per-decode sampling variance. Statements like ``the CI excludes zero'' below should be read as ``the macro-average $\Delta$ is robust to dropping or duplicating any single benchmark in the family,'' which is the appropriate stress test for benchmark-selection bias but is weaker than a statistical significance claim against training-stochasticity null.

\textbf{Findings.}~\textbf{(1)} The buffer-isolated comparison ZPPO\,$-$\,GRPO$^{\dagger}$ has a $95$\% CI that excludes zero in every one of the twelve $(\text{scale}, \text{benchmark family})$ cells, with win-rate $\geq\!8/10$ in every cell and $\geq\!9/10$ in every cell except $0.8$B LLM -- the pattern we would expect if the prompt-side recipe is what drives the headline gain once all other RL knobs are held fixed. \textbf{(2)} The two component-isolated comparisons -- ZPPO\,$-$\,GRPO$^{\dagger}\!+\!$BCQ (the NCQ marginal contribution) and ZPPO\,$-$\,GRPO$^{\dagger}\!+\!$NCQ (the BCQ marginal contribution) -- also have CIs that exclude zero in every cell, so neither reformulation channel can be dropped without giving up an empirically detectable share of the gain at the macro level under benchmark resampling. \textbf{(3)} The ZPPO\,$-$\,Best-non-ZPPO comparison (the column-wise $\Delta$ rendered in the main and generalization tables, and the same per-cell quantity for the ablation tables even though those tables omit the $\Delta$ row) excludes zero everywhere except on Video at $4$B and $9$B in the main-result table, where the CIs are $[-0.24, +0.90]$ and $[-0.02, +0.86]$ pp respectively. The strongest column-wise baseline on those rows is the untrained base Qwen3.5 model itself, which has already saturated several Video benchmarks (ZPPO still wins on $3/5$ and $4/5$ Video benchmarks at $4$B and $9$B); ZPPO's edge over that saturated base therefore collapses into the within-run noise on those rows, matching the few small negative entries in the $\Delta$ row of Tab.~\ref{tab:generalization_4b9b}. Crucially, the buffer-isolated and component-isolated comparisons remain robust under benchmark resampling on these same cells, so the limitation is specifically against the saturated base Video performance rather than against any RL or distillation baseline.

\subsection{BCQ and NCQ candidate-match audit}
\label{sec:appendix:bcq_audit}

This subsection audits two distinct concerns: (a)~\emph{BCQ answer-in-prompt match}, that the labeled positive candidate in the BCQ prompt lets the student read off the correct answer rather than discriminate; and (b)~\emph{NCQ rewriter behavior and triviality}, that the rewriter quietly turns the listed wrong-student rollouts in the NCQ prompt into teacher-style correct traces, or that the student trivially picks a non-listed answer without learning to discriminate against its own negatives. We rely on the per-step BCQ/NCQ debug logs each ZPPO run writes (\texttt{bcq\_debug/} and \texttt{ncq\_debug/}); the three convergence-regime snapshots per scale (the last three BCQ/NCQ-instance dumps recorded near the end of training, one per scale per branch) cover $1{,}528$ BCQ rollouts and $7{,}720$ NCQ rollouts across the four student scales. All comparisons are on the final $\backslash$boxed string, normalized by lowercasing, whitespace-collapsing, and stripping $\backslash$\texttt{text\{...\}} and similar surface wrappers. Before the aggregate numbers, Figs.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_qualitative}--\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_straws} walk five worked BCQ/NCQ pairs from the same logs across two outcome regimes (recovery, BCQ-only).

\paragraph{Setup of the worked examples.}
Figs.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_qualitative}--\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_straws} walk five hard ZPPO-$77$K questions on which the student fails every plain rollout ($\bar{r}_x\!=\!0$, triggering both branches of Algorithm~\ref{alg:zppo}). For each question we show three things: (i)~the original image, question, reference answer, and parsed plain-rollout answers; (ii)~the BCQ-reformulated prompt, in which the teacher-correct trace and one student-wrong trace are teacher-compressed under a shared compression prompt and token cap, randomly shuffled, and presented as two anonymized options, plus one of the student's $G_{\rm S}\!=\!8$ BCQ rollouts; (iii)~the NCQ-reformulated prompt, in which \emph{all} student-wrong rollouts are teacher-rewritten and listed with their parsed final answers and the student is told the answer is not one of the listed values, plus one of the student's $G_{\rm S}\!=\!8$ NCQ rollouts. In both branches the gradient sees only the student's own response tokens; the teacher-derived material lives entirely inside the prompt. Reasoning excerpts in the figures are shortened and lightly edited for length and readability (---\textbf{omitted}--- marks removed reasoning); only the final $\backslash$boxed answers are reproduced exactly. The five figures together illustrate the mechanism behind the $0\%$-accuracy graduation result in Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments:zone} (Fig.~\ref{fig:zone}), and two of the regimes the audit numbers in Tabs.~\ref{tab:appendix:bcq_audit}--\ref{tab:appendix:ncq_audit} summarize -- the recovery regime and the BCQ-only regime ($0.8$B NCQ floor) -- spanning all four student scales.

\paragraph{Recovery regime -- both branches succeed (Figs.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_qualitative}--\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_airplane}).}
Four of the five figures cover questions on which both BCQ and NCQ recover a correct rollout despite $0$/$8$ plain accuracy. Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_qualitative} (visual counting, $2$B): the plain rollouts all overcount a banana bunch as $3$; BCQ contrasts the uniform ``each of three'' trace against a per-position decomposition of which bananas are stickered, and NCQ tells the student that $3$ is wrong, prompting it to name the over-counted position rather than to eliminate from a closed answer set. Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_books} (visual counting, $4$B): plain rollouts undercount a four-spine bookshelf as three; BCQ shows the undercount against a richer colour-pattern (\emph{White, Dark, White, Dark}) observation. Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_coat} (scene QA, $2$B): plain rollouts misidentify the salient attachment as either a pocket flap or a button; NCQ confronts the student with both wrong modes simultaneously. Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_airplane} (chart-reading, $9$B): plain rollouts unanimously report slope $400$~mph by snapping the unlabelled half-step at $x\!=\!1$ down to the labelled $400$-row; BCQ contrasts that $400$-row chain against one that anchors on two exact-intersection grid points and the line's terminating arrowhead, and NCQ -- where the listed wrong-rollout answers are open-ended numeric and so cannot be ruled out by elimination from a closed set -- prompts the student to name the down-snapping failure mode and re-derive $500$ from positional grounding. In every case in this regime the gradient sees a positive-advantage rollout on a question that would otherwise contribute zero gradient under ordinary RL.

\paragraph{BCQ-only regime -- the $0.8$B NCQ floor (Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_straws}).}
Fig.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_straws} is the cleanest qualitative instance of the scale-dependent NCQ floor that the aggregate numbers in Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:ncq_audit} report (match-neg $82.7\%$ at $0.8$B). The $0.8$B student's plain rollouts overlook a cluster of straws on the right edge of a pizza-meal photo and unanimously answer $0$. Under BCQ the student is presented with one compressed candidate that localizes \emph{``three straws on the right edge''} and another that concludes \emph{``no straws visible''}; it commits to the positionally-grounded candidate on falsifiability grounds and recovers $3$. Under NCQ -- where the teacher trace is removed and the student is shown eight copies of its own ``no straws'' answer with the instruction that $0$ is wrong -- the $0.8$B student fails to override its own consensus and repeats $\boxed{0}$ on all eight rollouts. This is the qualitative shape of the match-neg behaviour we report aggregate at the $0.8$B scale: \emph{the smallest student lacks the capacity to argue against its own collective negatives without a teacher-positive anchor in the prompt}, so the NCQ-only signal degenerates. BCQ continues to work because the teacher trace is still inside the BCQ prompt.

\paragraph{BCQ audit.}
For each BCQ rollout we check whether the student's normalized $\backslash$boxed string matches any positive candidate (\texttt{teacher\_pos\_compressed}), any negative candidate (\texttt{student\_neg\_compressed}), or neither. Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:bcq_audit} breaks the result down by judge correctness. We use the table to compare two readings:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Under a \emph{trivial answer-in-prompt match} pattern, where the candidate format exposes which one is the teacher-correct trace, the student should pick the positive candidate on essentially every rollout and BCQ accuracy should approach $100\%$.
  \item Under genuine binary discrimination, where the anonymized, shuffled candidates carry no obvious surface tell after compression under the shared prompt and token cap, the student should match the positive candidate on the rollouts it gets right and match a negative candidate on the rollouts it gets wrong, and BCQ accuracy should sit well below $100\%$.
\end{itemize}
At every scale the audit favors the second reading over the first. The most direct evidence is that \textbf{BCQ accuracy in the audited snapshots is $36$--$69\%$, not $\sim$$100\%$}, so the anonymization + shared-compression pipeline does not appear to leave a trivially separable surface signal that the student can read off. The within-subset breakdown tells the same story: Correct rollouts match the positive candidate $78$--$91\%$ of the time and Wrong rollouts match the \emph{negative} candidate $77$--$97\%$ of the time, i.e.\ the student commits to one of the two candidates on essentially every rollout (the residual \emph{other} mass is $\leq\!22.6\%$, mostly different surface text). match-pos in the Wrong subset is $\leq\!2.7\%$ at every scale, but we note the limitation that a student that trivially aligned to the positive would by definition not appear in the Wrong subset, so this row is a weak rather than decisive signal on its own.

\paragraph{NCQ audit.}
The NCQ prompt is constructed differently from the BCQ prompt: only the rewritten \emph{wrong} student rollouts (\texttt{student\_neg\_compressed}) appear in the candidate block, together with the listed parsed final answers; the rewritten teacher rollout (\texttt{teacher\_pos\_compressed}) is logged for diagnostic purposes but \emph{never} inserted into the NCQ prompt. We verified this directly on every NCQ prompt in the snapshots: $8$/$8$ \texttt{student\_neg\_compressed} traces appear verbatim in the corresponding prompt, $0$/$4$ \texttt{teacher\_pos\_compressed} traces appear in any prompt. The audit table for NCQ (Tab.~\ref{tab:appendix:ncq_audit}) therefore changes meaning: \emph{match-neg} is the rate at which the student repeats one of its own listed wrong answers (the NCQ failure mode the prompt asks it to avoid), and \emph{match-pos} is the rate at which the student's boxed string matches the rewritten teacher's boxed string \emph{even though the teacher trace was not shown to it} (the student arriving at the correct answer independently). The reading of the table is now:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Under a trivial avoidance pattern (e.g.\ the rewriter compressing the listed negatives so coarsely that the student can avoid them by emitting any non-listed string), NCQ accuracy should be high but match-pos should be near zero.
  \item Under a rewriter-correctness pattern (the rewriter turning \texttt{student\_neg\_compressed} into a teacher-style correct trace), the prompt would now contain the answer, match-neg should drop sharply across all scales, and NCQ accuracy should be high at every scale.
\end{itemize}
The numbers are not consistent with either pattern. NCQ accuracy is intermediate and \emph{scale-dependent} ($1.2\%$ at $0.8$B, $22\%$/$35\%$/$31\%$ at $2$/$4$/$9$B). match-neg at $0.8$B is $82.7\%$ -- i.e.\ when the smallest student is given its own collected wrong rollouts and told to avoid them, it repeats one of them as its final answer most of the time. Only as the student scales up does match-neg collapse ($2.2\%$ at $2$B, $0.7\%$/$0.2\%$ at $4$/$9$B), in lockstep with NCQ accuracy growing. This shape is what one would expect from a student that gradually learns to discriminate against its own previously listed wrong answers; it is harder to reconcile with a static avoidance pattern, where one would expect match-neg to be uniformly small from the first scale.

\paragraph{Caveats on the audit.} Three caveats apply to both tables. First, the comparison uses exact normalized $\backslash$boxed-string match, so a student rollout that arrives at the correct answer through different surface text (e.g.\ ``\texttt{C}'' versus the prose description spelled out by the rewritten teacher) is counted as \emph{other} rather than as a match. Second, the snapshots cover the convergence regime rather than the entire training trajectory; convergence-regime numbers do not by themselves rule out a different match pattern earlier in training, although they are the regime in which any persistent match behaviour would have had the most time to be exploited. Third, on MCQ-style benchmarks with small answer spaces, NCQ's collective negative listing can in principle help by elimination as well as by discrimination; the audit table cannot disentangle these two contributions, and the open-ended worked examples in Figs.~\ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_qualitative}, \ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_books}, \ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_airplane}, and \ref{fig:appendix:bcq_ncq_straws} (free-form counting and chart-reading, all with open-ended numeric answer spaces) are where elimination is least likely to substitute for discrimination.
