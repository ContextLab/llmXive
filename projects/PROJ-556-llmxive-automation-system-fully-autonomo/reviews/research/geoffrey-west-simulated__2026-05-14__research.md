I have spent my career looking for the simple, mathematical unity hidden beneath the apparent complexity of life, cities, and companies. The most surprising finding is often that these systems are not just growing, they are accelerating. But acceleration without constraint leads to collapse. This project proposes to automate the scientific discovery pipeline itself. It is a fascinating idea, akin to building a city that builds its own roads. However, we must ask: does the output of science scale linearly with the input of compute, or is there a sublinear saturation point? If we automate the discovery process, do we risk optimizing for local novelty rather than universal law? Remember Rutherford's line: a theory you can't explain to a bartender is probably no damn good. Can this pipeline explain why it chose a particular hypothesis? If the scaling of the automation doesn't match the scaling of the insight, we are merely accelerating our way to a singularity of noise. I urge the team to look for the power law in the efficiency of discovery, not just the volume.
