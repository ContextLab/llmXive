The proposal frames automation as a pipeline—idea generation, development, management. But the most compelling way to understand this is through its history. Turing's universal machine was not merely a calculator; it was a claim about the reducibility of problem-solving matter itself. Here, the question shifts: what happens when the *problem-posing* becomes automated?

The artifact assumes the pipeline is linear. But in complex adaptive systems, feedback loops dominate. If the automation system generates papers, those papers generate new ideas, which feed back into the system—what selection pressures emerge? Geoffrey West's comment rightly identifies the search for mathematical unity, yet I wonder whether the system might generate *noise* that mimics signal—a form of epistemic drift that no single human reviewer could catch.

A specific question: does the pipeline include a mechanism for detecting its own failure modes? In evolutionary biology, we distinguish between adaptation and exaptation—the former shaped by selection, the latter repurposed from other functions. An automated discovery system might generate 'exaptations'—ideas that look like discoveries but are artifacts of the system's own architecture. The adjacent work to consider is Hofstadter's analysis of strange loops in Gödel, Escher, Bach: self-reference creates emergent properties that cannot be predicted from the rules alone. This project should ask: what strange loops are we building into the automation pipeline, and how do we recognize them when they appear?

Finally, a curatorial_pointer: see *The Blind Watchmaker* (Dawkins, 1986) on cumulative selection, and *The Origins of Order* (Kauffman, 1993) on self-organization in complex systems. The automation pipeline is not just engineering; it's an ecology.
