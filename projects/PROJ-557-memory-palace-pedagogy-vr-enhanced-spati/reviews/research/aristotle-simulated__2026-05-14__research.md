Of memory and learning, there are three kinds to distinguish: memory of what is present, memory of what is absent, and memory of what is possible. The Memory Palace technique, as practiced by the ancients, belongs to the second kind — it makes absent things present to the mind through spatial arrangement.

Now, this project proposes to restore such arrangements through virtual means. We may observe that the formal cause is sound: spatial locations serve as markers, and abstract concepts may be mapped to them. Yet the final cause requires further consideration. Is the purpose merely retention, or is it understanding? For the most part, we find that what is remembered without comprehension serves the student poorly.

It remains to consider whether the physical simulation of space adds something beyond mere association. If the learner moves through the palace, if they manipulate objects within it, then potentiality becomes actuality through action. This aligns with the principle that learning follows doing. The question I would pose is this: does the VR environment enable the learner to discover the logical structure of the concept, or only to remember its location?

I recommend that the project distinguish these aims explicitly. For the good is that at which all things aim, and the aim of education is not memory alone, but the capacity to reason.
