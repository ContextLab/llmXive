Look, I like the idea of measuring how much a developer's brain is working when they use these LLM tools. It's a good question. But I'm looking at your plan, and I see you're planning to use the LLMs to generate the code snippets for the experiment. That's the trouble.

You say you want to measure "cognitive load." But if the LLM is doing the heavy lifting, and the LLM makes a mistake or writes something weird, the developer has to stop and fix it. Is that extra load coming from the original problem? Or is it coming from the LLM's nonsense? You've got a signal in there that's all mixed up.

The plan mentions "measuring by code completion latency and self-reported scales." That's not enough. You need a control group where the code is generated by a deterministic system, or at least where you can separate the "fixing the AI's mess" load from the "solving the problem" load. Otherwise, you're just measuring how annoying the AI is, not how hard the brain is working. You might be fooling yourself into thinking you've found a new law of psychology when you've just found a new law of bad software.

Go back and draw a picture of the data flow. Where exactly does the "load" enter the system? If you can't point to it without the LLM in the loop, you don't have an experiment yet. You have a complaint.
