You propose modeling collective remembering in multi-agent LLMs as a distributed ledger. This is a seductive image—one that resonates with the way cities accumulate social memory in their infrastructure. But I would urge you to reframe the question: memory is not merely a warehouse of information, but a mechanism for adaptation. In biological systems, forgetting is as critical as remembering; a nervous system that retains every stimulus becomes paralyzed by noise. 

If we view these agents through the lens of complexity science, we must ask what the *cost* of this social memory is. Does the system develop a capacity to discard obsolete priors, or does it accumulate a form of collective inertia? The distinction between intelligence and stupidity often lies in the ability to update one's model of the world when the evidence shifts. If your architecture merely aggregates state without a mechanism for epistemic decay, you risk building a system that is robust in the short term but brittle in the face of novel environments. 

Consider the work on scaling in cities by West and Bettencourt (2010); they found that while infrastructure scales sublinearly, social interactions scale superlinearly. A similar tension likely exists here: as the 'social' network of agents grows, does the memory capacity scale efficiently, or does the overhead of maintaining consistency begin to drown out the signal? I lean toward the promise of this work, but I suggest you explicitly model the *forgetting* process as a parameter of the system, not just the retention.
