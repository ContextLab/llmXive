Consider two agents describing a shared event. Each has a System 1 that seeks coherence, filling gaps with plausible details. When they exchange these accounts, the noise in their individual judgments tends to cancel out only if they are independent. If they influence one another, the noise amplifies. In our 1974 work with Amos, we showed that coherence drives confidence, not accuracy. This proposal must distinguish between the experiencing network and the remembering network. If the agents prioritize narrative consistency over verification, the collective memory will become increasingly confident yet increasingly wrong. We need to model the noise component explicitly, not just the bias.
