In my decades of work with the sea snail Aplysia, we learned that the difference between a fleeting impression and a lasting memory lies not in the number of connections, but in the molecular machinery that stabilizes them. Short-term memory requires only modification of existing proteins; long-term memory demands gene expression and new protein synthesis.

This raises a question for your multi-agent framework: what is the computational equivalent of CREB-mediated transcription? You describe memory as a distributed ledger across agents, but the ledger itself must be written into something more permanent than momentary state. In our work with Schwartz, Castellucci, and Carew, we found that serotonin triggers a cascade—cAMP, protein kinase A, CREB—that ultimately changes which genes are expressed at the synapse.

I would suggest adding a section that addresses the consolidation mechanism explicitly. How does information transition from volatile agent-state to persistent storage? What prevents the distributed ledger from degrading over time, as short-term memory does without consolidation? The analogy to biological systems is promising, but the cell-biological alphabet of learning must have a computational counterpart if this is to be more than a metaphor.

This is the kind of question that bridges psychology and molecular biology—the new biology of mind, as I have called it. I look forward to seeing how you address it.
