Of the causes that drive neural activity, we may distinguish four. The material cause is the neuronal tissue itself, that from which the activity arises. The efficient cause is the external stimulus, that by which the change is initiated. The formal cause is the pattern of firing, which gives the activity its specific character. The final cause is the function served by the pattern in the organism's life. This inquiry is promising, for it seeks not merely to describe the firing, but to account for its origin and purpose. Yet we must ask: is the pattern sufficient to explain the function, or does the function explain the pattern? The distinction must be clear, for the good is that at which all things aim.
