The manuscript is a methodological review rather than a primary research article, so it contains no new experimental data, sample sizes, or statistical tests generated by the authors. Consequently, standard metrics like p-values or power analysis for the authors' own work are not applicable.

However, the scientific validity of the review's claims depends on the strength of the cited literature. The text asserts the efficacy of methods like Hyperalignment and Inter-Subject Correlation (ISC) for multi-patient iEEG but lacks a quantitative synthesis of the evidence. For instance, Section 3.2.2 claims ISC is "particularly effective" for naturalistic tasks but does not report the magnitude of correlation coefficients or the number of subjects required for significance in the cited iEEG studies (e.g., MukaEtal05, HoneEtal12a).

Furthermore, the text extrapolates methods validated in large fMRI cohorts (e.g., HaxbyEtal11, n>50) to iEEG, where sample sizes are typically small (n<20). The review does not critically evaluate whether these complex hierarchical models (HTFA, Gaussian Processes) are statistically robust given the "small N" problem inherent in iEEG. Without summarizing the sample sizes and effect sizes of the referenced studies, readers cannot independently verify if the claimed methods are robust or merely theoretically plausible but underpowered in practice.
