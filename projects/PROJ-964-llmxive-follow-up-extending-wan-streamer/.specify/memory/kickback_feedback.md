## Unresolved concerns

- The plan proposes a 'Hybrid Simulation' to claim latency reduction without quality loss. However, the validation section relies on FID and a 'proxy MOS' computed on the *same* generated outputs used to measure latency. This creates a circular dependency where the 'quality' metric is not independent of the generation process being optimized. The spec requires 'Validation Independence' (Constitution Principle VII), but the plan does not explicitly state that the FID/MOS models are frozen and trained on a *separate* dataset not used for estimator training or hyperparameter tuning.
- The plan states: 'Claims of causality... are avoided. Claims are framed as associational.' However, the core research question (US-3) is to validate a *causal* intervention: 'skipping flow-matching steps... reduces latency without degrading quality.' Observational correlation between 'low delta' and 'skippable' does not prove that *skipping* causes no degradation. The proposed 'Stratified Bootstrap with Propensity-Score Matching' corrects for selection bias in *which* frames are skipped, but it does not establish the causal effect of the *skip action* itself without a counterfactual (e.g., running the full solver on the exact same frames that were skipped). The current design cannot distinguish between 'frames that are easy to skip' and 'frames that are easy to generate'.
- The plan acknowledges 'Power Analysis' limitations but does not define the minimum detectable effect size for the TOST equivalence test. With a sample size of ~10,000 frames, the power to detect a small but meaningful FID degradation (e.g., 1-2%) might be insufficient if the variance in FID is high. The plan must specify the expected variance of the FID metric in this domain to justify that the sample size is sufficient to reject the null hypothesis of 'degradation > 5%' with 80% power.
- The plan assumes a 'Sampled dataset (≤ 1 GB), ~10,000 frames minimum'. For a time-series model (GRU) predicting latent deltas, 10,000 frames may be insufficient to capture the temporal dynamics of 'turn-taking' events (interruptions vs. pauses) if these events are rare. If interruptions are <1% of frames, the model may never see enough examples to learn the 'high-priority' pattern, leading to a model that only predicts 'low-priority' for everything. The plan must ensure the stratified sampling guarantees a minimum number of *events* (not just frames) for the rare classes.

- The plan validates the 'latency-quality trade-off' by comparing the hybrid pipeline (which skips steps based on predicted low delta) against a baseline. However, the 'quality' metric (FID) is computed on the *generated* output. If the estimator predicts a low delta and the system *skips* the solver, the resulting latent is an *estimate* or a *copy* of the previous state. The FID calculation then measures the distance between this 'skipped' (low-information) output and the ground truth. The core scientific flaw is that the plan assumes 'low delta magnitude' (the predictor) is definitionally equivalent to 'low information content' (the outcome). If the latent space is smooth, a low delta *is* a low-information frame by definition of the manifold. Validating that 'skipping low-delta frames preserves quality' is tautological if the quality metric (FID) is sensitive to the same smoothness properties that define the delta. The analysis must prove that the *skipping mechanism* does not introduce artifacts distinct from the natural manifold, not just that low-delta frames are low-delta frames. The current design risks a circular validation where the predictor (delta) and the validation target (FID stability) are both derived from the same geometric property of the latent space without an independent ground-truth of 'information content'.
- The plan proposes using 'Stratified Bootstrap with Propensity-Score Matching' to validate latency reduction. However, the 'treatment' (skipping a frame) is determined by the *estimator's prediction* of the delta. The propensity score (probability of being skipped) is therefore a deterministic function of the predictor variables (semantic/prosodic features) used to train the estimator. Using the same variables to define the treatment assignment (via the estimator) and then using propensity scores to 'correct' for bias in that assignment creates a methodological circularity. The propensity score model would essentially be re-learning the estimator's decision boundary. This does not correct for bias; it merely re-weights the data based on the very mechanism being tested. A valid causal inference approach would require an independent instrument or a randomized intervention (e.g., forcing skips on a random subset of frames regardless of prediction) to establish the counterfactual, which the current plan does not include.
- The plan uses TOST (Two One-Sided Tests) with a margin of Δ=0.05 to prove 'quality equivalence'. However, the FID metric is a distributional distance between generated and real images. A 5% degradation in FID is not a standard or scientifically validated threshold for 'perceptual equivalence' in video generation. FID is non-linear and scale-dependent; a 5% change in FID at a low baseline (e.g., FID=10) is perceptually different from a 5% change at a high baseline (e.g., FID=100). The plan treats FID degradation as a linear, scale-invariant quantity, which is a category error in the field of generative model evaluation. The equivalence margin should be defined in terms of perceptual units (e.g., MOS points) or a relative change that is empirically justified for the specific dataset, not an arbitrary 5% of the FID score.
- The plan relies on a 'Proxy MOS' (Mean Opinion Score) based on CLIP-based video-text similarity or a pre-trained video quality model. The validity of this proxy is assumed to be r ≥ 0.8 against human ratings. However, CLIP-based models are trained on image-text pairs and are known to have poor correlation with human perceptual quality for *video* generation, especially for temporal consistency and motion artifacts which are critical in 'turn-taking' scenarios. Using a static image-text similarity model as a proxy for dynamic video quality is a methodological mismatch. The plan does not account for the domain shift between the training distribution of the proxy model and the specific artifacts introduced by 'skipping flow-matching steps' (e.g., temporal jitter, frozen frames). Without a verified, domain-specific video quality metric, the 'quality' validation is scientifically unsound.
