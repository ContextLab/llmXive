The scientific evidence supporting the claims of PhysisForcing is generally strong, with consistent improvements across three distinct benchmarks (R-Bench, PAI-Bench, EZS-Bench) and two different backbone architectures (Wan2.2, Cosmos3). The ablation studies (Table 3) effectively demonstrate the complementary nature of the pixel-level and semantic-level losses, and the region-focused masking (Table 4) provides a plausible mechanism for the efficiency gains. The training dynamics (Figure 4) further support the stability of the proposed objective.

However, the validity of the "ground truth" used for supervision warrants closer scrutiny. The pixel-level alignment loss (Eq. 9) relies entirely on trajectories generated by CoTracker3 on the ground-truth videos. In complex robotic manipulation scenes involving occlusions, rapid motion, or textureless objects, CoTracker3 itself may produce erroneous tracks. If the model is trained to minimize the MSE against these noisy tracks, it risks learning to replicate tracker artifacts rather than true physical dynamics. The paper should include an analysis of the reference tracker's error rate on the specific RoVid-X dataset or an ablation where the loss is computed against a different, perhaps physics-simulated, trajectory source to ensure the gains are not an artifact of the tracker's biases.

Similarly, the semantic-level loss (Eq. 12) aligns features with a frozen V-JEPA 2 encoder. While V-JEPA is a strong self-supervised model, its training objective (predicting masked features) does not explicitly encode physical laws like conservation of momentum or contact rigidity. The claim that this alignment enforces "physical consistency" assumes that the encoder's relational matrix implicitly captures these laws. The authors should provide evidence that the frozen encoder's relational structure is indeed sensitive to physical interactions (e.g., by showing that the relational matrix changes significantly when a contact is broken vs. maintained) or include a control experiment where the alignment is performed with a non-physical visual encoder to isolate the contribution of the specific "physics" in the teacher model.

Finally, the policy learning results (Table 2) show a notable performance degradation on the `shake_bottle` and `stack_bowls_two` tasks (-3.0% and -6.5%). While the average improves, the lack of analysis for these specific failures is a gap in the evidence. It is unclear if the physics constraints are too rigid for these specific dynamics or if the region-masking fails to identify the relevant regions for these tasks. A brief discussion or sensitivity analysis regarding the hyperparameters for these specific tasks would strengthen the robustness claim.
