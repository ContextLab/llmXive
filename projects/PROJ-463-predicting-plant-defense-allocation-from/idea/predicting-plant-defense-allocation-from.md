# Predicting Plant Defense Allocation from Publicly Available Transcriptomic Data

**Field**: biology

## Research question

How does tissue‑specific transcriptomic response to chewing versus piercing‑sucking herbivores predict differential allocation of chemical versus physical defense traits across plant species?

## Motivation

Understanding which molecular signals foretell a plant’s investment in chemical (e.g., secondary metabolites) versus physical (e.g., trichomes, leaf toughness) defenses can inform breeding for pest‑resilient crops and improve ecological models of plant‑herbivore interactions. While many studies have catalogued defense traits, few have linked them quantitatively to herbivore‑induced gene expression patterns across tissues and herbivore feeding guilds.

## Related work

- [A biomarker based on gene expression indicates plant water status in controlled and natural environments (2013)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1310.2542) — Shows that transcriptomic signatures can reliably predict an ecological/physiological trait (water status), providing a methodological precedent for using RNA‑seq data to infer plant phenotypes.

## Expected results

We anticipate discovering a statistically significant relationship between the magnitude of tissue‑specific expression changes induced by chewing versus piercing‑sucking herbivores and the ratio of chemical to physical defenses observed in the same species. A positive correlation would suggest that distinct transcriptional programs reliably forecast defense allocation, whereas a null result would indicate that transcriptomic responses alone are insufficient predictors. Significance will be assessed via permutation‑based correlation tests (p < 0.05) and cross‑species validation performance (e.g., R² > 0.2) to establish moderate predictive power.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**
  - Query NCBI GEO / SRA for publicly available RNA‑seq experiments involving plant herbivory by chewing insects (e.g., *Spodoptera* spp.) and piercing‑sucking insects (e.g., aphids, whiteflies).
  - Filter for studies reporting tissue information (leaf, stem, root) and include at least two biological replicates per condition.
- **Pre‑processing**
  - Download raw FASTQ files via `wget`/`curl`.
  - Run fastp for quality trimming; align reads to reference genomes with HISAT2; quantify transcript abundances using featureCounts.
  - Normalize counts (TPM) and perform batch correction across studies with ComBat‑seq.
- **Differential expression**
  - For each species‑tissue pair, compute DE genes for chewing vs control and piercing‑sucking vs control using DESeq2 (FDR < 0.05, |log₂FC| > 1).
  - Derive a “herbivore‑response vector” per tissue: signed log₂FC of the top 200 DE genes common across studies.
- **Defense trait compilation**
  - Extract published chemical defense data (e.g., glucosinolate, alkaloid concentrations) and physical defense metrics (e.g., trichome density, leaf tensile strength) from the TRY plant trait database, literature surveys, and supplementary tables of the RNA‑seq studies.
  - Compute a “defense allocation index” = (sum of standardized chemical trait values) / (sum of standardized physical trait values) for each species.
- **Predictive modeling**
  - Train regularized linear models (Elastic Net) and random‑forest regressors to map herbivore‑response vectors to the defense allocation index.
  - Use leave‑one‑species‑out cross‑validation to ensure independence between training and test species.
- **Statistical evaluation**
  - Calculate Pearson/Spearman correlation between predicted and observed allocation indices; assess significance with 10,000 permutations.
  - Report effect sizes (R²) and 95 % confidence intervals via bootstrapping.
- **Robustness checks**
  - Repeat analysis restricting to a single tissue (e.g., leaf) to test tissue‑specificity.
  - Perform sensitivity analysis varying the number of DE genes included in the response vector.
