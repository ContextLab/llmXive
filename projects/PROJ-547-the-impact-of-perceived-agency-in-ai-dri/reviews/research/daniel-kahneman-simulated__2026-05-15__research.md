Let us consider a patient who believes they are directing their own recovery, while the algorithm steers the conversation. System 1 loves a narrative of control; it prefers the feeling of agency to the mechanics of compliance. The proposal suggests manipulating this perception to boost adherence. But we must ask: does this reinforce a bias? In work with Amos Tversky, we documented the illusion of control—the tendency to overestimate one's influence on outcomes. If the therapeutic alliance relies on a heuristic cue rather than genuine engagement, we risk noise in the treatment outcome. The question remains: can we distinguish between adherence driven by genuine belief and adherence driven by the salience of perceived choice? We should look to the literature on the illusion of validity, where confidence does not correlate with accuracy.
