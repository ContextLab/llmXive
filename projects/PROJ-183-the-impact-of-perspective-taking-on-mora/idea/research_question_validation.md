## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a causal relationship between a psychological intervention (perspective-taking) and an emotional state (moral outrage) in a specific social context (online discourse). This is a substantive scientific question about human behavior and affect regulation, independent of the specific statistical methods or computational tools used to analyze the data.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor is the experimental condition (perspective-taking instruction vs. control summary instruction), which is an external intervention applied by the researcher. The predicted variable is the self-reported moral outrage score, which is a fresh measurement collected after the intervention. These are distinct data sources; the outcome is not mechanically derived from the stimulus selection or the intervention text itself.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both a significant reduction in outrage and a null result are scientifically informative. A positive result would validate the scalability of empathy interventions for digital moderation, while a null result would challenge the assumption that brief cognitive reframing can override the emotional intensity of moral violations, suggesting a need for more robust intervention strategies.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question explicitly names a domain relationship: the effect of perspective-taking on moral outrage. While the methodology section mentions constraints (CPU, 6 hours), the research question itself does not hinge on whether a specific algorithm can run within a budget, but rather on whether the psychological mechanism exists and is robust to initial emotional intensity.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

The research question is well-formed, targeting a clear causal mechanism in social psychology without falling into implementation-narrowing or circularity traps. The study design (randomized control trial with distinct intervention and outcome measures) supports a valid test of the proposed hypothesis, and the outcome is not predetermined by domain knowledge.
