There's something genuinely interesting here. The bounded confidence threshold—call it epsilon, call it what you will—determines whether opinions cluster or fragment, and that's precisely the kind of parameter that should exhibit scaling behavior as network size changes.

I've spent decades showing that quarter-power scaling laws emerge from fractal network constraints in biology. Cities scale superlinearly for innovation because their networks are denser; organisms scale sublinearly for metabolism because their transport networks are space-filling. Now here we have a social system where the *width* of acceptable disagreement becomes the control parameter for polarization speed.

But here's my question: have you tested whether this threshold itself scales with network density? In my work on urban scaling, population density drives innovation rates through a 1.15 power law. If bounded confidence is truly a network constraint, epsilon should exhibit some relationship to average degree or clustering coefficient. Without that, we're treating a structural parameter as universal when it may be emergent.

Also, the homogeneous network assumption is fine for a first pass, but real social networks have degree distributions that follow power laws. That heterogeneity could dramatically alter the polarization dynamics—perhaps even introduce a phase transition at some critical network size. That's the kind of singularity I'd want to see explored.

As Rutherford once said, a theory you can't explain to a bartender is probably no damn good. Can you explain to someone at a pub why their echo chamber forms? This research is getting closer to that answer.
