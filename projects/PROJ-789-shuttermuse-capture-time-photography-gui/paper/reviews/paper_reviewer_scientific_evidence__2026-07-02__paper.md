The paper presents a novel benchmark and model for capture-time photography guidance, but the scientific evidence supporting the quantitative claims requires further clarification regarding evaluation robustness and sample statistics.

**Evaluation Reliability and Subjectivity:**
The subject-side guidance evaluation (Section 4.2, Table 2) relies exclusively on an MLLM judge (Gemini-3.0-Pro) to score "physical plausibility," "scene interaction," and "pose aesthetics." While the authors report a high correlation (SRCC=0.90) with human rankings in a small user study (n=100 samples, 6 participants), this does not fully validate the absolute scores or the fine-grained differences observed (e.g., ShutterMuse 0.34 vs. GPT-Image-2 0.35). The user study only validates *ranking*, not the *magnitude* of the scores. Given the high subjectivity of "aesthetics," the paper lacks a report on inter-annotator agreement (IAA) for the human study or a larger-scale human evaluation to confirm that the MLLM judge's absolute scoring is reliable. Without this, the claim of "competitive" performance based on a 0.01 difference in mean score is statistically fragile.

**Sample Size and Class Imbalance:**
In Section 4.2, the authors state the photographer-side benchmark contains 421 held-out samples. However, the text does not explicitly break down the number of samples for the three decision classes: `refine`, `keep`, and `reject`. The results in Table 1 show specialized models achieving 0.00% RSR and KSR, while ShutterMuse achieves 82.76% and 74.55%. If the `keep` or `reject` classes are very small (e.g., <20 samples), these high percentages could be artifacts of small sample sizes rather than robust generalization. The authors must provide the exact class distribution (n_keep, n_reject, n_refine) to allow for a proper assessment of statistical significance and to rule out the possibility that the results are driven by class imbalance.

**Proxy Metrics in Reinforcement Learning:**
The reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT) stage utilizes a reward function $R_{mask}$ (Eq. 5) based on the coverage of a salient object mask generated by BiRefNet. While BiRefNet is a strong model, it is a general-purpose saliency detector, not a ground-truth annotation of "photographic composition." Using it as a proxy for "correct composition" introduces a potential bias: the model may learn to optimize for what BiRefNet considers salient (often people or high-contrast objects) rather than what constitutes a good photograph in diverse contexts (e.g., negative space, abstract composition). The paper should discuss this limitation and how the reliance on an off-the-shelf saliency model might constrain the diversity of the learned guidance strategies.

**Conclusion:**
The core methodology is sound, but the evidence for the subject-side scores and the robustness of the photographer-side decision metrics needs strengthening. Providing class counts and expanding the human evaluation analysis will significantly improve the scientific rigor of the claims.
