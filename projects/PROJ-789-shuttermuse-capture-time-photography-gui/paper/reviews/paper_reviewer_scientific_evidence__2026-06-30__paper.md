The scientific evidence supporting the claims of ShutterMuse's superiority and the validity of the proposed benchmarks is currently insufficient due to reliance on small-scale human studies and potential data contamination in the self-distillation pipeline.

First, the validation of the MLLM-based evaluation metrics is weak. The paper claims a high Spearman rank correlation (SRCC = 0.90) between the Gemini-3.0-Pro judge and human preferences (Section: User Study). However, this conclusion is drawn from a user study involving only **six participants** evaluating **100 samples** per subset. In the context of subjective aesthetic and pose evaluation, a sample size of 100 with such a small number of raters yields wide confidence intervals and is prone to high variance. This sample size is statistically inadequate to robustly claim that an automated metric can replace human evaluation for such a nuanced task. A full revision requires a significantly larger user study (e.g., N > 30 raters, N > 500 samples) to establish the statistical significance of the correlation.

Second, the data construction methodology for the 100K photographer-side samples introduces a risk of confirmation bias and error accumulation. The "expert-seeded, MLLM-verified self-distillation pipeline" (EMDP) uses an MLLM to verify pseudo-labels generated by a model trained on a small expert seed set. While the paper reports an F1-score for the verifier against a small held-out set, it does not provide an independent audit of the final 100K training set. Without a blind human evaluation of a random subset of the expanded dataset, it is impossible to rule out that the model is learning from noisy or biased pseudo-labels that reinforce the verifier's own limitations. The evidence for the quality of the large-scale dataset is indirect and relies on the assumption that the MLLM verifier is perfect, which is unproven.

Finally, there is a misalignment between the reinforcement learning objective and the evaluation metric for the subject-side task. The GRPO reward ($R_{sub}$) is a binary exact-match on visibility vectors, yet the reported performance is a holistic MLLM-Score assessing plausibility and aesthetics. The ablation study (Table 4) shows that removing $R_{sub}$ has a negligible impact on the final MLLM-Score (0.58 vs 0.53 for Plausibility), suggesting the reward signal may not be driving the improvements in the reported metrics, or the metrics are not sensitive to the specific visibility constraints. This disconnect weakens the causal link between the proposed training method and the claimed performance gains.
