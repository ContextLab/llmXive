The manuscript presents a compelling idea—encoding textual agent skills as low‑rank LoRA adapters generated by a hypernetwork—but the writing quality detracts from its overall impact. Below are the main observations, organized by section.

**Abstract and Introduction**  
The abstract is dense and repeats the three‑point contribution list verbatim in the introduction. This redundancy could be avoided by summarising the contributions more succinctly in the abstract and then expanding on them with new details later. Several sentences contain unnecessary filler (“while preserving modular loading, scaling, and composition”) that could be tightened. The introduction mixes stylistic elements (e.g., “LLM agents increasingly solve complex tasks…”) with technical exposition without a clear transition, making the paragraph feel uneven. Adding a brief roadmap at the end of the introduction would improve flow.

**Related Work**  
The related‑work paragraph is a long run‑on sentence that lists many citations without clear separation. Breaking the paragraph into two—one for “LLM agents and skill systems” and another for “hypernetworks for LoRA generation”—would help readers follow the narrative. Also, the term “skill” is used interchangeably with “procedure” and “textual skill,” which can confuse readers; a single definition early on would resolve this.

**Method Section**  
The mathematical notation is generally correct, but the prose surrounding the equations is occasionally vague. For example, the sentence “Let Mθ denote a backbone LLM with frozen parameters θ, and let s be a textual skill document” could be re‑phrased to explicitly state that s is the raw skill description. The description of the injection coefficient α is introduced later in the paper; mentioning it here would give readers context earlier. Moreover, the paragraph on “Component‑level composition” introduces many new symbols (γc, U) without sufficient explanation, which interrupts readability.

**Experiments**  
The experimental tables are well‑formatted, but the accompanying narrative repeats numbers already present in the tables (e.g., “improves ALFWorld success by 21.4 and 13.4 points”). Summarising the key take‑aways without restating exact percentages would make the text less redundant. In the “Structured” subsection, the phrase “as shown in the left panel of Figure 1” is used before Figure 1 is actually introduced; re‑ordering the description to refer to the figure after it appears would improve logical flow. The “Composable” subsection suffers from a similar issue, with dense terminology that could be clarified with a short example sentence.

**Sensitivity and Security**  
The security analysis mixes quantitative results with qualitative statements in the same paragraph, leading to a cluttered reading experience. Consider separating the two: first report the numbers (e.g., “Under Hijack, performance drops from 52.9 % to 8.57 %”), then discuss the implications. Also, the term “latent‑skill approach” is introduced without a hyphen; maintain consistent hyphenation (“latent‑skill”).

**Conclusion and Limitations**  
Both sections are concise, but the conclusion repeats the three‑point contribution list again. This third repetition could be eliminated by focusing on broader implications and future directions. The limitation paragraph mentions “additional settings such as web browsing, software engineering,” which could be expanded into a bullet list for clarity.

**General Language Issues**  
- Inconsistent capitalization of “LoRA” (sometimes “LoRa”).
- Occasional misuse of “its” vs. “their” when referring to plural nouns (e.g., “the skills … their knowledge”).
- Missing articles before nouns (“the use of skills” vs. “use of skills”).
- Overuse of parenthetical citations that break sentence flow; consider moving citations to the end of sentences when possible.

**Recommendations**  
1. Perform a line‑by‑line edit to split long sentences, add missing commas, and ensure consistent terminology.  
2. Introduce a brief “Notation” box early in the method section to define symbols (θ, Δ, α, γ) for reader reference.  
3. Remove duplicated contribution statements and replace them with a concise summary of impact.  
4. Standardise hyphenation and capitalization of key terms (e.g., “latent‑skill”, “LoRA”).  
5. Add a short “Roadmap” paragraph at the end of the introduction to guide the reader through the paper’s structure.

Addressing these writing‑level issues will significantly improve the manuscript’s readability and make its technical contributions easier to appreciate.
