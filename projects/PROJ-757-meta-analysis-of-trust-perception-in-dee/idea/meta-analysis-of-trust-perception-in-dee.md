# Meta‑Analysis of Trust Perception in Deepfake Facial Stimuli  

**Field**: psychology  

## Research question

How do trust judgments differ between authentic human faces and AI‑generated deepfake faces, and to what extent are these differences moderated by stimulus realism and viewers’ media‑literacy levels?

## Motivation

Deepfake media can manipulate facial expressions to appear trustworthy, potentially misleading viewers in critical contexts such as news, politics, and online commerce. While isolated studies have measured trust responses to AI‑generated imagery, no synthesis quantifies the overall bias and identifies key moderators. Clarifying the magnitude and conditions of trust bias will guide the design of effective warning labels and media‑literacy interventions.

## Related work

- [The Influence of Facial Features on the Perceived Trustworthiness of a Social Robot (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.13948) — Shows that facial geometry alone drives trust judgments, providing a methodological precedent for quantifying trustworthiness from facial stimuli.  
- [Examining the Impact of Label Detail and Content Stakes on User Perceptions of AI‑Generated Images on Social Media (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19024) — Demonstrates that labeling information modulates trust in AI‑generated images, suggesting label detail as a relevant moderator for deepfake trust perception.

## Expected results

We anticipate a small‑to‑moderate overall reduction in trust ratings for deepfake faces compared with authentic faces (negative Cohen’s d). Greater stimulus realism is expected to attenuate this bias, while higher media‑literacy scores are expected to amplify it. Confirmation will be indicated by a statistically significant pooled effect (p < 0.05) and significant moderator coefficients in a random‑effects meta‑regression; a null finding (non‑significant pooled effect) would still be informative, indicating that deepfakes do not systematically erode trust.

## Methodology sketch

- **Define inclusion criteria**: peer‑reviewed experimental studies measuring explicit trust judgments (Likert, binary decision, or physiological proxy) toward authentic versus AI‑generated facial stimuli.  
- **Literature search**: query OpenAlex, Semantic Scholar, and arXiv with two queries:  
  1. `"deepfake" AND "trust"`  
  2. `"AI‑generated face" AND "trustworthiness"`  
  Export results as CSV via their public APIs.  
- **Screen abstracts** (two independent reviewers) to retain studies meeting criteria; record reasons for exclusion.  
- **Data extraction**: for each study, extract sample size, mean trust rating (or odds ratio) for authentic and deepfake conditions, standard deviations, and moderator information (realism level, media‑literacy score, presentation context). Compute Cohen’s d or log‑odds ratios as needed.  
- **Effect‑size coding**: use the `esc` R package (or Python `statsmodels` equivalents) to convert reported statistics to a common metric.  
- **Meta‑analysis**: employ the `metafor` R package (random‑effects model) to pool effect sizes.  
- **Moderator analysis**: fit mixed‑effects meta‑regressions with realism (continuous or categorical) and media‑literacy (continuous) as moderators.  
- **Robustness checks**: conduct leave‑one‑out sensitivity analysis, assess publication bias with funnel plots and Egger’s test.  
- **Reporting**: generate forest plots, moderator coefficient tables, and a PRISMA flow diagram using R Markdown; compile results into a PDF manuscript. All scripts and data files will be hosted in a public GitHub repository for reproducibility.
