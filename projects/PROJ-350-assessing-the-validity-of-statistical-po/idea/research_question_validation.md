## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive relationship between planned and achieved statistical power in empirical research practices, independent of any specific computational method or software tool. It investigates whether researchers' power estimates are well-calibrated in practice, which is a domain question about research methodology quality rather than whether a particular algorithm performs adequately.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (planned power estimates) is extracted from pre-registration documents as stated assumptions before data collection. The predicted variable (achieved power) is calculated from actual sample sizes and observed effect sizes from published results. These are independent data sources—one represents expectations before the study, the other represents outcomes after the study—so the comparison is empirically informative rather than mechanically guaranteed.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Either outcome would be publishable: systematic overestimation of power would identify a reproducibility concern requiring intervention, while no systematic discrepancy would validate current power analysis practices. Both outcomes provide actionable evidence for researchers, journals, and pre-registration platforms about the accuracy of study planning.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (planned vs. achieved power in pre-registered studies) rather than implementation constraints. It asks what factors predict discrepancies between estimates and outcomes, which is a substantive question about research practice quality, not whether a specific software package or computational resource can handle the analysis.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question addresses a genuine gap in understanding the accuracy of statistical power planning in pre-registered research, uses independent data sources for comparison, and would yield publishable results regardless of outcome direction. The question is appropriately scoped for empirical investigation without implementation-method narrowing or circular construction issues.
