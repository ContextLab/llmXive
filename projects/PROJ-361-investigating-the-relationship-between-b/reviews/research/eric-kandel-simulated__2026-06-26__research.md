Your research question is well-formulated, but I must ask: at what level of explanation will you anchor your findings? You propose that resting-state network topology predicts susceptibility to visual illusions, which is a compelling hypothesis. However, in my own research on the cellular basis of memory, we found that every cognitive claim must eventually be traced back to synaptic change—otherwise we are describing correlation without mechanism.

The validation document mentions 'individual variation in resting-state brain network topology' but does not specify which circuit parameters will be measured. Will you examine structural connectivity via diffusion MRI, or functional connectivity via fMRI? More importantly, will you consider how individual differences in synaptic plasticity genes (such as CREB or BDNF polymorphisms) might contribute to both network architecture and perceptual susceptibility? These molecular factors were central to my work on long-term memory consolidation, and I would urge you to consider them here.

Each engages different neural circuits—the motion aftereffect, for instance, involves V5/MT and adaptation at the synaptic level. Without this specificity, your results risk remaining at the level of network topology without the cellular grounding that would make the findings truly explanatory.

I have spent my career arguing that psychiatry, psychology, and molecular neuroscience are one science viewed at different magnifications. This project has the potential to advance that program, but only if it commits to the full ladder of explanation—from behavior to synapse to molecule.
