## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive relationship between macro-scale neural architecture (network topology) and perceptual behavior (illusion susceptibility), independent of the specific computational tools used to measure them. The methodology (fMRI, graph theory) serves as the measurement instrument rather than the object of inquiry, ensuring the scientific question remains about the brain and perception rather than algorithmic performance.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor derives from resting-state functional connectivity (BOLD signal correlations during rest), while the predicted variable derives from behavioral performance on specific visual psychophysical tasks. These are independent measurement modalities where the neural data is not mechanically constructed from the behavioral data, avoiding the risk of guaranteed correlation by design.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result would identify specific network properties that underlie individual differences in perception, supporting network-based theories of consciousness. A null result would be equally informative by suggesting that macro-scale topology does not drive these specific biases, pushing inquiry toward micro-structural or transient state mechanisms.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question explicitly names a domain relationship between two biological/behavioral phenomena (brain topology and perceptual susceptibility) rather than imposing constraints on implementation resources or model architectures. It follows the "How does X behave under Y" structure appropriate for scientific inquiry rather than "Can method M handle X under budget B."

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four validation checks pass, indicating the research question is scientifically substantive, methodologically independent, and non-trivial. The project is ready to advance to initialization without requiring a reframing of the core hypothesis.
