I have spent six decades persuading two suspicious tribes—psychiatrists and molecular biologists—to speak the same language. When I encountered the method of loci in ancient Greek rhetoric, I recognized what was happening: the brain was using its spatial mapping circuitry, housed in the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex, as a scaffold for organizing non-spatial information. This was not metaphor; it was a biological strategy.

Your proposal asks whether LLMs can be trained to use spatial memory techniques for improved episodic recall. This is promising, but I must ask: at what level of explanation are you operating? In my work with Aplysia, we learned that short-term memory involves covalent modification of existing proteins at the synapse, while long-term memory requires gene transcription and new synaptic growth. When you speak of 'virtual spatial environments,' I wonder—what is the synaptic correlate? Where does the consolidation occur?

The danger I see, which I have seen before in the field, is that we may mistake architectural analogy for mechanistic understanding. A transformer with positional encoding is not a hippocampus. The question is not whether spatial scaffolding improves performance—it clearly does—but whether you can trace that improvement to a specific computational principle that mirrors biological memory consolidation.

I would suggest: before scaling this approach, define what 'memory' means in your system. Is it the retention of tokens? The ability to retrieve context across long sequences? Or something closer to episodic recall—the binding of temporal, spatial, and semantic features into a coherent trace? These are different problems, and the solutions will require different cellular-level explanations.

One concrete suggestion: consider adding a benchmark that tests not just recall accuracy, but the *stability* of that recall over time, under conditions of interference. In the mammalian brain, long-term memory's defining feature is its resistance to disruption. If your system's memory degrades with competing context, you have not achieved episodic memory—you have achieved retrieval, which is a different process.

This is the path forward. Name the behavior, then ask which cells, which synapses, which molecules. The new biology of mind depends on this rigor.
