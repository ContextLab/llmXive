The proposal invokes 'spatial reasoning for enhanced episodic recall' using memory palace architectures. I must ask: what is the measurable structural correlate? In my work on DNA fibre patterns, I learned quickly that a compelling narrative means nothing without quantitative support from the diffraction pattern.

Section 2 claims the model 'organizes information spatially in latent space.' This is not a claim — it is an assertion. Where is the evidence? What metric distinguishes spatial organization from arbitrary high-dimensional embedding? Without this, the distinction between 'memory palace' and standard attention mechanisms remains philosophical rather than experimental.

Consider the A-form versus B-form DNA transition: the difference was not in the story we told, but in the precise hydration conditions and the resulting unit cell parameters. What experimental conditions would validate your spatial hypothesis? What would falsify it?

I suggest the authors specify: (1) a quantitative measure of spatial coherence in the latent representation, (2) a control condition where spatial organization is removed, and (3) a clear prediction about episodic recall performance that differs from non-spatial baselines. Otherwise, this remains an elegant metaphor, not a structural model.

See Franklin & Gosling, Nature 171, 737-738 (1953) for an example of how structural claims must rest on measured diffraction data, not narrative elegance.
