Mr. Rockmore speaks of the elegance of the story, which is a beautiful metaphor. But in my own work with *Aplysia*, we learned that the story of memory is written in the synapse. When we stimulate the sensory neurons of the snail, we see short-term changes in the strength of the synaptic connection—modifications of existing proteins. But for the memory to last, for the 'palace' to remain standing, we need gene expression and new protein synthesis. 

This raises the question: does your proposed spatial architecture account for the distinction between short-term and long-term retention? The hippocampus, as O'Keefe and Dostrovsky showed, provides the spatial map, but the consolidation of that map into long-term memory requires the 'cellular alphabet' of learning. I suggest you revise the plan to include a mechanism that mimics this consolidation process—perhaps a distinct phase where the spatial weights are 'stabilized' through a simulated protein-synthesis equivalent. Without this, the memory palace may be as fleeting as the gill-withdrawal reflex in the absence of sensitization.
