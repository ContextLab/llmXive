\subsection{Hard Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:error_analysis}
% ============================================================

To understand \emph{why} frontier models fail, we identify the 30 questions on which \emph{all seven evaluated models} score $\leq 3$ out of 10 --- questions so hard that not a single model produces even a partial answer. We use GPT-5 to classify each failure into one of seven mutually exclusive error categories based on the question, ground-truth answer, and all model responses. Figure~\ref{fig:error_analysis} shows the resulting distribution.

\paragraph{Primary failure mode: Visual Hallucination.}
The model confidently reads a wrong value from the chart (e.g., reports 1000 ppm instead of 30 ppm). The chart is found but the pixel-level reading is incorrect.
This is the dominant error category, accounting for 17 of 30 hard failures (57\%).
It concentrates heavily in chart-reading questions (9 of 17 cases), where models must read precise numerical values from dense heatmaps, stacked bars, and multi-series line charts embedded in 50-page documents.

\paragraph{Figure Not Found and Precision Error.}
The model cannot locate the relevant figure in the document and responds 'Not found in the document' or retrieves a different figure. Together, these two categories account for 10 failures (34\%). The model extracts the correct element but with wrong exact value, scale, or unit (e.g., reads 29% instead of 27.5%, or billions instead of millions).

\paragraph{Implications.}
The dominance of visual hallucination and precision errors reveals that the primary bottleneck in hard cases is \emph{low-level perceptual fidelity}---the ability to read exact values from dense chart encodings at scale---rather than high-level reasoning. Cross-modal grounding failures, which are concentrated in cross-modal and complex questions, point to a distinct second bottleneck: models can retrieve text and chart evidence separately but cannot perform the quantitative alignment step that the question demands. These findings suggest that future progress on \textsc{SynthDocBench} will require improvements to both chart-reading resolution and structured cross-modal reasoning, neither of which is addressed by prompt engineering alone (see Section~\ref{sec:ablation_prompt}).
