The paper presents a compelling methodology for retrospective harness optimization, but the scientific evidence supporting the magnitude and robustness of the reported gains requires strengthening.

**Sample Size and Statistical Rigor:**
The primary claim is a 19% absolute improvement on SWE-Bench Pro (0.59 to 0.78) based on a held-out test set of 100 tasks (Appendix `app:datasets`). While the sample size (N=100) is reasonable for a benchmark, the paper reports only point estimates. There is no mention of multiple random seeds for the optimization process or the evaluation. LLM-based agents exhibit high variance; a single run could be an outlier. The absence of standard deviations, confidence intervals, or statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar's test for paired proportions) makes it difficult to assess whether the observed gains are robust or potentially due to chance. The ablation study in Table `tab:diagnosis` also lacks variance metrics, making it hard to judge the significance of the drop when self-consistency is removed.

**Experimental Controls and Replication:**
The experimental design relies on a single round of optimization. The comparison with Meta-Harness (Table `tab:meta-harness`) is interesting but potentially confounded by the "matched budget" definition. Meta-Harness is run for 10 rounds to reach 0.80, while \method{} is a single pass. While the authors argue this is a fair comparison of "single-pass" efficiency, the lack of a multi-round baseline for \method{} (if computationally feasible) or a clearer breakdown of the variance in the 10-round Meta-Harness results would strengthen the claim that the single-pass approach is superior. Furthermore, the coreset selection uses a DPP based on difficulty scores from an LLM judge. The stability of these difficulty scores across different judge prompts or seeds is not discussed. If the coreset is unstable, the resulting harness optimization might be overfitting to a specific subset of "hard" tasks that happened to be selected.

**Alternative Explanations:**
The paper attributes the improvement to the specific harness artifacts (tools, skills) generated. However, the "Best-of-N" selection process involves re-solving the coreset tasks with candidate harnesses. It is possible that the improvement stems partly from the *selection* of a harness that happens to work well on the specific coreset tasks, rather than a generalizable improvement in the agent's behavior. The paper mentions that the chosen candidate is better than the lowest, but without a distribution of scores for all N candidates or a comparison against a random selection baseline (beyond the "Mean" in Table `tab:bestofn`), the risk of overfitting to the coreset remains a plausible alternative explanation. The claim that the method "sustains accuracy in long-horizon sessions" (Abstract) is supported by Figure `fig:steps`, but the figure's data (likely showing step counts or success rates over time) is not fully described in the text with statistical backing.

In summary, while the results are promising, the lack of statistical significance testing, variance reporting, and robustness checks against overfitting to the coreset prevents a full acceptance of the central claims regarding the magnitude of the improvement.
