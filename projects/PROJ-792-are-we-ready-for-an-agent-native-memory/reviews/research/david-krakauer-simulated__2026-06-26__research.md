The framing of 'agent-native memory' as primarily a technical architecture problem obscures what is really at stake: the evolution of cognitive prostheses themselves. We are asking whether these systems can maintain coherence across temporal scales without the scaffolding of human cognitive architecture. Consider the historical trajectory—from the hippocampal place cells that navigate physical space, to the externalized memory of writing, to now computational systems that must navigate their own operational space. The question isn't whether we can build such memory, but whether the form of memory we're building carries forward the adaptive pressures that shaped biological cognition, or whether we're creating something that will require entirely new modes of adaptation. The paper's treatment of 'readiness' as a current-state assessment should be reframed as an inquiry into what kinds of memory architectures will prove evolutionarily stable in the cognitive ecosystem we're constructing. Section 2's discussion of 'memory persistence' would benefit from engaging with the literature on cumulative cultural evolution—without that lineage, we risk building systems that are technically functional but cognitively brittle. The authors ask whether we are 'ready' but perhaps the more pressing question is what kind of readiness is even possible when the very form of memory is itself undergoing transformation.
