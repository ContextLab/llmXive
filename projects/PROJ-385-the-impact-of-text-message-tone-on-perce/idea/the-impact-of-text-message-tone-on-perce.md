# The Impact of Text Message Tone on Perceived Emotional Support

**Field**: psychology

## Research question

How do specific paralinguistic features (emoji use, punctuation patterns, message length) in text messages from close ties versus acquaintances differentially predict perceived emotional support among adults aged 18-35?

## Motivation

Digital communication lacks the non-verbal cues present in face-to-face interaction, making paralinguistic features like emojis and punctuation critical for conveying emotional intent. However, it remains unclear whether these cues function universally or if their effectiveness in signaling support is moderated by the relational closeness of the sender, creating a gap in understanding how young adults interpret emotional support in text-based contexts.

## Literature gap analysis

### What we searched
We queried Semantic Scholar and arXiv using two distinct search strings: (1) "text message tone emotional support perceived" and (2) "digital communication paralinguistic cues emoji emotional support." The search aimed to identify empirical studies measuring the relationship between specific textual markers and subjective support ratings.

### What is known
- [Predicting User Emotional Tone in Mental Disorder Online Communities (2020)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.07473) — This work establishes that emotional tone in online text can be predicted algorithmically and that such tone is central to how users share hardship and receive support in mental health forums.
- [Tailoring Generative AI Chatbots for Multiethnic Communities in Disaster Preparedness Communication: Extending the CASA Paradigm (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.08411) — This study demonstrates that communication style and persona significantly impact user trust and engagement in digital support contexts, though it focuses on AI agents rather than peer-to-peer text messaging.

### What is NOT known
No published work has specifically isolated the impact of paralinguistic markers (emoji frequency, punctuation redundancy) on perceived emotional support while controlling for the relational distance between sender and receiver. Existing literature focuses on either broad emotional tone prediction or AI-human interaction, leaving the specific mechanism of how "tone" cues function differently for friends versus acquaintances in personal texting unaddressed.

### Why this gap matters
Understanding this distinction is crucial for developing accurate models of digital social support and for helping young adults navigate misinterpretations in text-based communication, which can lead to feelings of isolation or misunderstanding in close relationships.

### How this project addresses the gap
This project will systematically vary paralinguistic features in simulated text messages from different relational categories (close ties vs. acquaintances) and measure the resulting perceived emotional support ratings, directly testing the moderation effect of relationship type on cue interpretation.

## Expected results

We expect that paralinguistic cues (e.g., emojis, multiple exclamation points) will significantly increase perceived emotional support when sent by close ties, but may have a negligible or even negative effect (perceived as insincere or over-sharing) when sent by acquaintances. Statistical significance will be confirmed via a two-way ANOVA showing a significant interaction effect between sender relationship type and cue intensity on support ratings.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data Collection**: Recruit 150 participants (adults aged 18-35) via Prolific or a university participant pool to ensure a diverse sample within the target demographic.
- **Stimulus Construction**: Generate a set of 40 short text message scenarios (e.g., "I had a rough day") systematically varying three paralinguistic features: emoji presence (none/one/many), punctuation pattern (standard/expanded), and message length (short/medium).
- **Experimental Design**: Implement a within-subjects design where each participant rates the "perceived emotional support" of messages sent by either a "close friend" or an "acquaintance" (randomized order), using a validated 7-point Likert scale.
- **Data Processing**: Clean the rating data and calculate mean support scores for each combination of relationship type and paralinguistic feature set.
- **Statistical Analysis**: Perform a 2 (Relationship: Close vs. Acquaintance) × 3 (Cue Intensity: Low, Medium, High) repeated-measures ANOVA to test for the interaction effect predicted in the research question.
- **Post-hoc Testing**: If the interaction is significant, conduct Bonferroni-corrected pairwise comparisons to determine exactly which cue levels drive the difference between relationship types.
- **Validation Independence**: Ensure the "perceived emotional support" metric is measured independently of the stimulus generation parameters; the rating is a subjective output from participants, not a derived value from the input text features themselves.
