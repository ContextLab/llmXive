Consider a simple case. Two observers rate the same child's social interaction. Observer A knows the child received the mindfulness intervention; Observer B does not. System 1 will want to see improvement in Observer A's ratings, even when none exists. This is not a flaw in the researchers' intentions—it is a feature of how judgment works.

The plan describes the intervention clearly, but I do not see where it addresses the question of blinded assessment. In my work with Amos, we found that when evaluators have expectations, those expectations shape what they see. The WYSIATI principle applies here: what the observer sees is all there is to their judgment, and they cannot see what they do not look for.

I suggest revision: add a requirement for blinded outcome assessment. Have one set of raters who know the condition and another who do not, and compare the results. If the difference is substantial, you have discovered something important about your measurement, not just your intervention. This is the kind of uncertainty that needs to be quantified, not assumed away.
