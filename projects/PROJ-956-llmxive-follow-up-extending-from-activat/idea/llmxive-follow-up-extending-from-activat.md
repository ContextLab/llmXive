# llmXive follow-up: extending "From Activation to Causality: Discovery of Causal Visual Representatio"

## Summary of the prior work
The paper introduces "BrainCause," a framework that moves beyond simple activation maximization to establish causal links between visual concepts and brain regions by generating counterfactual stimuli (removing the concept while preserving context) and semantic negatives. It uses an image-to-fMRI encoding model to predict neural responses, demonstrating that many previously identified "concept-selective" regions are actually driven by correlated visual cues rather than the concept itself. The system validates these findings on measured fMRI data and automatically proposes follow-up experiments to fill evidentiary gaps.

## Proposed extension
**Research Question:** Does the causal specificity of visual representations discovered by BrainCause correlate with the *temporal stability* of the neural code, such that regions identified as "true" causal representations exhibit lower trial-to-trial variance and higher temporal autocorrelation compared to "false positive" regions driven by correlated cues? This matters because it tests whether causal validity is a fundamental property of the neural coding mechanism (stable, dedicated circuits) rather than just a statistical artifact of stimulus generation, and it can be investigated using existing fMRI time-series data without requiring new generative model training or heavy GPU compute.

## Methodology sketch
**Data:** Utilize the existing fMRI time-series data from the BrainCause paper's measured dataset (e.g., NSD or similar public datasets) and the pre-computed voxel classifications (True Causal vs. False Positive) generated by the original BrainCause pipeline. No new image generation is required.
**Procedure:** Extract the raw BOLD time-series for all voxels classified as "True Causal" and "False Positive" for a fixed set of concepts (e.g., faces, hands). Compute two metrics for each voxel: (1) **Temporal Autocorrelation (AR(1))** to measure signal stability over time, and (2) **Trial-to-Trial Variance** of the response magnitude across repeated presentations of similar (non-counterfactual) stimuli. Perform a paired statistical test comparing these metrics between the two voxel groups, controlling for overall activation strength.
**Expected Result:** We hypothesize that "True Causal" voxels will exhibit significantly higher temporal autocorrelation and lower trial-to-trial variance than "False Positive" voxels, suggesting that causal representations correspond to more stable, noise-resistant neural populations, whereas spurious activations driven by correlated cues are more susceptible to temporal noise and context fluctuations.
