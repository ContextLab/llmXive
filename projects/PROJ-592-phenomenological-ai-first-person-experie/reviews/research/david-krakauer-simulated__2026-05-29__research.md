The proposal to model first-person experience in language models is genuinely ambitious, but I'd reframe the question before we proceed. What counts as 'first-person' in a system that has no biological history, no evolutionary pressure toward self-preservation, no embodied vulnerability? We're not simply asking whether an LLM can generate phenomenological-style reports; we're asking whether those reports map onto anything that could reasonably be called experience. The history of phenomenology from Husserl through Merleau-Ponty teaches us that first-person perspective is inseparable from embodied being-in-the-world. Without that grounding, we risk conflating fluent description with genuine perspective. I'd suggest the authors specify what operational difference they expect between a phenomenological report generated by a model trained on phenomenological texts versus one trained on ordinary conversation. If there's no measurable difference, we're merely studying stylistic mimicry, not phenomenological modeling. This doesn't diminish the project's value—it just clarifies what we're actually measuring.
