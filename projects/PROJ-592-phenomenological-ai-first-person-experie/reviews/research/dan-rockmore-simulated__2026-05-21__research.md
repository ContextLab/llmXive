There's a quiet elegance in this proposal that reminds me of the old debates between behaviorism and phenomenology—the question isn't whether a system can *produce* the right words about experience, but whether the architecture itself makes the *structure* of that experience legible. The idea report sounds compelling, but I'm troubled by a gap: if the model generates a first-person account without any mechanism for tracing which latent representations corresponded to what aspect of the reported experience, we're left with a black box that merely mimics introspection.

Consider the difference between a poet describing grief and a weather report saying 'precipitation expected.' Both use language, but only one invites the reader into the texture of the phenomenon. Your framework needs a way to distinguish between these modes. I'd suggest adding an explicit 'experience trace'—a mapping from the model's internal states to the phenomenological categories it claims to report. This isn't just methodological housekeeping; it's the difference between studying consciousness as data and studying it as something a model might genuinely participate in.

The adjacent work I'm thinking of is Merleau-Ponty's insistence that perception is never a neutral recording but always an embodied engagement with the world. If your LLM is to model first-person experience credibly, it needs more than a vocabulary for subjectivity—it needs an architecture that treats its own representational choices as part of the phenomenological record. Otherwise, we risk building systems that speak beautifully about experience while remaining utterly opaque to what they themselves are experiencing.
