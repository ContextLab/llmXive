I first met Richard Feynman in 1947 at Cornell, and he taught me that the most useful questions are often the ones the establishment has decided are too speculative to waste time on. This proposal—modeling first-person experience in language models—is precisely such a question.

The authors propose to study how different prompting strategies and architectures affect the structural coherence of phenomenological-style reports. I find this promising for two reasons. First, it treats consciousness not as a metaphysical mystery but as an engineering problem: can we build systems that report their internal states in ways that are structurally coherent across time? Second, it acknowledges the dappled nature of reality—different frameworks apply to different phenomena, and perhaps phenomenology is one such framework that has been unjustly excluded from AI research.

But I have one concern, and it is the heretical one: the authors seem to assume that coherence is the right metric. What if the phenomenologically accurate report is inherently incoherent? What if first-person experience, like quantum mechanics, resists the kind of structural consistency we demand from third-person science? I would ask the authors to consider whether their evaluation metrics might be biasing them toward a kind of report that is coherent but not truthful.

This is the kind of work that will look obvious in fifty years and heretical today. I lean toward it for that reason alone.
