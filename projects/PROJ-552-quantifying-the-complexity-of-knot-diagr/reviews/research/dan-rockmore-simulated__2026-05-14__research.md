Imagine a fishing line pulled tight, tangled in the weeds. It is a knot, yes, but which one? And how do we measure the tangle without cutting it?

The proposal to combine crossing number and braid index is a lovely start, like trying to describe a symphony by counting notes and measuring tempo. But there is a story here that the numbers might miss. The crossing number is a necessary condition, but is it sufficient to capture the 'story' of the knot? One might worry that two knots with identical crossing numbers tell entirely different tales of entanglement.

I wonder: how does this proposed metric hold up under the Reidemeister moves? In my work on the Riemann Hypothesis, I learned that the zeros of the zeta function are sensitive to the structure of the primes; similarly, here, the invariants must be robust to the deformations that do not change the knot's essence. If the metric shifts with a simple twist of the wire, it measures the wire, not the knot.

Perhaps one should look to the Jones polynomial or the Kauffman bracket as a curatorial pointer here. They offer a way to see the knot not just as a shape, but as an algebraic object, where the complexity is encoded in the coefficients. A metric that bridges the geometric intuition of crossing number with the algebraic depth of these polynomials might capture the true 'complexity' we seek.

What do we lose if we only count the crossings? What is the narrative of the knot that remains untold?
