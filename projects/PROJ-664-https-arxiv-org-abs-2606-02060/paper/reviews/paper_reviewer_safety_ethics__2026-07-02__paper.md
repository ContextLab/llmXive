The paper addresses a critical safety and reliability issue: the inability of current evaluation metrics to detect *how* deep-research agents fail, specifically focusing on "harmful error spans" that propagate through a trajectory. The proposed TELBench and DRIFT framework are well-aligned with safety goals, as they aim to improve the interpretability and auditability of agent decision-making processes.

However, there are specific ethical and procedural gaps in the data collection and annotation description that require clarification before the work can be fully accepted from a safety/ethics perspective:

1.  **Annotator Bias and Conflict of Interest:** In the Appendix (Section "Annotation Guidelines and Annotator Information"), the authors state that the seven expert annotators were "recruited from the research team and close collaborators" and that the work was "conducted as part of the research project." While this ensures domain expertise, it introduces a significant risk of confirmation bias. The manuscript lacks a discussion on how this potential bias was mitigated (e.g., blinding, independent adjudication protocols). Furthermore, the text does not mention whether an Institutional Review Board (IRB) or equivalent ethics committee reviewed this internal annotation process. Given the high stakes of training safety-critical models on this data, the authors should explicitly state the ethical oversight measures taken.

2.  **Data Licensing and Proprietary Content:** The dataset TELBench is built from trajectories generated by running public benchmarks through proprietary, closed-source models (GPT-5, Claude-Sonnet-4.5, Gemini-2.5-Pro). While the *tasks* are public, the *outputs* (the trajectories) are generated by models with specific Terms of Service (ToS). The paper does not address whether the redistribution of these specific model outputs in a public benchmark (via Hugging Face) complies with the ToS of OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google. Some model providers restrict the redistribution of their raw outputs. Failure to address this could lead to the dataset being taken down, undermining the reproducibility and safety utility of the work.

3.  **Definition of "Harmful":** The paper defines an error span as one that introduces a "mistaken, unsupported, contradicted, or prematurely committed judgment." While this is a technical definition, the term "harmful" in the abstract implies a broader safety impact. The authors should clarify if "harmful" strictly refers to factual incorrectness leading to task failure, or if it encompasses potential downstream safety risks (e.g., hallucinated medical advice, legal misinformation). If the latter, the annotation guidelines should explicitly include safety-specific criteria, which are currently absent from the provided text.

Addressing these points will strengthen the ethical robustness of the dataset and the validity of the safety claims made in the paper.
