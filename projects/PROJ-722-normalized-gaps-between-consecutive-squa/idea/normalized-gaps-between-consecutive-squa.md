# Normalized Gaps Between Consecutive Squarefree Numbers

**Field**: mathematics  

## Research question

Do the gaps between consecutive squarefree integers, after normalizing by their empirical mean, follow an exponential distribution?

## Motivation

Squarefree numbers appear with density \(6/\pi^{2}\) and are often modeled as a random thinning of the integers. Demonstrating that their normalized gaps obey an exponential law would provide quantitative support for this “random‑like” heuristic, linking elementary number‑theoretic objects to classic waiting‑time statistics. Moreover, a simple, reproducible benchmark would aid future work on probabilistic models of arithmetic sequences.

## Related work

- [Negative moments of the gaps between consecutive primes (2018)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1805.04940) — Provides heuristic formulas and statistical tests (e.g., KS test) for prime gap distributions, offering a methodological template for assessing exponentiality in gap data.  
- [On moments of gaps between consecutive squarefree numbers (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.08448) — Directly studies moments of squarefree gaps, establishing asymptotic formulas that motivate examining the full distribution and its scaling.  
- [A note on gaps (2008)](https://arxiv.org/abs/0809.3458) — Analyzes prime gap statistics and discusses exponential‑type limiting behavior, useful for comparative interpretation of our squarefree‑gap results.  
- [Poisson spacing statistics for value sets of polynomials (2006)](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0602673) — Shows that normalized spacings in polynomial value sets exhibit Poisson (exponential) statistics, reinforcing the expectation of similar behavior for squarefree numbers.

## Expected results

We anticipate that the empirical cumulative distribution of normalized squarefree gaps will be close to the exponential CDF, with a Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic decreasing as the cut‑off \(N\) grows (e.g., from \(10^{6}\) to \(10^{7}\)). A failure to observe convergence would suggest additional arithmetic structure beyond the simple random‑thinning model. Confirmation will be quantified by KS‑test p‑values > 0.05 and linear QQ‑plots; refutation will be indicated by systematic deviations and low p‑values.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**: Download the integer range \(\{1,\dots,N\}\) for each chosen \(N\) (e.g., \(10^{6}, 5\times10^{6}, 10^{7}\)) directly on the GitHub‑Actions runner.  
- **Squarefree sieve**: Implement a linear sieve (O(N log log N)) to flag squarefree numbers; store the ordered list \(s_{1},s_{2},\dots\).  
- **Gap computation**: Compute raw gaps \(\Delta_i = s_{i+1} - s_i\).  
- **Normalization**: Calculate the empirical mean \(\bar{\Delta}\) for each \(N\) and form normalized gaps \(g_i = \Delta_i / \bar{\Delta}\).  
- **Statistical testing**:  
  - Perform a one‑sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov test of \(\{g_i\}\) against the standard exponential distribution (rate = 1).  
  - Record the KS statistic and p‑value.  
- **Visualization**: Generate (i) empirical CDF vs. exponential CDF plots, (ii) QQ‑plots of \(\{g_i\}\) against the exponential quantiles, using `matplotlib` (pure‑CPU).  
- **Convergence analysis**: Repeat the above for each \(N\) and plot KS statistic and p‑value as functions of \(\log N\) to assess asymptotic behavior.  
- **Reproducibility**: All scripts, along with exact URLs for data (none external) and a `requirements.txt`, will be committed; the workflow can be executed in a single GitHub‑Actions job (< 6 h, ≤ 2 GB RAM).
