## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive psychological relationship (anticipated regret → choice deferral) independent of any specific method's performance. The statistical modeling approach (mixed-effects logistic regression) is the tool to answer the question, not the question itself. The controls listed (option set size, perceived risk, etc.) are domain-relevant covariates, not implementation constraints.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (anticipated regret, whether from self-report or option-attribute proxy) and the predicted variable (choice deferral behavior) are measured from distinct sources: one is a psychological state or attribute-derived proxy, the other is an observed behavioral outcome. They are not both summaries of the same primary signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result would support regret theory and help explain the "too-much-choice" mechanism; a null result would challenge the assumed causal link and prompt reconsideration of what drives deferral. Either outcome is theoretically informative and would be publishable in a psychology or behavioral economics venue.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

Names a domain relationship (anticipated regret affecting deferral likelihood) rather than an implementation constraint. The question is about human decision-making under specific psychological conditions, not about whether a particular algorithm or budget works.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question is well-formed as a substantive psychological inquiry about the mechanism linking anticipated regret to choice deferral. The methodology (mixed-effects logistic regression on public datasets) is appropriate for answering the question without becoming the question itself. The project can proceed to initialization.
