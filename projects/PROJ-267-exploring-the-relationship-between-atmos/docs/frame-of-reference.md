# Frame of Reference: Gravity Anomaly Definition

## 1. Definition of Gravity Anomaly in this Study

In this research project, the term **gravity anomaly** refers specifically to the **perturbation in the Earth's gravitational potential measured at the altitude of the GRACE-FO satellites**, converted to an equivalent **geoid height variation** (often expressed in millimeters of equivalent water height, EWH).

The operational definition used for data processing and correlation analysis is:

> **Gravity Anomaly (ΔN)** = The deviation of the geoid height at the satellite's mean orbital altitude from a reference geopotential model (e.g., EGM2008), attributed to temporal mass redistribution (specifically atmospheric river water vapor and precipitation).

This definition aligns with the standard practice in GRACE/GRACE-FO data processing, where spherical harmonic coefficients (Stokes coefficients) are converted to spatial grids of equivalent water height.

## 2. Physical Curvature vs. Coordinate Artifacts

A critical distinction must be made between the physical phenomenon being measured and the mathematical representation used to describe it, addressing concerns regarding the covariant nature of the gravitational field.

### 2.1 Physical Curvature (Invariant Quantity)
The underlying physical reality is the curvature of spacetime generated by the stress-energy tensor of the Earth system. When an Atmospheric River (AR) transports a significant mass of water vapor and precipitation across the West Coast of North America, it alters the local distribution of mass.
* **Physical Effect:** This mass shift induces a genuine, coordinate-independent change in the local gravitational field (the Riemann curvature tensor changes).
* **Measurement:** GRACE-FO measures the inter-satellite distance changes caused by the gradient of this gravitational potential. [UNRESOLVED-CLAIM: c_ff97ba39 — status=not_enough_info] This is a physical observable.

### 2.2 Coordinate Artifacts (Representation Dependent)
The "anomaly" value reported in processed datasets is a result of a specific coordinate choice and reference frame.
* **Reference Potential:** We define a static reference potential (e.g., a global geopotential model). The "anomaly" is the difference between the instantaneous potential and this reference.
* **Geoid Height:** Converting potential differences to "geoid height" involves a linearization and a choice of the reference ellipsoid. This is a mathematical construct used to visualize the potential.
* **Satellite Altitude:** The measurement is taken at ~500 km altitude. [UNRESOLVED-CLAIM: c_d8aaa627 — status=not_enough_info] The signal is attenuated compared to the surface signal due to the inverse-square law and smoothing effects. The "anomaly" at altitude is not the same as the "anomaly" at the surface, but it is the physically measured quantity.

**Distinction Summary:**
* The **mass redistribution** (AR water) is the physical cause.
* The **change in spacetime curvature** is the physical effect.
* The **geoid height anomaly at satellite altitude** is the *coordinate-dependent representation* of that effect used in this study. It is not a coordinate artifact in the sense of being a mathematical illusion; it is a valid, measurable projection of the physical curvature onto a specific reference surface.

## 3. Justification for this Frame of Reference

We adopt the **geoid height at satellite altitude** frame of reference for the following reasons:

1. **Observability:** GRACE-FO directly measures the gradient of the potential at the satellite's orbital altitude. This is the most direct link to the raw data.
2. **Standardization:** The GRACE Tellus and JPL Level-2 data products are standardized in this format (Equivalent Water Height). [UNRESOLVED-CLAIM: c_70a7e1ee — status=not_enough_info] Using this frame ensures compatibility with established scientific literature and validation datasets.
3. **Linearity:** For small perturbations (like AR events relative to the total Earth mass), the relationship between mass change and geoid height is approximately linear, simplifying the correlation analysis with AR intensity metrics.
4. **Covariance Preservation:** While the specific value of "geoid height" depends on the reference potential, the *temporal variations* (the anomalies) are robust against the choice of the static reference model, provided the reference model does not contain the temporal signal of interest.

## 4. Implications for Correlation Analysis

When correlating AR intensity (a surface/moisture metric) with Gravity Anomalies (a potential metric at altitude):
* We are correlating a surface mass flux proxy with a volume-integrated potential perturbation.
* The "smoothing" inherent in the satellite altitude acts as a low-pass filter. We expect the correlation to be strongest for large-scale, persistent AR events rather than small, transient ones.
* The frame of reference does not introduce a *bias* in the sign of the correlation, but it does attenuate the magnitude. This attenuation is accounted for in the signal-to-noise ratio analysis (T022).

## 5. Conclusion

The gravity anomaly in this study is defined as the **time-varying component of the geoid height at the GRACE-FO satellite altitude**. This definition explicitly acknowledges that while the underlying cause is a physical change in spacetime curvature due to mass redistribution, the measured value is a representation projected onto a specific reference surface. This distinction ensures scientific rigor while maintaining compatibility with operational satellite gravimetry data products.
