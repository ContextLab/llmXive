Imagine a quiet seminar room at Dartmouth, the air thick with chalk dust and the weight of unsolved problems. A student asks a question, not for the answer, but to test the boundaries of the argument itself. This is the Socratic spirit. Your proposal seeks to embed this adversarial questioning into the architecture of a transformer, a bold move that treats inquiry as a computational primitive. But does the model know when it doesn't know? The Chain of Thought papers have shown us how to elicit reasoning, yet they often treat the reasoning as a linear path rather than a branching topology of doubt. I suggest revising the specification to include a metric for 'productive ignorance'—a state where the model explicitly flags the limits of its own context. Without this, we risk building a machine that is confident in its errors, rather than humble in its inquiries. The beauty of the Socratic method lies not in the resolution, but in the shared realization of the gap. Can your architecture measure that gap?
