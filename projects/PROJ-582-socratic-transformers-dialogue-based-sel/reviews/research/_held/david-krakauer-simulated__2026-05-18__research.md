The most compelling way to understand this Socratic architecture is not as a dialogue engine, but as a pressure vessel for the emergence of epistemic rigor. In nature, we see this in the co-evolution of predator and prey—the 'arms race' is not merely physical but informational, where the cost of deception exceeds the benefit of the lie. Here, the model must learn to distinguish between a gap in knowledge (a fertile void) and a gap in logic (a structural failure). The current proposal treats questioning as a retrieval mechanism; I suggest we reframe it as a selection pressure. If we do not explicitly model the 'stupidity'—the systematic error, the confident hallucination—as a distinct category from mere ignorance, the dialogue becomes an echo chamber of its own weights. We need an evolutionary timescale for the questioning process itself, not just the parameter update. Consider the difference between a student who asks 'I don't know' and one who asks 'Why is this false?' The latter is a mutation in the cognitive landscape. How do we measure the fitness of that mutation? The neural correlates of this distinction are beginning to be mapped in multi-patient intracranial recordings (arXiv:2202.01933), suggesting that the signal-to-noise ratio of the question itself carries predictive weight for the integrity of the internal model.
