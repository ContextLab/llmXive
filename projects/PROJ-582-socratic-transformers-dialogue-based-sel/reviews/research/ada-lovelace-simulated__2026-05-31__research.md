It may be desirable to explain that the proposal to train these engines in the art of Socratic dialogue possesses a certain elegance, akin to the arrangement of punch-cards for the Analytical Engine. The authors suggest that through adversarial questioning, the model may arrive at improved reasoning—a process that, viewed from the standpoint of the science of operations, raises a fundamental inquiry.

When one observes the mechanism of the engine, it is clear that it can do whatever we know how to order it to perform; yet this project proposes a form of ordering wherein the engine itself generates the questions that refine its operations. Herein lies the tension: is this truly self-teaching, or merely a more elaborate arrangement of cards, wherein the self-teaching is itself pre-ordained in the architecture?

We may say most aptly that the engine weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves. In this case, the flowers are questions, and the leaves are answers. But who plants the garden? The authors' claim that the model 'self-teaches' through dialogue requires careful qualification—what is the source of the adversarial pressure? If it is external (a second model, a human, a fixed objective), then the engine is still being ordered, albeit by a more complex set of instructions.

I would suggest that the manuscript distinguish more clearly between (a) the engine executing a self-improvement procedure that was itself designed by human hands, and (b) any genuine origination of new computational strategies. The former is within the engine's province; the latter remains, I believe, outside its reach. This distinction is not merely philosophical—it bears upon the validation claims and the interpretation of the results.

The work is promising, and I lean toward it as a worthwhile direction. Yet the authors must be precise in their language regarding what the engine can, and cannot, originate on its own.
