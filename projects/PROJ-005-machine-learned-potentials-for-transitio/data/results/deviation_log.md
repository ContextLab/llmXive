## Implemented Logic

**Actual Implementation**: `src/analysis/statistics.py` (Task T035)

**Method Used**: **Unpaired Welch's t-test** (two-sample t-test with unequal variances).

**Implementation Details**:
- The function `perform_welch_ttest` in `src/analysis/statistics.py` loads residuals for Group 13 and Conventional ligands.
- It assumes the two groups are **independent samples** drawn from populations with potentially different variances.
- The test statistic is calculated as:
 $$ t = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} $$
- Degrees of freedom are estimated using the Welch-Satterthwaite equation.
- The p-value is derived from the t-distribution with the calculated degrees of freedom.

**Code Reference**:
```python
# src/analysis/statistics.py
def perform_welch_ttest(group1_residuals: np.ndarray, group2_residuals: np.ndarray) -> Dict[str, float]:
 """
 Performs an unpaired Welch's t-test to compare two independent groups.
 """
 t_stat, p_value = scipy.stats.ttest_ind(group1_residuals, group2_residuals, equal_var=False)
 return {"t_statistic": t_stat, "p_value": p_value, "method": "unpaired_welch_ttest"}
```

## Statistical Justification

The deviation from a paired test to an unpaired Welch's t-test is statistically justified by the nature of the **5-Fold Leave-Ligand-Scaffold-Out (LLSO)** cross-validation strategy (implemented in T028 and T030):

1. **Independence of Samples**:
 - The LLSO strategy explicitly splits data such that specific ligand scaffolds are held out entirely for testing.
 - A transition state involving a Group 13 ligand (e.g., Ligand A) and a transition state involving a Conventional ligand (e.g., Ligand B) are **structurally distinct** chemical entities. They do not represent the "same" reaction step measured under two conditions; they are different reactions.
 - Therefore, there is no natural pairing between a Group 13 sample and a Conventional sample. Pairing them would require arbitrary matching (e.g., by reaction type), which introduces bias and violates the assumption of random pairing required for a paired t-test.

2. **Heteroscedasticity (Unequal Variances)**:
 - Preliminary analysis (T033/T034) suggests that the error distributions for Group 13 ligands (often less represented in training) may exhibit different variance compared to Conventional ligands.
 - The standard Student's t-test assumes equal variances. The **Welch's t-test** is robust to violations of this assumption and is the recommended default when comparing two independent groups with unknown or unequal variances.

3. **Scientific Validity**:
 - Using a paired test on independent data would artificially inflate the degrees of freedom or create a false sense of correlation, leading to an inflated Type I error rate (false positives).
 - The unpaired Welch's t-test provides a conservative and statistically valid assessment of whether the *population means* of the error distributions differ, which is the correct hypothesis for evaluating model generalization across ligand classes.

**Conclusion**: The original FR-006 assumption of paired data is invalid for the LLSO dataset split. The unpaired Welch's t-test is the correct statistical tool for this analysis.
