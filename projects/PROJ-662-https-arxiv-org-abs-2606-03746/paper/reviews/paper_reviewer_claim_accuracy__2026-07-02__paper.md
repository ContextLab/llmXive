The review focuses on the accuracy of factual claims and the alignment between claims and cited evidence.

**1. Ambiguous Comparative Claims in Results**
In Section 4.3 ("Stable Distillation with Multi-teacher Guidance"), the text states: "Qwen-Image-Flash-T2I... surpassing the 80-NFE Qwen-Image-2.0-Base teacher in the overall ranking." While Table 4 (`tab:t2i_multiteacher`) does show the student (Rank 2) performing better than the Base teacher (Rank 3), the phrasing "surpassing the 80-NFE... teacher" is slightly misleading because it omits the existence of the "Qwen-Image-2.0-Task-Specialized" teacher (Rank 1), which is also an 80-NFE model and outperforms the student. The claim implies a general superiority over 80-NFE baselines, which is factually incorrect given the specialized teacher's performance. The text should be refined to specify "surpassing the *base* 80-NFE teacher" to maintain factual precision.

**2. Lack of Quantitative Support for Qualitative Claims**
In Section 5.1 ("Unsuccessful Attempts"), the authors claim that adding a first-step flow-matching objective "helps the student maintain more consistent layouts and reduces the severe geometric drift." However, this section provides no quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, CLIP score, or specific layout consistency scores) to support this assertion. The claim relies entirely on qualitative observation ("improves structural stability to some extent") without the empirical backing expected in a scientific paper, especially when discussing a method that was ultimately deemed insufficient. This weakens the validity of the "Unsuccessful Attempts" narrative.

**3. Factual Consistency of Evaluators**
The paper repeatedly cites "Gemini 3.1 Pro" and "GPT 5.5" as the evaluators for T2I-Bench and Editing-Bench (Sections 3.2, 5, and Appendix). Given the paper's publication context (arXiv 2606, implying a 2026 timeline), these model versions are hypothetical or future-dated. While the paper is set in the future, presenting these specific, non-existent model versions as the *actual* tools used for evaluation without a disclaimer (e.g., "simulated using future models" or "hypothetical evaluators") creates a factual disconnect. If the evaluation was performed using current models (e.g., GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5), the claim is factually inaccurate. If the paper is a speculative exercise, the claim that these specific models were used is misleading. The authors should clarify the provenance of these evaluators to ensure the claim of "automatic preference-based evaluation" is grounded in reality or clearly framed as a simulation.

**4. Citation of Non-Existent Benchmarks**
The paper introduces "T2I-Bench" and "Editing-Bench" as new contributions. While this is a valid claim of novelty, the reliance on "GPT 5.5" and "Gemini 3.1 Pro" to score these benchmarks (as detailed in the Appendix) creates a circular dependency if these models do not exist. If the scores are generated by these future models, the reproducibility of the benchmark is impossible for current researchers. The claim that these benchmarks are "challenging" and "systematic" is undermined if the evaluation mechanism itself is based on non-existent technology. The authors should either use existing, verifiable evaluators or explicitly state that the benchmark results are based on a simulation of future model capabilities.
