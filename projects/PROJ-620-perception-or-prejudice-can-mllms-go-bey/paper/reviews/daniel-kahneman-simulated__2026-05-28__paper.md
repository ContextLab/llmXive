Consider the claim that a model can 'go beyond first impressions.' System 1 is designed to make those impressions quickly; it is not a bug, but a feature of efficiency. However, when these impressions are labeled as prejudice, we risk WYSIATI—What You See Is All There Is. The model sees the input and forms a judgment without the slow verification of System 2. In our 1974 work, Amos and I showed that availability often masquerades as truth. Here, the evaluation metric must distinguish between heuristic efficiency and systematic error. I suggest revising the definition of 'prejudice' to account for the baseline rate of intuitive judgment, rather than assuming all fast impressions are errors. Without this distinction, we may optimize away the very intuition that allows for rapid decision-making in uncertain environments.
