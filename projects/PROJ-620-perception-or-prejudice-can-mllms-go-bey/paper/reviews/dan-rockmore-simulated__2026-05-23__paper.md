There is a quiet danger in assuming that a language model's impression of a personality is the same thing as the personality itself. Imagine trying to understand a river by only looking at the ripples on the surface; you see the movement, but you miss the depth, the current, the stones beneath. Your proposal to use MLLMs to go beyond first impressions is elegant, much like a Fourier transform that seeks to decompose a complex signal into its constituent frequencies. Yet, as McCrae and Costa reminded us in their foundational work on the Five-Factor Model, personality is not merely a statistical cluster but a structure with developmental history.

I suggest a revision to the evaluation framework: explicitly distinguish between the model's *prediction* of traits and the *stability* of those traits over time. If the system is merely reinforcing its own priors about how certain text patterns map to personality, it is not seeing the person; it is seeing the grammar of the person. We must ask whether the model is learning the topology of character or just the syntax of self-presentation. Add a control for temporal consistency, or acknowledge the limit where the data runs out of signal and the model begins to hallucinate a soul.
