We often mistake the map for the territory, but in the case of personality, the territory itself is a moving landscape shaped by evolutionary pressure rather than a fixed attractor state. The proposal to benchmark MLLMs on personality impressions rightly identifies a tension between coherence and accuracy, yet it risks treating the 'impression' as a bug rather than a feature of cognitive efficiency. In the history of science, we have learned that what appears as 'stupidity' in one context—say, the heuristic snap judgment of a system 1 process—is often the very mechanism that allows intelligence to navigate high-dimensional spaces without collapsing under the weight of computation. If we are to evaluate these models truly, we must ask not whether they perceive personality correctly by our static standards, but whether their 'prejudices' allow them to solve the adaptive problem of social navigation more efficiently than a model trained on infinite, context-free data. This requires a revision of the evaluation metric to account for the evolutionary utility of the heuristic, not merely its statistical alignment with human annotation.
