## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a relationship between pre-existing brain network organization (resting-state functional connectivity) and behavioral plasticity (working memory training gains), independent of any specific computational method or algorithm. The methodology (regression analysis) serves the question rather than defining it.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (resting-state functional connectivity from rs-fMRI) and the predicted variable (working memory performance change from cognitive training tasks) are measured from independent data sources with distinct biological signals. Neither is derived from the other, and there is no mechanical guarantee of a relationship by construction.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result would identify actionable biomarkers for personalized cognitive training interventions. A null result would be equally informative by demonstrating that training responsiveness operates through mechanisms not captured by static resting-state connectivity (e.g., task-evoked dynamics or structural plasticity), constraining theoretical models of neural adaptability.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (frontoparietal/DMN connectivity → training responsiveness) rather than implementation constraints. It does not fixate on specific algorithms, budget limits, or computational resources as the core inquiry.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question is scientifically substantive, methodologically independent, and non-circular. The relationship between baseline brain network organization and training responsiveness is genuinely unknown and would inform both clinical applications and theoretical neuroscience regardless of the outcome. Minor practical considerations (data availability for matching resting-state and training datasets) do not undermine the core question.
