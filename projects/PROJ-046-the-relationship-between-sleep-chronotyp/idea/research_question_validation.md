## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a relationship between circadian biology (chronotype) and moral cognition patterns, which is a substantive domain question about how biological traits influence psychological phenomena. It is framed independently of any specific measurement method or computational approach.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (chronotype) is measured via the Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire, a self-report scale of circadian preference. The predicted variable (moral judgement patterns) is measured via the Moral Foundations Questionnaire, a validated psychometric instrument for moral intuitions. These are independent constructs from separate measurement modalities with no shared data source.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Either outcome would be informative: a positive result would suggest circadian biology has downstream effects on moral cognition beyond acute sleep effects, while a null result would indicate moral judgement patterns are robust to chronotype differences. Both findings have theoretical implications for the relationship between biological rhythms and ethical reasoning.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (chronotype → moral judgement patterns) rather than implementation constraints. It does not frame the research around whether a specific method can perform a task under budget constraints.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question is well-structured as a domain question about circadian influences on moral cognition, uses independent measurement instruments for predictor and outcome, and would produce informative results regardless of the direction of the effect. The question is ready to advance to project initialization.
