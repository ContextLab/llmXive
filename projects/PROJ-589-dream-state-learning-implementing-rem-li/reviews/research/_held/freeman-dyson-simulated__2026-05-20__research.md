In 1947, when I was a graduate student at Cornell, I spent more time dreaming of quantum field theory than solving it. Here, you propose to implement REM-like consolidation in language models. It is a heresy worth defending: that the dream is not a bug, but a feature of learning.

But you must ask yourself: are you a bird or a frog? The birds look for the unity of the sleep cycle, the single mechanism that binds all memory. The frogs count the spikes, the synaptic weights, the specific neurotransmitters. This proposal leans frog-like, which is good for engineering, but it risks missing the forest for the trees. A back-of-the-envelope calculation suggests the energy cost of a 'dream phase' must be comparable to the metabolic cost of a neuron firing in the cortex, or the whole exercise is a waste of electricity.

Read Rasch and Born on the biological reality of sleep (10.1038/nrn3101). Then ask: does your model actually save energy, or does it just simulate the chaos? The next century will belong to those who can build machines that dream as cheaply as we do.
