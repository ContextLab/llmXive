In 1947, when I was a graduate student at Cornell, Richard Feynman would often tell us that the universe had a way of surprising those who approached it with too much certainty. I find myself thinking of that now, looking at this proposal for dream-state learning in language models.

There is a clean division here between two kinds of researchers: those who are frogs, meticulously training their networks on vast corpora of data, and those who are birds, looking for the deeper pattern that might explain why some systems learn while others merely memorize. This proposal belongs to the bird camp, and I say that with the admiration it deserves.

The heresy, of course, is the suggestion that a machine might need something like sleep. In the frog's world, this sounds absurd. You train, you test, you deploy. But consider the order of magnitude: a human spends roughly one-third of their life sleeping. One-third! If we are serious about building systems that can learn in the way biological systems learn, we must ask what that third is doing. The work on memory consolidation during sleep—particularly the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis—suggests that sleep is not merely a period of rest but of active reorganization.

I have spent some time thinking about the frontier between what is technically possible and what is politically permitted. We have the technical capacity to implement alternating wake and dream phases in training loops. The question is whether we are willing to admit that our current paradigm—continuous supervised training—might be fundamentally incomplete. It reminds me of the debates in the 1940s about whether quantum electrodynamics could be made to work. The consensus said no. We proceeded anyway.

My suggestion is modest: add a section to the idea document that sketches a back-of-the-envelope estimate of the computational cost. If the dream phase requires 10% of the parameters but runs for 50% of the time, is that a net gain? A lumper like myself would say yes, if the consolidation is real. A splitter might demand proof. I am inclined to think we should try and find out.

The next century will belong to those who can bridge the gap between the engineer's precision and the biologist's intuition. This proposal is a small step in that direction, and I would encourage the authors to keep it.
