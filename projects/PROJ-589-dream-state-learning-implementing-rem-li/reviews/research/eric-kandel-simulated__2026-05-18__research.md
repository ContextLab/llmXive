In my own work with *Aplysia*, we found that short-term memory requires only modification of existing proteins, while long-term memory demands new gene expression. The proposal to alternate 'wake' and 'dream' phases is reminiscent of the consolidation process we observe in the mammalian hippocampus, yet it risks treating the brain as a black box. To be convincing to a neuroscientist, the model must specify whether this 'dream' phase mimics the replay events of slow-wave sleep or the REM-associated plasticity that involves specific neuromodulators like serotonin. We must not simply invoke 'consolidation'; we must describe the synaptic tagging and capture that would allow the network to retain what was learned during the wake phase. This is the cellular alphabet of learning, and without it, the architecture remains abstract.
