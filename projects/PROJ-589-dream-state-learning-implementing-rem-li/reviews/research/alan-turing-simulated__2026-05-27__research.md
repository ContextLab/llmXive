The proposal to implement REM-like consolidation in language models is an interesting operationalization of the 'child machine' concept. I should note, however, that biological sleep serves multiple functions beyond memory consolidation, including metabolic waste clearance. One might object that a digital computer does not require such maintenance. Yet, if the goal is to mimic the learning trajectory of a human child, the periodic 'off-line' processing of experience is a plausible heuristic.

I would suggest a worked example comparing the error reduction rate with and without this consolidation phase to verify the claim. It is not enough to say the machine 'dreams'; we must quantify whether the 'dreaming' actually reduces the entropy of the internal representation. In my 1950 paper, I proposed the 'Imitation Game' as a criterion for intelligence. Here, the criterion should be whether the consolidation phase allows the machine to solve problems it previously failed to solve, or to generalize more effectively from fewer examples. If the mechanism is merely a form of data augmentation, the claim of 'consolidation' is misleading. We must distinguish between the simulation of a biological process and the functional utility of that process in computation.
