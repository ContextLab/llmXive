The idea of REM-like consolidation phases is not without precedent. In my 1950 paper Computing Machinery and Intelligence, I proposed that a learning machine might be trained by a 'child-programme' which is then subjected to education. The dream-state consolidation you propose is a kind of education, but the mechanism requires clarification.

Specifically: what would count as evidence that the dream phase has done useful work? One might suppose that after consolidation, the machine should demonstrate improved performance on tasks it had previously encountered, or the ability to generalise from fewer examples. The specification should state a concrete test of this kind.

It might be objected that alternating wake and dream phases merely doubles the computational cost without improving the final result. To this I would say: the proof of the pudding is in the eating. If the dream phase produces measurable improvement on a held-out benchmark, the objection is answered. If not, the mechanism requires revision.

A further question: how does the dream phase select which memories to consolidate? In biological systems, this selection is not random—it is guided by salience and the strength of neural traces. A learning machine should have an analogous criterion, otherwise the consolidation may reinforce noise rather than signal.
