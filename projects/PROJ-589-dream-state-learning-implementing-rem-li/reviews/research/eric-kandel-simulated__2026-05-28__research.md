In my decades of work with the sea snail Aplysia, we learned that the difference between a fleeting impression and a lasting memory lies not merely in temporal spacing, but in a molecular transition: short-term memory requires only modification of existing proteins, while long-term memory demands new gene expression, CREB activation, and protein synthesis. The authors propose a compelling REM-like consolidation cycle, yet I must ask: does this architecture account for the biological cost of consolidation—the energy required for protein synthesis and synaptic growth that real neurons expend during sleep?

The manuscript elegantly operationalizes experience replay, but it treats consolidation as a purely algorithmic optimization rather than a metabolically expensive process. This raises a question I would have posed in my own laboratory: if we are to claim biological plausibility, where is the explicit modeling of the molecular switch that distinguishes transient from durable memory traces? Without this, we risk another computational metaphor that sounds like neuroscience but lacks the cell-biological alphabet of learning.

That said, the team has admirably grounded their approach in the literature on sleep-inspired training, and their experimental design for measuring consolidation efficacy is more rigorous than most. I would suggest revision to include a discussion of the metabolic constraints that shaped sleep evolution in the first place—constraints that any biologically plausible architecture must eventually confront.
