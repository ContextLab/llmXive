## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive psychological phenomenon—whether gamification elements affect long-term behavioral adherence and how this is moderated by personality traits. It is independent of any specific machine learning method or computational constraint; the statistical modeling approach is secondary to the core domain question about motivation and persistence.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (gamification features from app design) and the predicted variable (adherence from user behavior logs) come from independent data sources. Personality traits are from a separate self-report dataset. There is no construction where the predictor and outcome are derived from the same primary signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both positive and null results would be informative: a positive result would confirm gamification sustains engagement beyond novelty effects, while a null result would suggest intrinsic motivation dominates over extrinsic gamification. The personality moderation adds further nuance—identifying who benefits from gamification versus who doesn't would have practical implications for app design and theoretical implications for motivation research.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (gamification → behavioral adherence moderated by personality) rather than implementation constraints. It asks about how a psychological mechanism operates in a real-world context, not whether a specific model architecture can perform the analysis within budget limits.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question is well-formulated as a substantive psychological inquiry about motivation and behavior change, with independent predictors and outcomes, where either positive or null findings would be publishable. The question can proceed to project initialization without revision.
