## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a relationship between gut microbiome composition/diversity and mental health outcomes, which is a substantive scientific question about the gut-brain axis. It does not frame the inquiry around whether a specific algorithm or computational method performs well under certain constraints.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (microbiome diversity/composition from 16S rRNA sequencing) and the predicted variable (self-reported mental health scores from PHQ-9, GAD-7 questionnaires) come from independent measurement modalities. One is derived from DNA sequencing data, the other from psychometric surveys, with no mechanical construction linking them.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive finding (significant correlations between specific taxa and mental health scores) would support population-level evidence for the gut-brain axis and guide mechanistic studies. A null finding would be equally informative, suggesting the relationship is context-dependent, confounded, or too weak to detect at this population level. Either outcome advances the field.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (microbiome composition → mental health indicators) rather than implementation constraints. While the methodology specifies public datasets and statistical tests, the core question is about the biological relationship itself, not whether a particular method can handle the data.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question is well-posed, independent of any specific method's performance, uses independent data sources for predictor and outcome, and both positive and null findings would be scientifically informative. The project is ready to advance to initialization.
