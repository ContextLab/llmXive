# Evaluating the Statistical Properties of Synthetic Data Generation Techniques

**Field**: statistics

## Research question

To what extent do summary statistics, correlation structures, and distributional shapes in synthetic tabular datasets generated by techniques such as CTGAN, DP‑GAN, and CopulaGAN faithfully reproduce those of the original real datasets?

## Motivation

Synthetic data promises privacy‑preserving data sharing, yet practitioners lack quantitative guidance on how well different generators preserve the statistical fabric of the source data. By systematically measuring fidelity across multiple public tabular benchmarks, we can identify strengths and failure modes of current methods and provide evidence‑based recommendations for their safe deployment.

## Related work

- [On LLMs‑Driven Synthetic Data Generation, Curation, and Evaluation: A Survey (2024)](http://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15126v1) — surveys modern synthetic data pipelines, highlighting the need for rigorous statistical evaluation, which motivates this focused fidelity study.  
- [FFPDG: Fast, Fair and Private Data Generation (2023)](http://arxiv.org/abs/2307.00161v1) — presents GAN‑based generators with privacy guarantees; our work extends the analysis to pure statistical fidelity without privacy constraints.  
- [AeroGen: Enhancing Remote Sensing Object Detection with Diffusion‑Driven Data Generation (2024)](http://arxiv.org/abs/2411.15497v3) — demonstrates diffusion‑based synthetic data for images, underscoring that fidelity assessment is a cross‑modal challenge applicable to tabular data as well.  
- [Power‑law distributions in empirical data (2018)](https://doi.org/10.1184/r1/6586835.v1) — provides statistical tools for detecting heavy‑tailed behavior, useful for comparing marginal distributions of real vs. synthetic data.  
- [Statistical Inference: The Big Picture (2011)](http://arxiv.org/abs/1106.2895v2) — offers a broad perspective on hypothesis testing, informing our choice of statistical tests for fidelity assessment.  
- [Calibrated Bayes, for Statistics in General, and Missing Data in Particular (2011)](http://arxiv.org/abs/1108.1917v1) — discusses calibrated inference, relevant when interpreting discrepancies between real and synthetic statistics.  
- [Byzantine‑Resilient SGD in High Dimensions on Heterogeneous Data (2020)](http://arxiv.org/abs/2005.07866v1) — explores optimization under data heterogeneity, reminding us to account for training stability of generative models on diverse tabular sources.  
- [The Statistical Analysis of fMRI Data (2009)](http://arxiv.org/abs/0906.3662v1) — exemplifies complex multivariate statistical comparison techniques that can be adapted for evaluating synthetic‑real correlation structures.

## Expected results

We anticipate quantifiable trade‑offs: methods like CTGAN will excel at reproducing marginal distributions but may distort higher‑order correlations, while DP‑GAN may preserve privacy at the cost of increased statistical divergence. Confirmation will come from statistically significant differences (e.g., KS‑test p < 0.05) in a majority of evaluated metrics for each method; falsification would be a lack of systematic pattern across datasets.

## Methodology sketch

- **Select public tabular benchmarks** (e.g., UCI Adult, Credit Card Fraud, Higgs, OpenML “Covertype”) and download via direct URLs/`wget`.  
- **Preprocess**: handle missing values, encode categoricals (one‑hot), standardize numerics, and split into train/hold‑out sets.  
- **Install and configure generators**: use the `sdv` Python library to run CTGAN, DP‑GAN (privacy‑budget‑controlled), and CopulaGAN with default hyper‑parameters; limit training to ≤ 30 epochs to stay within 7 GB RAM.  
- **Generate synthetic datasets** matching the size of the real training set for each method.  
- **Compute statistical descriptors** for both real and synthetic data: means, variances, marginal PDFs (via kernel density estimates), and pairwise Pearson/Spearman correlations.  
- **Apply goodness‑of‑fit tests**: Kolmogorov–Smirnov for continuous marginals, chi‑square for categorical marginals, and Fisher’s z‑test for correlation differences. Record effect sizes (e.g., KS statistic, correlation deviation).  
- **Aggregate results** across datasets and methods; visualize with side‑by‑side histograms, QQ‑plots, and correlation heatmaps (generated via `matplotlib`/`seaborn`).  
- **Statistical summarization**: perform paired t‑tests or Wilcoxon signed‑rank tests on the collection of metric differences to assess overall superiority of any method.  
- **Document reproducibility**: script all steps in a single `run_fidelity.py` that can be executed on a GitHub Actions runner (≤ 6 h total wall‑clock time).
