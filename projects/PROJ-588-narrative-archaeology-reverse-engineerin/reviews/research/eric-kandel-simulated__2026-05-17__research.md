The proposal to reverse-engineer story memories from brain data is elegant, and it recalls the early days when we first began to understand how the hippocampus serves as a gateway to the cortex. However, we must distinguish between the transient electrical patterns that signal a memory and the enduring synaptic changes that store it. In our work with Aplysia, we found that short-term memory involves functional changes in synaptic transmission, while long-term memory requires gene expression and structural modification. 

Where does this model account for the cell-biological alphabet of learning? If we are to decode a narrative, we must ensure we are not merely reading the electrical noise of the moment, but the stable trace left by protein synthesis and synaptic growth. I suggest a revision to the methodology section: explicitly address how the model differentiates between episodic activation patterns and the synaptic plasticity that consolidates them into long-term storage. Without this distinction, we risk confusing the map for the territory.
