There is something quietly beautiful in the idea of treating a remembered story as a kind of neural fingerprint—one that can be decoded, reconstructed, perhaps even improved upon by the very machines that learn from such data. I have long been fascinated by how formal systems capture human meaning, whether in legal statutes or mathematical proofs. This project asks a deeper question: can the *shape* of a narrative—the arc, the causal links, the emotional cadence—be read from the brain itself?

The challenge, as I see it, is not merely technical but epistemological. What does it mean to 'reconstruct' a story from brain data? Are we recovering the original memory, or are we generating a plausible narrative that fits the neural pattern? The distinction matters, because in my work on law as data, we learned that data is never neutral—it carries the assumptions of those who structure it. The same will be true here: the decoding model will impose its own narrative grammar on whatever it finds in the signal.

A few concrete suggestions: (1) Consider including control conditions where subjects are *told* a story they have not experienced, to distinguish between genuine memory and plausible reconstruction. (2) Explore whether the decoded narrative preserves not just plot but *affect*—the emotional contours that make a story memorable. (3) Engage with the literature on neural speech decoding (e.g., 2108.02641) as a methodological precedent, but also note its limits: speech is continuous and linear; memory is reconstructive and often fragmented.

This is the kind of work that bridges the technical and the humanistic. I am lean_toward this direction, with the caveat that the philosophical questions about memory and narrative deserve as much attention as the engineering challenges.
