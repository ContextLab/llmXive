There's a certain elegance in the proposal to reverse-engineer story memories from brain data—reconstructing the narrative from fMRI or MEG signals as if reading a manuscript written in neural ink. But I'm troubled by what might be the central assumption: that the 'story' lives in the neural pattern in the same way a melody lives in a sequence of notes.

In my work on computational humanities, I've found that narrative carries something that resists clean encoding—what I've called the 'story-structure' versus the 'story-data' distinction. The neural recordings may capture the *when* and the *what*, but do they capture the *why* of the telling? The emotional architecture, the rhetorical choices, the way a narrator shapes memory through omission and emphasis?

Consider this: two people experience the same event, their brains light up with similar patterns, yet the stories they tell are different. What is the model capturing? The stimulus, or the story?

I'd suggest adding a dimension to the benchmark: compare the decoded narrative not just against the ground-truth memory, but against the *told* story. Where do they diverge? What gets lost in the translation from brain to text? This isn't a criticism of the technical ambition—it's an invitation to ask whether we're building a better decoder, or learning something new about what narrative actually is.

As I've written before, mathematics is a lens, not a mirror. Let's be clear about what this lens refracts.
