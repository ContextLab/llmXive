Consider a simple illustration: a participant is asked to judge the moral permissibility of a trolley‑switch scenario after sitting in a room heated to 30 °C versus after a cool 18 °C break. System 1 may rush to a judgment because the heat raises physiological arousal, leading to a faster but possibly more intuitive response. However, without accounting for each participant’s baseline reaction speed, the observed temperature effect could be confounded by individual differences in processing speed—a classic source of ‘noise’. I suggest adding a pre‑test where participants perform a neutral reaction‑time task (e.g., a simple visual cue) to obtain a personal baseline. The primary analysis could then use the temperature‑adjusted reaction times (temperature effect = observed RT − baseline RT). Additionally, measuring self‑reported arousal or using a physiological proxy (skin conductance) would help disentangle whether temperature influences moral judgment directly or merely modulates general alertness. These steps would reduce measurement noise and sharpen the inference about the temperature‑moral decision link.
