# Ambient Temperature Influence on Moral Decision Speed

**Field**: psychology

## Research question

Does ambient temperature affect the response time of individuals when they make moral decisions?

## Motivation

Environmental context can subtly shape cognitive processes, yet the influence of ambient temperature on moral decision speed has received little empirical attention. Demonstrating a temperature‑driven modulation would extend embodied cognition theories and inform the design of decision‑support systems that operate under varying climatic conditions.

## Related work

- [Between Rules and Reality: On the Context Sensitivity of LLM Moral Judgment (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23114) — Shows that contextual factors (e.g., framing, surrounding information) substantially alter human moral judgments, providing precedent that external conditions can modulate moral decision behavior.  
- [Quantum decision making by social agents (2012)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1202.4918) — Explores how additional external information influences agents’ decision processes, illustrating a general mechanism whereby environmental cues affect decision dynamics.  
- [Modeling and Interpreting Real‑world Human Risk Decision Making with Inverse Reinforcement Learning (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.05803) — Demonstrates computational modeling of human decision latency in risk tasks, offering methodological inspiration for analyzing response‑time data in moral dilemmas.

## Expected results

We anticipate detecting a modest but statistically reliable association between higher ambient temperature and slower moral‑decision response times (or the opposite, which would be equally informative). A significant temperature coefficient (p < 0.05) in a mixed‑effects regression, after controlling for demographics, dilemma type, and cultural region, would confirm the hypothesis; a nonsignificant coefficient would falsify it.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**  
  1. Download the Moral Machine dataset (https://github.com/ethical-ai/moral-machine) containing timestamps, geographic coordinates, dilemma identifiers, and response latencies.  
  2. Retrieve historical hourly temperature data for the corresponding locations and dates from NOAA’s Global Surface Summary of the Day (https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/data/global-summary-of-the-day/).  
- **Data preprocessing**  
  3. Match each Moral Machine response to the nearest NOAA station and hour, creating a temperature variable for each trial.  
  4. Clean the merged dataset: remove trials with missing timestamps, out‑of‑range latencies, or ambiguous location data.  
- **Statistical modeling**  
  5. Fit a linear mixed‑effects model (using `statsmodels` or `lme4`) with response time as the dependent variable, ambient temperature as the fixed effect of interest, and random intercepts for participant ID and cultural region.  
  6. Include covariates: participant age, gender, dilemma complexity score, and time‑of‑day.  
- **Inference and validation**  
  7. Assess model assumptions (normality of residuals, homoscedasticity).  
  8. Perform likelihood‑ratio tests comparing the full model to a temperature‑null model.  
  9. Conduct robustness checks: (a) alternative temperature metrics (e.g., 3‑hour moving average), (b) exclusion of extreme temperature outliers, (c) bootstrap confidence intervals.  
- **Reporting**  
  10. Generate visualizations: scatter plots of temperature vs. response time, conditional effect plots, and region‑wise summary tables.  
  11. Export results and figures to the repository’s `results/` directory for downstream reproducibility.

All steps rely on publicly available data and Python/R packages that run comfortably within a GitHub Actions runner (≤2 CPU cores, ≤7 GB RAM, ≤6 h runtime).
