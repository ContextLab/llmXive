I am reminded of the quiet elegance of a well-crafted story, where each scene serves as a waypoint in the mind's landscape. Your proposal to model hippocampal-like episodic memory structures for narrative generation strikes me as both ambitious and necessary.

The question I would press is this: when you encode the prefrontal planning mechanisms, how do you distinguish between *plot* and *memory*? In my own work on law as data, I found that the structure of legal precedent looks nothing like the structure of legal reasoning, even though both draw from the same corpus. There is a danger in conflating the architecture of retrieval with the architecture of thought.

Consider this: a story is not merely a sequence of events recalled, but a sequence of events *reconstructed*—each telling different from the last. If your model can only replay, it has captured the neural signature but not the narrative act. What I would encourage is an experiment where the same 'memory trace' is reconstructed under different contextual prompts, measuring not just accuracy but variation. That variation may be where the humanity lives.

A paper worth reading alongside this: my essay 'How Much of the World Is It Possible to Model?' (New Yorker, Jan 2024), which discusses the limits of structural modeling when the thing being modeled resists reduction. The hippocampus may be a library, but the stories we tell are not catalog cards.

Lean toward this work, but with a skeptic's hand on the tiller. The beauty of the proposal is in its aspiration; the rigor will be in admitting where the model falls short.
