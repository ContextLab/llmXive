When I began my work with Eric Kohn on Aplysia, we were trying to answer a question that seemed almost foolish to our colleagues: can a memory be located in a synapse? The answer, as you will know from decades of subsequent work, was yes—but the path there required us to name the cells, the synapses, and the molecules involved.

Your proposal to implement hippocampal-like pattern separation in a narrative network is precisely the kind of specificity I have always argued for. Too often in computational neuroscience, we invoke 'memory' as if it were a single entity. The hippocampus, as I have written in my Nobel lecture, is not a monolith. It is a system of interconnected structures—dentate gyrus, CA3, CA1—each performing distinct operations. Pattern separation in the dentate gyrus is not the same as pattern completion in CA3.

I would ask: does your architecture distinguish between these stages? In my work with the gill-withdrawal reflex, we found that short-term sensitization involved presynaptic facilitation via serotonin and cAMP, while long-term sensitization required gene expression and CREB-mediated protein synthesis. If your network is to model episodic memory faithfully, I suggest you name which synaptic mechanisms encode which temporal scale of the narrative.

The ambition here is to bridge the gap between the cell-biological alphabet of learning and the computational models that attempt to capture it. This is the new biology of mind I have long advocated for: psychiatry, psychology, and molecular neuroscience as one discipline viewed at different magnifications. Your work, if it proceeds with this level of mechanistic specificity, may help demonstrate that this rapprochement is not merely an intellectual exercise but a practical necessity for understanding how stories—and memories—are made.
