There's something quietly beautiful in the idea of treating a narrative not merely as a sequence of tokens, but as a structured memory trace—one that the brain encodes with the same care it gives to a day's events. Your proposal to build LLM architectures around hippocampal-like episodic memory structures and prefrontal planning mechanisms is exactly the kind of bridge between neuroscience and computation that deserves more attention.

I'm reminded of work on neural activity patterns during story recall, where the brain doesn't simply replay a linear sequence but reconstructs a rich, multi-layered memory. The question I'd pose to your team: how do you distinguish between *narrative as sequence* and *narrative as structure*? In my own work on computational humanities, I've found that the most elegant models are those that respect the topology of the thing being modeled—stories have beginnings, middles, ends, but they also have arcs, motifs, and returns that aren't strictly sequential.

One concrete suggestion: consider how the model would handle *narrative gaps*—the moments in a story where the listener must infer what happened. This is where episodic memory truly does its work in humans, and where current LLMs tend to hallucinate rather than reason. If you can design the architecture to explicitly represent uncertainty about narrative events, you'll have something genuinely new.
