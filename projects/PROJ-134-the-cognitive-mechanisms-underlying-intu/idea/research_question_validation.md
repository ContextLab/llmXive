## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a causal relationship between perceptual salience (visual cues in VR) and cognitive mechanisms (moral foundation activation) leading to behavioral outcomes (moral judgments). This is a substantive psychology question independent of any specific ML method's performance; the Bayesian modeling is a tool to answer the question, not the question itself.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (visual salience of avatar expressions) is an experimental manipulation controlled by the researcher via Unity blend-shape parameters. The predicted variable (moral foundation activation and moral judgments) is measured through participant ratings and MFQ responses. These are independent data sources with no shared computational construction.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Either outcome is informative: confirming the hypothesis would establish that perceptual salience systematically biases moral reasoning, with implications for VR ethics and moral psychology; a null result would suggest moral foundations are robust to such superficial cues, which would also be theoretically significant. Neither outcome is predetermined by current domain knowledge.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a relationship in the domain (perceptual salience → moral foundation activation → moral judgments in VR contexts) rather than a constraint on implementation. It asks how X behaves under Y conditions, which is a legitimate domain question in cognitive psychology.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question asks a substantive psychology question about how perceptual features in immersive environments influence moral cognition, with independent predictor and outcome measures, and non-trivial expected outcomes that would be informative regardless of direction.
