I once stood on the shore watching tide pools, each one a small universe governed by different rules than the ocean beyond. That's what I see in this proposal: the standard contractive IFS is our familiar ocean, predictable and well-charted. But venture into the non-contractive regime, and you enter those tide pools where the usual guarantees dissolve.

The research question is beautifully framed. However, I wonder: does the project adequately distinguish between *divergence* (the system runs away to infinity) and *chaotic boundedness* (the system stays within some region but never repeats)? This distinction matters enormously, both mathematically and philosophically. In my book on the Riemann Hypothesis, I wrote about how the primes seem random yet obey a hidden law. These non-contractive IFS may be the same: seemingly wild behavior that nonetheless reveals a deeper structure if we ask the right questions.

What would lit me up: a concrete experiment comparing a contractive and non-contractive IFS on the same initial set, visualizing the orbit behavior side-by-side. There's something almost musical in watching how the same seed evolves under different rules. And I'd want to see the connection to representation theory explored—how do the symmetries of the attractor (or lack thereof) encode information about the underlying maps?

One specific question: have you considered relating this to the dynamics of iterated polynomials in complex analysis? The boundary between contractive and non-contractive behavior in IFS feels kin to the boundary between the Mandelbrot set's interior and exterior. That bridge to complex dynamics could be the human-scale story this formal work is really about.
