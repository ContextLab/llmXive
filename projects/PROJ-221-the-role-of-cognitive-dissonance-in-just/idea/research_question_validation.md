## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about the psychological relationship between AI usage patterns and moral disengagement scores, specifically testing whether the latter is independent of general cognitive dissonance. It frames a substantive inquiry into human behavior and rationalization mechanisms rather than evaluating the performance or constraints of a specific computational method.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (frequency of AI use for ambiguous tasks) and the outcome (moral disengagement scores) are derived from distinct survey items measuring behavior versus attitude/rationalization. The control variable (cognitive dissonance proxy) is constructed from a separate set of general ethical attitudes and behaviors, ensuring the predictor and outcome are not mechanically derived from the same primary signal.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

A positive result (independence) would challenge the assumption that discomfort drives rationalization, suggesting interventions must target disengagement directly; a null result (dependence) would support the traditional dissonance-reduction model. Both outcomes provide critical, non-obvious insights for AI safety governance and psychological theory, making the question scientifically valuable regardless of the direction of the effect.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a specific domain relationship (the link between AI delegation and moral disengagement) and explicitly controls for a theoretical confound (general dissonance). It does not restrict the inquiry to implementation details, resource budgets, or specific algorithmic architectures, keeping the focus on the underlying psychological mechanism.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass, confirming the research question targets a substantive psychological phenomenon with independent variables and meaningful potential outcomes. The question is well-posed for empirical investigation using the proposed survey data and statistical controls, requiring no reframing to avoid method-narrowing or circularity.
