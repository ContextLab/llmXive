The manuscript "Code as Agent Harness" is a comprehensive survey that organizes a vast landscape of agentic systems. However, from a statistical analysis perspective, the paper relies heavily on point estimates and qualitative comparisons without the necessary statistical scaffolding to support rigorous scientific claims.

The primary concern is the reporting of performance metrics. Throughout the text, specific numerical claims are made regarding the efficacy of various systems (e.g., LingmaAgent resolving 16.9% of issues autonomously in §5.1; AIDE achieving 16.9% bronze medal rate in §5.1; QualityFlow's Imagined Execution achieving 98%+ precision in §4.2). In the absence of experimental data generated by the authors, the survey must explicitly contextualize these numbers. For each cited metric, the manuscript should specify:
1.  **Sample Size (N):** How many tasks, issues, or episodes were evaluated? A 16.9% success rate on 10 tasks is statistically distinct from the same rate on 1,000 tasks.
2.  **Variance and Confidence:** Are these point estimates from a single run, or are they means with standard deviations or confidence intervals? The text currently presents these as fixed facts, ignoring the stochastic nature of LLM-based agents.
3.  **Statistical Significance:** When comparing systems (e.g., "AIDE achieves roughly three times the medal rate of the next agent"), the text implies a significant difference. Without reported p-values or effect sizes, such comparative claims are descriptive rather than inferential.

Specifically, in Section 4.2, the claim of "98%+ precision" for Imagined Execution is a strong statistical assertion. The review requires the authors to cite the specific benchmark size and the ground-truth methodology used to derive this figure. Similarly, in Section 5.1, the aggregation of failure attribution accuracies into a "14–53% range" combines results from different studies (cemri2025whymas, zhang2025whoandwhen, etc.). The authors must clarify if these studies used comparable evaluation protocols and sample sizes, or if the range reflects heterogeneity in experimental design rather than a unified statistical distribution.

Finally, the discussion of "oracle adequacy" in Section 6.1 highlights the difficulty of evaluation but does not propose a statistical framework for it. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion on how future evaluations should report uncertainty (e.g., bootstrapped confidence intervals for pass rates) to distinguish between genuine system improvements and random variance. Until these statistical details are clarified or the claims are qualified as "reported point estimates," the quantitative rigor of the survey remains insufficient for a definitive scientific assessment.
