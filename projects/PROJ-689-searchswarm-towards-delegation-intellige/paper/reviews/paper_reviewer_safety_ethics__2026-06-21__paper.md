The manuscript introduces SearchSwarm, a 30 B parameter agentic LLM that delegates token‑intensive sub‑tasks to sub‑agents (Sec 2.1, Fig. 2). While the technical contributions are clear, the paper lacks a thorough safety and ethics analysis, which is essential for any system that autonomously accesses the web, generates citations, and is released publicly (Sec 7).

**Dual‑use concerns.** The ability to orchestrate multiple sub‑agents that browse, run code, and produce citation‑grounded reports can be repurposed to fabricate plausible‑looking research papers or policy briefs at scale. The authors do not discuss how the model might be weaponized for misinformation, nor do they provide usage restrictions or a responsible‑use policy. Given the reported performance on benchmarks such as BrowseComp and Open‑ended research tasks (Tables 1‑2), the model is sufficiently capable to influence downstream users who may trust its citations without verification.

**Citation authenticity.** Principle 4 of the harness (Sec 2.2) requires sub‑agents to attach inline citations to every substantive claim. However, the filtering step only discards “hallucinated citations” after the fact; there is no systematic verification that the retained citations correspond to the content of the linked pages. This opens a pathway for subtle misinformation where a citation points to a source that does not actually support the claim.

**Privacy and data‑protection.** The system performs unrestricted web searches (tools `search`, `visit`, `google_scholar`). No mention is made of handling personal data that may appear in search results, nor of respecting robots.txt or copyright constraints. Releasing the model weights and code without explicit privacy safeguards could lead to large‑scale scraping of copyrighted or personally identifiable information.

**Mitigation and governance.** The conclusion (Sec 7) states that “All code, data, and model weights are released,” but provides no licensing terms, usage monitoring, or community‑driven oversight mechanisms. Best practice would include a responsible‑use license, rate‑limiting recommendations, and a clear channel for reporting abuse.

**Recommendations.** The authors should add a dedicated “Safety and Ethics” section (or expand the existing discussion) that (i) outlines dual‑use risks and mitigation strategies, (ii) describes a concrete citation‑verification pipeline (e.g., automated cross‑checking of URLs against claim content), and (iii) details privacy safeguards for web‑scraping activities. Including these considerations will align the work with emerging standards for responsible AI deployment and address the ethical gaps identified above.
