The manuscript frequently extrapolates beyond what its experimental evidence can substantiate. In the Introduction (Sec. 1, lines 1‑4) the authors claim that SearchSwarm “attains 68.1 on BrowseComp and 73.3 on BrowseComp‑ZH (best among comparable‑scale models)”, and later state that it “remains competitive with models over 10× larger.” However, the comparison to larger models relies on asterisks (*) in Table 1 (lines 70‑78) indicating “context‑management enabled” without clarifying whether the larger baselines were evaluated under identical settings. Moreover, many of these baselines (e.g., GPT‑5.2‑Thinking, Claude‑4.5‑Opus) are cited only via announcement papers (e.g., \citep{openai2025gpt52}, \citep{anthropic2025sonnet45}) and lack publicly available benchmark scores, making the claim of superiority unverifiable.

The “Open‑ended deep‑research” results in Table 2 (lines 115‑130) further illustrate over‑reach. While SearchSwarm is highlighted as “matching or surpassing larger closed‑source systems,” Dr Tulu clearly outperforms it on ScholarQA‑v2 (88.3 vs 79.2) and HealthBench (52.8 vs 52.8, tie) and achieves the highest average (65.6 vs 64.2). The narrative in §4.5 does not acknowledge this, presenting an inflated view of the model’s standing.

The paper also omits essential limitation discussion. The SFT data are generated entirely from the harness‑guided trajectories (Sec. 3.3), yet there is no analysis of potential bias introduced by this synthetic pipeline or its impact on out‑of‑distribution tasks. The “Generalization to Single‑Agent Setting” (Sec. 4.4) reports modest gains (52.0 → 68.1) but the authors claim the delegation‑learned reasoning “benefits even without subagents,” which overstates the effect given the relatively small absolute improvement.

Finally, the claim in the Conclusion that “All code, data, and model weights are released” lacks any concrete repository link or DOI, preventing reproducibility and violating standard transparency expectations.

To bring the manuscript into alignment with the presented evidence, the authors should (1) qualify all comparative statements with explicit notes on evaluation parity, (2) acknowledge where other models outperform SearchSwarm, (3) discuss the limitations of synthetic SFT data, (4) provide statistical significance for reported gains, and (5) supply a verifiable URL for the released artifacts. Addressing these points will eliminate the current over‑claiming and improve the paper’s scientific rigor.
