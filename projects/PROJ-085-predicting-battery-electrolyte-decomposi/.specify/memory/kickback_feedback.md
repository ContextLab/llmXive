Training a model on synthetic data to predict real-world experimental onset potentials (FR-006) creates a fundamental validity gap: the model learns the distribution of the generator, not the physics of the electrolytes. This approach cannot answer the research question of predicting actual decomposition energies.
- The plan acknowledges a 'Physics Gap' between DFT thermodynamic stability and experimental kinetic onset, yet proposes a 'linear offset regression' (bias correction) to bridge them. A simple linear offset cannot account for the non-linear kinetic barriers (activation energy) that define experimental onset. This methodological choice risks validating a model that is mathematically aligned but physically meaningless.
- The assumption that 'synthetic labels... are a valid proxy for the training target' is scientifically unsound for a project aiming to predict experimental outcomes. Synthetic labels derived from ground-state DFT ($E_{products} - E_{reactants}$) inherently lack the kinetic terms (activation energy, solvation dynamics) required to model experimental onset potentials, making the target variable definitionally mismatched with the validation target.
