The manuscript addresses a relevant application domain (industrial anomaly detection) using agentic tools and reinforcement learning. From a safety and ethics perspective, the primary concerns revolve around transparency, reproducibility, and the potential for harm in deployment scenarios.

First, the **NeurIPS Paper Checklist** (Section `NeurIPS Paper Checklist`) contains a critical inconsistency regarding open science. Item 5 ("Open access to data and code") is answered with "No," justified by the promise to release data/code "upon the publication of our paper." While common in preprints, this stance conflicts with the growing expectation for immediate reproducibility and open access in AI research. The authors should update the manuscript to specify the exact repository (e.g., GitHub, Zenodo), the license under which the code/data will be released, and a concrete timeline. Relying on a future release without a binding commitment or link reduces the paper's scientific integrity and hinders independent verification of safety claims.

Second, the **methodology** relies on "Indus-CoT" generation via a "strong teacher model" (Section `sec:limitations`). The paper does not specify which model serves as the teacher or the provenance of the training data used to generate these Chain-of-Thought examples. In the context of industrial inspection, where false negatives can lead to physical defects or safety hazards, it is crucial to disclose the source of the teacher model to assess potential biases. If the teacher model has known hallucination issues or biases in specific industrial domains, these could be amplified in the student model. The authors should add a brief disclosure in the "Limitations" or "Methodology" section identifying the teacher model and discussing any steps taken to mitigate bias in the generated CoT data.

Third, the **Safeguards** checklist item is marked "NA" with the justification that the method uses pre-trained models. This is insufficient for an agentic system that actively interacts with industrial data via tools (e.g., cropping, measuring). While the paper focuses on detection, the deployment of such an agent in a real-world setting carries risks. For instance, if the agent fails to detect a critical anomaly (false negative) due to tool limitations or model uncertainty, it could lead to the release of defective products or safety incidents. The authors should briefly address these deployment risks in the "Limitations" or "Broader Impacts" section and consider recommending a human-in-the-loop verification step for high-stakes applications.

Finally, the paper does not involve human subjects or crowdsourcing, so IRB/IACUC concerns are not applicable. However, the use of proprietary commercial models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 4) as baselines raises questions about the cost and accessibility of reproducing the results, which is an ethical consideration regarding the democratization of AI research. While not a fatal flaw, acknowledging this limitation would strengthen the paper's ethical standing.

Overall, the paper is sound but requires minor revisions to address transparency in data/code availability, teacher model disclosure, and a more nuanced discussion of deployment risks.
