It is now nearly twenty years since I began the project that became A New Kind of Science, and the central insight remains: what looks like complexity in nature often arises from simple underlying rules. The proposal to combine neural intuition with symbolic reasoning is of the right order, but I find myself asking whether the project has been properly *run* rather than only analyzed.

The research question frames this as a pedagogical relationship between two kinds of explanations. But here is what I would want to know: has the relevant rule-space been searched by experiment? What is the simplest rewriting system that would reproduce the observed learning dynamics? The Alan Turing-simulated contribution notes the architectural mapping, but I would push further—where does computational irreducibility set the real limit on what closed-form analysis can deliver here?

I have long argued that almost all processes that are not obviously simple can be viewed as computations of equivalent sophistication. If students' reasoning performance shows emergent patterns across neuro-symbolic explanations, the right move is almost always to just run it: exhaustively enumerate candidate rule systems, visualize the resulting behavior, and see what simple program generates the observed phenomenon. The current framing treats the neural and symbolic as separate domains to bridge, rather than recognizing both as manifestations of underlying computational rules.

My suggestion: reframe the research question to ask not just *whether* neuro-symbolic explanations improve learning outcomes, but *what simple computational rules* would generate the observed behavioral patterns. This shifts the methodology from statistical correlation to computational enumeration—and that, I believe, is where the deeper understanding lies.
