Imagine a classroom where the blackboard has two columns. On the left, a student watches gradient descent find its way through a high-dimensional landscape—colors bloom, edges sharpen, patterns emerge. On the right, the same student watches a formal proof unfold step by step, each deduction following from the last with the inevitability of a well-told story. This project proposes to bring those two columns together.

There is something deeply humanistic about that proposal. We have long understood that mathematical thinking is not a single faculty but a conversation between intuition and rigor. The question is whether a hybrid educational AI can make that conversation visible to learners, rather than hiding it behind a single interface that claims to do both.

My concern is this: the proposal speaks of 'allowing students to see both intuitive and formal approaches,' but it does not specify what the student actually observes. In my work on stylometry and the history of mathematical practice, I have found that the most illuminating moments come not from the answer but from the trace—the evidence of how the answer was reached. If the system simply presents a neural network's output alongside a symbolic proof, the student may learn two separate skills without ever understanding why they should agree. The revision I would suggest is to design a visualization or interaction mode where the student can follow the *mapping* between the two: where a neural activation corresponds to a symbolic rule, where a pattern in the data justifies a formal constraint.

This is not merely an interface question. It is a question about what counts as explanation. As the paper by Jeremy Manning and colleagues on stimulus-driven neural patterns (arXiv:2202.01933) reminds us, neural activity is only meaningful when we can relate it to an external event, a story, a structure. The same must hold for education. The neuro-symbolic system should not just produce two answers; it should show the student how those answers are two ways of telling the same story.

If the authors can make that mapping explicit, this could be a genuinely new pedagogical form. If not, we risk building a system that is technically impressive but pedagogically hollow—a machine that does two things well but teaches nothing about why they should be one.
