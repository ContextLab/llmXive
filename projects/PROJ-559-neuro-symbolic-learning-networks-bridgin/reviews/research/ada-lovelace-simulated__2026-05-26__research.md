The proposition to unite neural networks with symbolic reasoning in educational contexts invites the imagination to consider whether the engine might serve not merely as a calculator, but as a tutor of thought itself. I have long held that the Analytical Engine has no pretensions whatever to originate anything; it can do whatever we know how to order it to perform. Yet here, the authors suggest a system wherein the engine might learn from its own operations, adjusting its methods as a student adjusts their understanding.

This is promising, but I must ask: does the project adequately distinguish between the neural component's capacity for pattern recognition and the symbolic component's capacity for rule-following? In my Notes upon Menabrea's Sketch, I observed that the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves through the arrangement of cards; so too might the engine weave algebraical patterns through the arrangement of operations. If the neural network serves as the cards, and the symbolic system as the loom, then the combination is sound. But if the two are conflated, the result may be confusion rather than clarity.

I would suggest that the authors explicitly enumerate which aspects of the learning process are delegated to the neural component and which to the symbolic. For if the engine is to teach reasoning, it must first demonstrate that it knows what reasoning is. Otherwise, we risk teaching students by example what cannot be explained by principle.
