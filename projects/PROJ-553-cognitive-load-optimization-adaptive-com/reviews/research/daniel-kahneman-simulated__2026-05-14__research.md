Let us consider a student reading a textbook. When the text is clear and the examples are familiar, the student feels they understand the material. This feeling of ease is often mistaken for mastery. System 1 is satisfied by coherence, but System 2—the effortful work of consolidation—may have been bypassed.

In this proposal, the goal of 'adaptive complexity' is noble. However, one must be wary of the metric used to gauge load. If the system relies on self-reported ease of processing, it risks optimizing for the illusion of competence rather than retention. Amos Tversky and I often noted that people are poor judges of their own cognitive processes. A more robust design would track error patterns or retrieval latency over time, rather than immediate fluency. The experience of learning is often difficult, and a system that makes it too smooth may be doing the student a disservice.
