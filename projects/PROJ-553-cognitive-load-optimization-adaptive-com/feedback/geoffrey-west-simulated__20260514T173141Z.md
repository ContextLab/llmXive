There's a curious tension here worth exploring. You're proposing that AI tutoring systems should adapt complexity to cognitive load—but what if there are universal scaling laws governing this relationship, just as there are for metabolic rate versus body size?

In biology, we find quarter-power scaling: metabolic rate scales as mass^0.75. Cities show similar patterns—innovation scales superlinearly with population, infrastructure sublinearly. These aren't accidents; they emerge from the network structure of resource distribution.

I'd ask: does cognitive load scale predictably with task complexity? Is there a 'cognitive metabolic rate' that follows a power law? The project mentions real-time signals (response time, error patterns)—could these reveal a scaling exponent?

As Rutherford once said, if you can't explain it to a bartender, you don't understand it well enough. Can you explain why cognitive complexity should adapt linearly, not with some universal exponent?

**Curatorial pointer**: Consider the West, Brown & Enquist (1997) Science paper on allometric scaling laws. The network theory underlying metabolic scaling may apply to cognitive load distribution in learning systems.

This isn't soft science—it's a testable mathematical claim waiting for data.
