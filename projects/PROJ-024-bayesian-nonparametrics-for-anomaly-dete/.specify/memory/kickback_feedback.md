## Unresolved concerns

- The plan proposes using ADVI (variational inference) to estimate the posterior mean of the concentration parameter ($lpha$) and its derivative ($\dot{lpha}$) in small sliding windows (n=30). ADVI is known to underestimate posterior variance and can be unstable for non-conjugate, non-parametric models like DP-GMM, especially with limited data. Relying on the *derivative* of a potentially biased point estimate as the primary 'early-warning' signal risks high false-positive rates due to inference noise rather than true regime shifts. The plan lacks a robustness check (e.g., comparing ADVI results against a small-scale MCMC subset or using a more stable estimator) to validate that the observed $\dot{lpha}$ signal is not an artifact of the variational approximation.
- The power analysis assumes a target of ≥10 anomalies to achieve 80% power for a paired t-test (Cohen's d=0.5). However, the plan defines 'time-to-detection' as a discrete step count. If the detection latency is often 0 or 1 step (immediate detection), the distribution of differences will be heavily skewed and non-normal, violating the t-test assumptions. The plan mentions a fallback to bootstrap resampling if anomalies <10, but does not address the distributional shape of the detection time differences when anomalies are ≥10. A non-parametric test (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) or a permutation test on the detection times should be the primary method, with the t-test as secondary, to ensure causal validity of the 'earlier detection' claim.
- The plan relies on a synthetic data generator as the primary source because verified real-world datasets with regime shifts are missing. While this ensures ground truth, the 'early-warning' hypothesis (that $\dot{lpha}$ spikes *before* the anomaly) is specific to the dynamics of the regime shift. If the synthetic generator injects anomalies as abrupt point changes or simple mean shifts without simulating the *precursor* dynamics (e.g., increasing variance, changing autocorrelation) that would theoretically cause the DP-GMM concentration parameter to fluctuate, the experiment will fail to validate the hypothesis even if the code works. The synthetic generator design must explicitly model the 'pre-anomaly' regime dynamics to test the 'early-warning' claim; otherwise, the study only tests detection of the anomaly itself, not the warning signal.
- The assumption that 'the rate of change of $lpha$ is a valid and observable proxy' is the core scientific claim. However, in a DP-GMM, $lpha$ controls the expected number of clusters. In a short window (n=30), the posterior for $lpha$ is often dominated by the prior or the data's inherent noise, leading to high variance in the estimate. The plan does not account for the 'signal-to-noise' ratio of the $\dot{lpha}$ metric. Without a simulation study (power analysis) specifically on the *variance* of the $\dot{lpha}$ estimator under the null hypothesis (normal data), the threshold for 'spike' detection (e.g., >3 std devs) is arbitrary and may not control the false discovery rate.
- FR-017 requires validation against a 'dataset with known real-world regime shifts (e.g., documented grid failure)'. The plan explicitly states in 'Dataset Strategy' that 'NO verified source found' for such datasets and resolves to use 'Synthetic Data' as the primary source with a fallback to 'dunzing/ARIMA-Date-Prediction' (which is text/date prediction, not regime shifts). The plan does not define a concrete task or data source to satisfy FR-017, leaving this requirement unbacked.
- FR-015 mandates a 'statistical test (KS test) for distribution comparison of baseline metrics'. The plan's 'Statistical Testing & Power' section lists 'Paired t-test' (FR-006) and 'Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) Test' for DP-GMM signatures (FR-010), but does not explicitly mention performing a KS test on the *baseline* metrics (GMM/ARIMA) as required by FR-015. The plan focuses on comparing DP-GMM to baselines via t-test on time-to-detection, potentially missing the specific distributional comparison of baseline scores.
- FR-011 and FR-012 require a 'power analysis check' and a 'switch to non-parametric bootstrap resampling' if anomalies < 10. The plan mentions 'Power Analysis' in the 'Statistical Testing & Power' section but describes it as a target (≥10 anomalies) and a fallback to bootstrap. However, it does not explicitly detail the *implementation* of the power analysis check logic or the bootstrap switch mechanism in the 'Methodology' or 'Sensitivity Analysis' sections, leaving the specific task of 'checking' and 'switching' ambiguous in the plan.

- The plan validates the 'early-warning' hypothesis by comparing the rate of change of the concentration parameter ($\dot{lpha}$) against synthetic anomaly injection timestamps. However, $lpha$ in a DP-GMM is a global parameter governing the expected number of clusters. In a sliding window of size 30, $lpha$ is heavily dominated by the prior and the limited data, making its posterior mean a high-variance estimator of local distributional complexity. The plan assumes $\dot{lpha}$ will exhibit 'high-frequency oscillations' specifically at regime shifts. This is not a standard result in Bayesian nonparametrics; $lpha$ typically adapts slowly to new data. The validation target (oscillation at injection) is not independent of the model's structural assumption that $lpha$ tracks regime complexity. If the model fails to adapt $lpha$ quickly enough (due to the small window size), the metric will be flat, and the 'validation' will simply confirm the model's inability to track, not the absence of an early warning signal. The analysis risks being circular: it tests if a parameter that is mathematically constrained to be stable in small samples can detect rapid changes.
- The acceptance criteria define a 'distinct high-frequency oscillation' in $\dot{lpha}$ with specific frequency (>0.1 Hz) and amplitude (>3 std) as the ground truth for anomaly detection. This creates a tautological validation loop. The plan defines the 'early-warning signal' as a specific statistical behavior of the metric itself, then validates the metric by checking if it exhibits that behavior. This is not an empirical test of whether $lpha$ dynamics *predict* anomalies; it is a test of whether the model *produces* the expected artifact. The 'ground truth' should be the injection timestamp, and the metric's behavior should be measured against it without pre-defining the specific waveform (oscillation) as the success condition. The plan conflates the definition of the signal with the validation of the signal.
- The plan proposes using a paired t-test on 'time-to-detection' values across datasets. However, 'time-to-detection' is a derived metric that depends on the threshold selection (FR-007). If the threshold is swept to optimize performance (as implied by the sensitivity analysis), the resulting time-to-detection values are not independent samples but optimized outcomes. Using a t-test on optimized thresholds without correcting for the multiple comparisons inherent in the threshold sweep (Bonferroni/Holm) inflates Type I error. The plan mentions Bonferroni correction for multiple tests but does not explicitly address the dependency introduced by threshold optimization on the same data used for the t-test.
- The assumption that 'the rate of change of the concentration parameter $lpha$ is a valid and observable proxy for the early-warning signal' is a strong, unverified claim. In standard DP-GMM theory, $lpha$ controls the *expected number of components*, not the *rate of change* of the distribution. A regime shift might be better captured by the variance of component weights or the number of active components, not necessarily $lpha$ itself. The plan treats $\dot{lpha}$ as a known proxy without citing literature that supports this specific relationship for time-series regime shifts. This is a category error: applying a global hyperparameter's dynamics as a local anomaly detector without theoretical justification.
