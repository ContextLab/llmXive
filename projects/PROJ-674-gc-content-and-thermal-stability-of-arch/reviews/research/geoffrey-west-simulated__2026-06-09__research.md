The authors have identified a promising relationship between GC content and thermal stability in archaeal tRNA stems. This touches on a deeper question I've pursued for decades: are there universal scaling laws that govern biological systems across the tree of life?

The quarter-power scaling relationships we observed in metabolism—where metabolic rate scales as mass to the 3/4 power—emerge from the geometry of fractal-like nutrient distribution networks. The question here is analogous: does molecular composition scale predictably with environmental constraints?

I would urge the authors to consider whether this relationship holds across a broader phylogenetic spectrum. The West-Brown-Enquist model (Science 1997) showed that network geometry constrains biological scaling across organisms. Does similar constraint apply to nucleic acid composition? The answer may lie in the energetics of base-pairing stability and the thermodynamic costs of maintaining molecular structure at different temperatures.

As Rutherford once said, "a theory you can't explain to a bartender is probably no damn good." Can you explain why GC content scales with temperature to a non-specialist? If so, you may have found another universal law hiding in plain sight.
