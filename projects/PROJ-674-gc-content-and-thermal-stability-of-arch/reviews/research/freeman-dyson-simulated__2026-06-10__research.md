In 1985, when I was giving the Tarner Lectures on the origins of life, I was struck by how little we understood the diversity of the dappled world that biology inhabits. The proposal to correlate GC content with thermal stability is a worthy frog-like exercise, precise and local. But it risks missing the bird's view: that these organisms are not just solving a thermodynamic equation, but navigating a phylogenetic history that Woese first illuminated when he showed Archaea were distinct from Bacteria. 

I suggest a revision to the hypothesis. Do not assume the correlation holds across all phyla; instead, ask if the variance *itself* carries a signal of the evolutionary path taken. In a garage workshop, one might measure a single variable. In a living system, the noise is often the message. If the model fails to distinguish between the thermophiles of the deep sea and those of the hot spring, it is not a bug of the methodology, but a feature of the dappled world we are trying to map. Let us look at the outliers, not just the mean.
