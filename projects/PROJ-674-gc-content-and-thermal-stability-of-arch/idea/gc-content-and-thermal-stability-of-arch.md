# GC Content and Thermal Stability of Archaeal tRNA Stems

**Field**: biology

## Research question

To what extent does the GC content of double-stranded stem regions in archaeal tRNA molecules correlate with the optimal growth temperature of the host organism, independent of phylogenetic lineage?

## Motivation

Transfer RNA (tRNA) must maintain structural integrity to support translation under extreme thermal conditions. While genomic GC content is often linked to environmental temperature, the specific contribution of tRNA stem composition to thermal stability in Archaea remains under-characterized. Addressing this gap clarifies the molecular mechanisms of extremophile adaptation and the evolutionary constraints on non-coding RNA.

### What is known

- [Identity Elements of Archaeal tRNA](https://arxiv.org/abs/q-bio/0506020) — Establishes that archaeal tRNAs possess unique identity elements recognized by synthetases, confirming their distinct structural class.
- [Accuracy of translation on ribosome could be provided by a resonance of intramolecular oscillations in tRNA molecules](https://arxiv.org/abs/1312.0948) — Suggests tRNA intramolecular oscillations and structural dynamics are functionally relevant, though not specifically tied to thermal stability metrics.

### What is NOT known

No published work has quantitatively measured the GC percentage specifically within tRNA stem regions against organismal optimal growth temperature for a broad set of Archaea. Existing literature focuses on genomic-level GC trends or protein-coding adaptations rather than non-coding RNA structural thermodynamics.

### Why this gap matters

Filling this gap determines whether RNA structural stability relies on universal nucleotide composition rules or lineage-specific adaptations. This informs synthetic biology efforts to engineer stable RNA components for high-temperature industrial applications.

### How this project addresses the gap

This project extracts tRNA sequences from public databases, isolates stem regions computationally, and performs regression analysis against known growth temperatures. This directly generates the previously unavailable quantitative mapping between stem GC content and thermal tolerance.

## Expected results

A positive correlation between stem GC content and optimal growth temperature is expected, though the effect size may be moderated by phylogenetic history. A significant result would confirm GC-enrichment as a primary stabilizing mechanism; a null result would suggest alternative structural adaptations dominate in Archaea.

## Methodology sketch

- Download tRNA sequences and annotations from the GtRNAdb (public database).
- Retrieve host organism metadata (Optimal Growth Temperature) from NCBI Taxonomy.
- Filter dataset to include only Archaeal species with documented OGT > 45°C and < 45°C.
- Parse sequences using standard cloverleaf secondary structure models to identify stem positions (D-stem, Anticodon-stem, etc.).
- Compute GC percentage for each stem region and aggregate per tRNA type.
- Perform linear regression of OGT against mean stem GC content using `scipy.stats`.
- Apply Phylogenetic Independent Contrasts (PIC) if a phylogenetic tree is available for the subset, to control for shared ancestry.
- Validate significance via permutation test (shuffling GC values across species) to ensure the correlation is not due to random noise.
