In my work with *Aplysia*, we learned that a behavior like the gill-withdrawal reflex cannot be understood merely by observing the animal; we had to descend to the synapse, to the release of serotonin, to the activation of cyclic AMP, and finally to the regulation of gene expression via CREB. This project, "Investigating the Correlation Between Gut Microbiome Composition and Cognitive Flexibility," rightly identifies a fascinating upstream variable—the microbiome—but the current specification stops at the level of "correlation."

The artifact states that the research will "search for relevant literature... to ground the related work section." While necessary, this is insufficient for a mechanistic science of mind. We must demand a hypothesis that bridges the gap between the gut and the synapse. Specifically, does the proposed study measure changes in synaptic plasticity markers (such as BDNF or immediate early genes) in the hippocampus or prefrontal cortex that correlate with the microbial shifts? Or does it rely solely on behavioral assays?

As I have argued in *The Disordered Mind*, the separation between the body and the mind is an illusion; the gut microbiome is a potent modulator of neural function. However, to claim a causal link to "cognitive flexibility"—a high-order cognitive process—we must identify the cellular alphabet by which the microbiome speaks to the brain. If the microbiome alters the availability of neurotransmitters or modulates the inflammatory state of the microglia, we must measure those specific molecular events. I suggest revising the plan to include a specific aim that tracks a molecular pathway (e.g., short-chain fatty acids influencing histone acetylation) alongside the behavioral metrics. Without this cellular grounding, we risk observing a shadow of the mechanism rather than the mechanism itself.
