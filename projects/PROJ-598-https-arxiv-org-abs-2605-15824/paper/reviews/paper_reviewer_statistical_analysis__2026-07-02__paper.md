The manuscript presents a novel framework for interactive garment customization, but the statistical rigor of the evaluation section requires significant strengthening to support the quantitative claims.

First, the primary evaluation of garment consistency relies on three metrics (HGC, LGC, NTP) generated by the vision-language model Gemini-3.0 (Appendix, "Evaluation Details"). While VLMs are increasingly used for evaluation, the paper treats their outputs as ground-truth scalar values without addressing the inherent variance or subjectivity of such models. There is no report of inter-rater reliability (if multiple prompts/models were used), confidence intervals, or statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) comparing the proposed method against the baselines in Table 1. Without these, the claim that the method "outperforms all baselines" in these specific metrics is statistically unsupported.

Second, the ablation studies in Tables 1, 2, and 3 present only point estimates (means) for various metrics. For instance, in Table 2, the difference between the best performing $\tau=0.2$ (0.4265) and the runner-up $\tau=0.5$ (0.4252) in the Cur. metric is negligible. Without reporting standard deviations, standard errors, or the results of a statistical test, it is impossible to determine if these differences are meaningful or simply noise. The authors should report variance metrics for all quantitative results and perform significance testing for ablation comparisons.

Finally, the user study described in the Appendix ("Additional User Study") aggregates 672 responses into preference percentages. The manuscript fails to apply a statistical test (such as a binomial test against a null hypothesis of 50% or a chi-square test for multiple categories) to validate that the observed preferences are statistically significant. Given the high stakes of the claims regarding "superiority," providing p-values or 95% confidence intervals for the user study results is essential.
