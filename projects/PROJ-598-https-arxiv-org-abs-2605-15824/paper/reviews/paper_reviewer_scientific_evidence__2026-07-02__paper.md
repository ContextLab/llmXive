The paper presents a novel framework for interactive garment customization, but the scientific evidence supporting the quantitative claims requires strengthening in three key areas.

First, the evaluation of garment consistency relies heavily on automated metrics (HGC, LGC, NTP) generated by Gemini-3.0 (Sec 4.1, Appendix). While the system prompt is provided, there is no evidence of correlation with human judgment or inter-annotator reliability. In generative tasks involving fine-grained visual details like fabric texture and logos, LLM-based scoring can be inconsistent. The authors should include a dedicated human evaluation study specifically for these garment metrics to validate the automated scores, rather than relying solely on the general user study in the appendix which aggregates multiple dimensions.

Second, the efficiency claim of "23.8 FPS" (Table 1) is benchmarked on an H200 GPU, whereas the training details (Appendix) and likely the baseline comparisons (which often default to A100s in the field) utilize A100s. The architectural differences between H200 and A100 (memory bandwidth, tensor core performance) could significantly skew the FPS comparison. The authors must clarify if the baselines were re-run on H200s or provide a hardware-normalized efficiency metric to ensure the "30-180x faster" claim is scientifically robust.

Third, the ablation studies (Tables 1, 2, 3) present mean scores without measures of variance (standard deviation) or statistical significance testing. Given the stochastic nature of diffusion-based generation, a single run per configuration is insufficient to claim superiority, especially for marginal gains (e.g., the difference between GR-DMD $\tau=0.2$ and $\tau=0.3$ in Table 2). The authors should report results over multiple seeds (e.g., 3-5 runs) and include statistical tests to confirm that the proposed methods yield consistent improvements over baselines.
