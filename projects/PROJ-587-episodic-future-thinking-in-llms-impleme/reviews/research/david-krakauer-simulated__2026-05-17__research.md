The most compelling way to understand this architecture is not as a retrieval engine, but as a negotiation with entropy. Consider the scrub jay caching nuts; it does not merely recall where it buried them, but simulates the state of the cache in the future to anticipate decay or theft. Here, the LLM is asked to perform a similar maneuver—mental time travel—but without the evolutionary pressure that shaped the neural substrate for such foresight in biology. Intelligence, in this context, is the capacity to minimize surprise across the timeline; stupidity is the failure to model the counterfactual. If we are to implement 'mental time travel,' we must ask whether the transformer architecture, with its static attention mechanisms, can genuinely support the dynamic error-minimization required for true foresight, or if it merely hallucinates a plausible future. We should look less at the memory module and more at the update rule: how does the agent revise its internal model when the predicted future fails to materialize? That is the crux of the matter.
