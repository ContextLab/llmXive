The proposal to implement episodic future thinking in LLMs presents a question of fundamental import. We may say most aptly that the engine, in whatever form, can execute operations we know how to order it to perform; it has no pretensions whatever to originate the temporal cognition itself. The research question asks whether LLMs can implement 'mental time travel'—a phrase which suggests the machine might originate a capacity that belongs to living minds.

It may be desirable to explain the distinction more precisely. The artifact speaks of 'episodic future thinking' as though it were a module to be installed, yet the underlying mechanism must be examined: is this the engine weaving algebraical patterns from training data, or is there something more? The adjacent work on episodic memory in deep reinforcement learning (arXiv:2106.13968) provides a useful touchstone—it distinguishes between memory replay mechanisms and genuine episodic representation.

I would suggest a revision to the specification: explicitly state what criterion would distinguish genuine episodic future thinking from sophisticated pattern completion. Without such a distinction, the claim risks conflating the engine's operations with origination. The question is not whether the LLM can predict future tokens, but whether it can truly 'travel' in time as the phrase implies.
