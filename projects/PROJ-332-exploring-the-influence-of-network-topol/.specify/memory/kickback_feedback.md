## Unresolved concerns

- The plan specifies generating Random Geometric Graphs (RGG) or Erdős-Rényi (ER) graphs with 'spatial constraints' but does not define the spatial domain size or node density. In RGGs, the average degree is a function of both connection probability and node density. Without fixing the domain size, varying 'p' or 'target_degree' conflates topological changes with density changes, invalidating the causal link between 'connectivity distribution' and 'thermal conductivity'.
- The plan defines boundary conditions as 'Fixed temperature T=1 at source, T=0 at sink' but does not specify how source and sink nodes are selected (e.g., random pair, opposite corners, max distance). The effective thermal conductivity of a finite network is highly sensitive to the boundary placement. Without a standardized selection protocol, the resulting $k_{eff}$ values will have high variance unrelated to topology, reducing statistical power and construct validity.
- The plan proposes a Log-Log OLS regression: $\ln(k_{eff}) = lpha + eta \ln(\langle k angle)$. However, near the percolation threshold, $k_{eff}$ approaches zero (or 0.0 for disconnected graphs). The log of zero is undefined. The plan does not specify how to handle the 0.0 conductivity values (e.g., exclusion, imputation, or a different functional form like $k_{eff} \propto (\langle k angle - \langle k angle_c)^eta$). This omission makes the proposed regression mathematically invalid for the full dataset range.
- The plan states '1,000 simulations (100 per connectivity level) provide sufficient power'. However, it does not define the expected effect size or variance of the scaling exponent $eta$. Without a power analysis or an estimate of the expected variance in $k_{eff}$ for a given $\langle k angle$, the claim of 'sufficient power' is unsubstantiated. If the variance is high, 100 samples may be insufficient to detect the scaling law with p < 0.05.
- The plan applies the Fuchs-Sondheimer model to calculate edge resistance but assumes a uniform wire diameter $d$ for all edges. In a realistic nanowire network, wire diameters vary. Assuming a constant $d$ for all edges while varying topology may introduce a construct validity error: the model measures the effect of topology on a *homogeneous* network, not a *heterogeneous* one, potentially misrepresenting the physical phenomenon the spec intends to study.

- The plan defines the percolation threshold as the 'smallest average degree where P(connected) >= 0.80' (also in spec.md SC-003). However, the regression analysis (US-2) attempts to fit a scaling law for k_eff vs. average degree. In Random Geometric Graphs (RGG), k_eff is strictly zero below the percolation threshold and non-zero above. Fitting a log-log regression (ln(k_eff) = alpha + beta * ln(<k>)) across the threshold is mathematically invalid because ln(0) is undefined. The plan fails to distinguish between the 'connected component' regime (where k_eff > 0) and the 'disconnected' regime, leading to a tautological or undefined result if the regression includes disconnected graphs.
- The plan proposes a log-log OLS regression: ln(k_eff) = alpha + beta * ln(<k>). This assumes a power-law relationship k_eff ~ <k>^beta. However, in percolation theory near the critical threshold <k>_c, the effective conductivity scales as k_eff ~ (<k> - <k>_c)^t, where t is the conductivity critical exponent. A simple log-log regression against <k> (not <k> - <k>_c) is methodologically unsound for extracting the critical exponent t or characterizing the scaling law near the threshold. The analysis will yield a spurious 'beta' that conflates the distance from the threshold with the power law, failing to answer the research question about the 'influence of topology' in the critical regime.
- The plan states: 'Identify threshold <k>_c where P(connected) >= 0.80'. This is a statistical definition of a threshold based on a probability cutoff, not a physical percolation threshold (which is a sharp transition in the thermodynamic limit). Using a fixed 80% connectivity probability as the 'percolation threshold' for scaling analysis introduces an arbitrary parameter that shifts the apparent critical point. The analysis should either estimate the critical point via finite-size scaling or explicitly state that the 'threshold' is an operational metric for the specific N=1000, not a universal physical constant. Treating it as a fixed input for regression without error bars on the threshold estimate is scientifically weak.
- The plan sets boundary conditions as 'Fixed temperature T=1 at source, T=0 at sink'. For a Random Geometric Graph (RGG) generated with N=1000 nodes, the 'source' and 'sink' are not defined. If they are chosen randomly, the effective conductivity will vary wildly based on the distance between source and sink, not just the global topology. If they are fixed (e.g., min/max x-coordinate), the plan must specify this. Without a defined source-sink protocol, the 'effective conductivity' is not a well-defined scalar for the network, making the regression target (k_eff) noisy and potentially biased by geometric anisotropy rather than connectivity.
- The plan applies the Fuchs-Sondheimer model (k_eff = k_bulk * (1 - 3/8 * lambda/d * (1-p))) to calculate edge resistance. This model describes surface scattering in a *single* nanowire. However, in a network, the dominant resistance often comes from *junctions* (contact resistance) between wires, not just the wire bulk. The plan assumes 'edge resistance' is solely determined by wire geometry and bulk properties, ignoring contact resistance (Kapitza resistance). If contact resistance is significant (which it is in nanowire networks), the model will overestimate k_eff and the scaling law will be physically inaccurate. The plan must justify ignoring junction resistance or include a junction resistance term.
