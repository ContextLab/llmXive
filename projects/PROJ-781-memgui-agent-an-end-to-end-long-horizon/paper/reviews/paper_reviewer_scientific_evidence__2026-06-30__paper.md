The scientific evidence presented in the paper is compelling in terms of raw performance metrics but lacks the statistical rigor required to fully substantiate the robustness of the central claims, particularly regarding the generalizability of the proposed method and the significance of the observed improvements.

**Sample Size and Statistical Significance:**
The primary ablation study (Table 2, Section 5.4) is conducted on "MemGUI-Bench-40," a subset of 40 tasks. While the reported improvements are substantial (e.g., Pass@1 increasing from 5.0% to 40.0%), the small sample size ($N=40$) makes the results highly susceptible to variance. The paper does not report standard deviations, confidence intervals, or p-values derived from bootstrapping or multiple random seeds. Without this, it is impossible to determine if the observed gains are statistically significant or if they could be attributed to the specific selection of tasks in the subset. For a claim of a "new SOTA," the evidence must demonstrate that the improvement is robust across different task samples.

**Data Bias and Teacher-Student Loop:**
The construction of the MemGUI-3K dataset (Section 4) relies entirely on rollouts generated by a single teacher model, Qwen3-VL-235B-Thinking. The filtering process (MemGUI-Eval) also likely utilizes this model or a variant thereof. This creates a potential "teacher-student" bias where the MemGUI-8B-SFT model is optimized to replicate the specific context management strategies and reasoning patterns of the 235B teacher, rather than learning a generalizable algorithm for context management. The paper claims the method "generalizes to MobileWorld," but without a control group trained on data from a different teacher architecture or a human-annotated subset, the evidence for true generalization is weak. The risk is that the model is overfitting to the idiosyncrasies of the Qwen3-VL-235B-Thinking model's output format and reasoning style.

**Experimental Reproducibility and Variance:**
The main results for the 8B model (Table 1) appear to be based on a single experimental run. In supervised fine-tuning (SFT) experiments, performance can vary significantly based on random weight initialization, data shuffling, and hyperparameter sensitivity. The absence of results averaged over multiple seeds (e.g., 3 or 5) undermines the reliability of the "best open-data 8B performance" claim. A minor revision should include a variance analysis to ensure the reported gains are consistent.

**Error Analysis Rigor:**
The failure analysis (Figure 5) presents a heatmap of failure counts, showing a reduction in "process hallucination" and "output hallucination." However, this is a descriptive statistic. To support the causal claim that the ConAct mechanism *causes* this reduction, a statistical test (e.g., chi-squared test for independence) comparing the distribution of error types between the baseline and the full ConAct variant is necessary. The current evidence is suggestive but not statistically conclusive.

In summary, while the methodology is sound and the results are promising, the paper requires additional statistical validation to move from "promising results" to "robust scientific evidence." The authors must address the sample size limitations, potential data bias, and lack of variance reporting.
