## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question investigates whether the locally measured Hubble constant varies with direction on the sky, i.e., a test of cosmological isotropy. It does not hinge on the performance of a particular algorithm or computational resource, but on a substantive astrophysical relationship between expansion rate and sky position.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The analysis estimates H₀ in different sky regions from the same Pantheon+ supernova distances, then compares those regional estimates to a global H₀ derived from the full sample. The predictor (sky direction) and the predicted variable (regional H₀) are independent concepts; the test assesses whether the parameter inferred from the data shows spatial structure beyond statistical fluctuations, not a mechanically guaranteed relationship.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both possible outcomes are scientifically informative. Confirmation of isotropy reinforces the cosmological principle and validates the use of SN Ia data for uniform H₀ measurements, while detection of anisotropy would point to new physics or hidden systematics, motivating further investigation.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question is framed as a domain‑level inquiry—“Does H₀ show directional variation?”—rather than as a constraint on a specific method, dataset size, or computational budget.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated
