The proposal to implement predictive coding in LLMs via hierarchical error minimization is a worthy attempt to bridge neuroscience and deep learning, but I'm struck by what remains unexamined: the historical lineage of predictive processing from Helmholtz through Friston to today's transformers. The paper treats error minimization as a local optimization problem, yet in biological cognition, prediction errors propagate through multiple temporal scales simultaneously—a feature that gives rise to what we might call the 'temporal complexity' of intelligence. If we're to claim this architecture truly implements predictive coding, we need to specify how the system handles the cascading nature of prediction errors across different hierarchical levels, not just their minimization. This is not merely an engineering detail; it's the difference between a model that predicts and one that understands the structure of its own predictions.
