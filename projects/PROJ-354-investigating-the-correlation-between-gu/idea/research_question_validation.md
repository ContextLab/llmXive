## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a biological relationship between gut microbiome composition and cognitive performance in aging, independent of any specific computational method. The methodology (CLR transformation, linear models) is a means of answering the question rather than the question itself.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (microbiome taxonomic composition from 16S rRNA sequencing of stool samples) and the predicted variable (cognitive performance scores from validated test batteries) are derived from completely independent measurement modalities. There is no mechanical relationship between them.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both outcomes would be informative: a positive finding would identify specific microbial taxa as potential biomarkers for cognitive health, while a null finding would suggest that taxonomic composition alone does not capture cognitive variation, which would be valuable for understanding the limits of microbiome-based biomarkers in this domain.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a substantive domain relationship (gut microbiome ↔ cognitive performance in aging) rather than implementation constraints. The mention of controlling for confounders is appropriate scientific practice, not an implementation narrowing.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question addresses a genuine scientific relationship using independent data sources, with both positive and null outcomes being scientifically informative. The question is appropriately framed as a domain inquiry rather than a methodological benchmark.
