# Energy Profiling of Quantized LLM Inference on Edge CPUs

**Field**: computer science

## Research question

How does the quantization level of a language model (FP16, INT8, 4‑bit) influence the energy‑per‑token consumed during inference on an edge‑class CPU, after controlling for model size?

## Motivation

Deploying LLMs on battery‑powered devices demands a clear understanding of the energy cost of different quantization schemes. Existing deployments trade accuracy for speed, but the energy implications are rarely quantified, leaving developers without actionable guidance for battery‑life‑constrained applications.

## Related work

- [Sustainable LLM Inference for Edge AI: Evaluating Quantized LLMs for Energy Efficiency, Output Accuracy, and Inference Latency (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.03360) — Directly measures energy, latency, and accuracy of quantized LLMs on edge hardware, providing a methodological baseline for our study.  
- [Position: LLM Inference Should Be Evaluated as Energy-to-Token Production (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11733) — Argues that energy‑per‑token is the proper metric for large‑scale deployment, motivating our choice of evaluation statistic.  
- [Compute and Energy Consumption Trends in Deep Learning Inference (2021)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.05472) — Surveys historical trends in inference energy use, offering context on why quantization may be essential for edge scenarios.  
- [Inference Acceleration for Large Language Models on CPUs (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07553) — Discusses CPU‑centric acceleration techniques and performance bottlenecks, informing our selection of hardware and software stacks.

## Expected results

We anticipate a monotonic decline in joules‑per‑token as quantization moves from FP16 → INT8 → 4‑bit, with larger models exhibiting higher absolute energy costs. Statistical confirmation will come from a one‑way ANOVA (quantization as factor) and post‑hoc pairwise tests; a significant effect (p < 0.05) will validate the hypothesis, while non‑significance would indicate that energy savings are offset by other overheads.

## Methodology sketch

- **Select models**: GPT‑Neo 125 M, GPT‑Neo 1.3 B, and LLaMA 7 B (public checkpoints).  
- **Create quantized variants**: Use `bitsandbytes`/`GPTQ` to produce FP16 (baseline), INT8, and 4‑bit weights for each model.  
- **Benchmark data**: Download WikiText‑2 from HuggingFace Datasets (`https://huggingface.co/datasets/wikitext`).  
- **Edge‑CPU environment**: Provision a Raspberry Pi 4 (ARM v8) via QEMU on the GitHub Actions runner; alternatively, run on the runner’s x86 CPU and record hardware specs for reproducibility.  
- **Energy measurement**: Install `pyRAPL` and calibrate it on the target CPU; wrap each inference call to capture joules per token.  
- **Inference procedure**: Run a left‑to‑right generation loop over the WikiText‑2 test set, recording energy, latency, and per‑token perplexity for every model‑quantization pair.  
- **Repetitions**: Execute three independent runs per configuration (different random seeds) to capture variance.  
- **Aggregation**: Compute mean ± SD of joules‑per‑token, latency, and perplexity for each condition.  
- **Statistical analysis**: Perform one‑way ANOVA on energy‑per‑token across quantization levels, followed by Tukey HSD; compute Spearman correlation between quantization bit‑width and energy.  
- **Reporting**: Generate plots (energy vs. bit‑width, energy vs. model size) and a concise guideline table for edge developers.
