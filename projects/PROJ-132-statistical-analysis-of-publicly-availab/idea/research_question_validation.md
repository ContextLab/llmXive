## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a substantive ecological relationship between bird migration phenology (timing, routes, stopover duration) and climate variables (temperature, precipitation, extreme weather). This is a domain question about how organisms respond to environmental change, independent of the statistical methods used to detect the relationship (GAMMs, mixed models, etc.).

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor's data source is climate records from NOAA/PRISM (weather station and satellite-derived measurements). The predicted variable's data source is eBird citizen-science observations (bird sightings and counts). These are independent measurement modalities with no shared primary signal, so the correlation is empirically testable rather than mechanically guaranteed.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

Both positive and null results are informative: a significant correlation quantifies climate sensitivity for specific species and supports migration phenology as a climate indicator; a null result would suggest resilience or dominance of non-climate factors (habitat loss, land use) in driving migration changes. While the general association between climate and phenology is established in literature, the species-specific effect sizes and geographic consistency remain open empirical questions.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a domain relationship (migration phenology ↔ climate variables across North America) rather than implementation constraints. The 6-hour runtime, specific statistical packages, and dataset versions appear in the methodology section, not in the research question itself.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question addresses a substantive ecological phenomenon using independent data sources, with both positive and null outcomes contributing to scientific understanding. The question is appropriately framed as a domain inquiry rather than a method-evaluation or implementation-constraint question.
