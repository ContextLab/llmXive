# Testing the Isotropy of Cosmic Expansion with Type Ia Supernova Data

**Field**: physics

## Research question

Does the cosmic expansion rate exhibit directional dependence, i.e., anisotropy, when measured with the distance‑modulus residuals of Type Ia supernovae from the Pantheon+ compilation?

## Motivation

The Cosmological Principle assumes that the Universe expands uniformly in all directions. Detecting a statistically robust anisotropy would challenge this foundational assumption and could point to new physics or unaccounted systematic effects. Existing supernova analyses have focused on the average expansion history, leaving the directional uniformity of the Hubble flow relatively under‑explored with the latest, large‑scale supernova datasets.

## Related work

- [Testing Cosmic Acceleration with Type Ia Supernovae (2001)](https://arxiv.org/abs/astro-ph/0101521) — Provides the first comprehensive supernova‑based evidence for cosmic acceleration and discusses systematic uncertainties that are relevant when interpreting residuals.  
- [Type Ia Supernovae, the Hubble Constant, the Cosmological Constant, and the Age of the Universe (2001)](https://arxiv.org/abs/astro-ph/0105413) — Explores how supernova distance measurements constrain global cosmological parameters, establishing the baseline ΛCDM model used as the isotropic reference in anisotropy tests.

## Expected results

We anticipate either (a) a dipole or higher‑order spherical‑harmonic signal in the residual map that exceeds the 95 % confidence level derived from isotropic simulations, indicating genuine anisotropy, or (b) no such signal, reinforcing isotropy at the sensitivity of the Pantheon+ sample. The key metric will be the amplitude of the dipole term (and optionally quadrupole) compared to the distribution obtained from 10 000 bootstrap‑generated isotropic catalogs.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**: Download the Pantheon+ release (≈ 1500 SNe Ia) from the official repository (e.g., `https://github.com/PantheonPlusDataRelease`).  
- **Pre‑processing**: Convert observed distance moduli to residuals relative to the best‑fit flat ΛCDM model (using the published cosmological parameters).  
- **Sky mapping**: Associate each supernova with its celestial coordinates (RA, Dec) and project residuals onto a HEALPix grid (Nside = 32).  
- **Spherical‑harmonic analysis**: Compute the coefficients \(a_{\ell m}\) up to \(\ell = 3\) and extract dipole (\(\ell=1\)) and quadrupole amplitudes.  
- **Simulation of isotropic catalogs**: Generate 10 000 mock catalogs by randomising sky positions while preserving the original redshift distribution and measurement uncertainties.  
- **Bootstrap resampling**: For each mock catalog, repeat the spherical‑harmonic decomposition to build the null distribution of dipole/quadrupole amplitudes.  
- **Statistical assessment**: Compare the observed amplitudes to the null distributions to obtain p‑values; report significance levels and confidence intervals.  
- **Systematics checks**: Repeat the analysis after (i) removing low‑z (< 0.02) SNe, (ii) applying alternative light‑curve fitters, and (iii) masking regions of high Galactic extinction to ensure robustness.  
- **Visualization**: Produce sky maps of residuals and dipole vectors, and plot the empirical cumulative distribution of simulated amplitudes alongside the observed value.
