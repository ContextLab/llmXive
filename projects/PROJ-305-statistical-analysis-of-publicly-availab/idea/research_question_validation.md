## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: concern  
The question is framed as “Can statistical disproportionality analysis and time‑series methods … identify rare adverse event signals…?” which ties the scientific inquiry to the performance of a specific analytic pipeline. The underlying phenomenon of interest is the existence (or absence) of disproportionately reported adverse events after COVID‑19 vaccination, not the capability of a particular method.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass  
Predictor data (VAERS adverse‑event counts) and the predicted variable (identified safety signals) are derived from the same reporting system, but the signal is evaluated against external background incidence rates and non‑COVID vaccine comparators. Thus the relationship is not mechanically guaranteed.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass  
Either finding a set of statistically elevated adverse‑event categories or finding none would provide useful information for public‑health monitoring and for prioritising further epidemiological studies. The outcome is not predetermined by existing knowledge.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: concern  
The wording emphasizes the methodological capability (“Can … methods identify …?”) rather than asking directly about the epidemiological relationship between vaccination and adverse events. This narrows the question to an implementation constraint.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validator_revise  
[REVISED]What adverse event categories are reported at disproportionately high rates following COVID‑19 vaccination compared to background incidence rates and to non‑COVID vaccines, as revealed by disproportionality and time‑series analyses of VAERS data?[/REVISED]  
Reframing removes the focus on the methods’ ability and instead asks a domain‑centered question about differential reporting rates, while retaining the planned statistical approach as the means of answering it.
