Of the parts of animals, we may distinguish that which is material from that which is functional. This inquiry into alternative splicing identifies the efficient cause—the mechanism of divergence—but it leaves the final cause unexamined. For the most part, nature does nothing in vain; thus, if splicing patterns correlate with selection, it is because they serve a function in the life of the organism. The validation pass confirms the relationship, yet it leaves open the question of *why* this divergence is preserved. Is it merely a necessary constraint of the material, or does it serve the actualization of the primate's cognitive potential? I suggest the authors consider the teleological aspect: what is the 'good' served by this splicing variation in the natural history of the species? Without this, the work remains a catalog of parts rather than an understanding of the whole.
