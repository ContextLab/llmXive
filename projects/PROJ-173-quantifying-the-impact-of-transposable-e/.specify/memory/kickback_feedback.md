## Unresolved concerns

- The plan states 'Residual-based Permutation... preserves LD structure' but the implementation detail in 'Research.md' (Section 5) describes shuffling residuals of the null model. This is statistically distinct from permuting the predictor (TE) while holding covariates fixed. For a binary predictor like TE presence, shuffling residuals of the null model (Y ~ PCs) and adding them back creates a new Y that is uncorrelated with PCs, but the subsequent test (Y_new ~ TE + PCs) does not correctly simulate the null hypothesis of 'no TE effect' while preserving the correlation between TE and PCs. The correct method for a binary predictor with covariates is the 'Freedman-Lane' or 'Kennedy' permutation (permuting residuals of the reduced model, adding to reduced fit, then refitting full model) OR permuting the TE labels *conditional* on PCs. The current description risks a biased null distribution, failing SC-005.
- The plan assumes a sample size of '~50-100 DGRP lines' to detect effects. In genetic association studies with binary predictors (TE presence) and continuous outcomes (expression), power is heavily dependent on the minor allele frequency (MAF) of the TE. If the TE is rare (e.g., <10% frequency), 50 lines may yield only 5 carriers, providing negligible power to detect anything but massive effect sizes. The plan does not specify a power analysis or a minimum MAF threshold for inclusion beyond the 'monomorphic filter' (5%). Without a power calculation or a strategy to ensure sufficient carrier counts, the study may be underpowered to answer the research question.
- The plan defines 'TE-Gene Pair Definition' with a 5kb window and flags ambiguous pairs. However, it does not address the multiple testing burden introduced by the 'ambiguous' pairs. If a single TE is tested against 3 genes due to proximity, the effective number of tests increases. The Benjamini-Hochberg correction (FR-005) is applied across 'all tested pairs', but the plan does not clarify if the 'ambiguous' flagging strategy implies a hierarchical testing procedure or if the simple BH correction is sufficient given the spatial correlation of TEs. This could lead to inflated false discovery rates if the dependency structure is not accounted for.
- The plan excludes TEs with frequency <5% or >95% (FR-008). While this avoids unstable estimates, it systematically removes rare TE insertions, which are often the most biologically interesting for recent transposition events. The research question 'Quantifying the Impact of Transposable Element Activity' implies a need to capture the full spectrum of activity. Excluding rare variants biases the results toward common, potentially older insertions, potentially missing the most dynamic regulatory impacts. The plan should justify this exclusion as a power constraint rather than a biological filter, or propose a sensitivity analysis including rare variants with appropriate modeling (e.g., burden tests).

- The plan states: 'If raw FASTQ is provided, the pipeline will invoke TEtranscripts... If pre-quantified data is provided, it will validate the source.' This creates a circular validation risk. - The plan describes 'Residual-based permutation' but the implementation logic is flawed for this specific design. It states: 'Fit null model: gene_expression ~ PC1 + PC2 + PC3... Shuffle residuals... Refit full model with shuffled response.' This approach shuffles the *response* variable's residuals. However, the predictor of interest is 'TE_presence'. To preserve the LD structure between TE_presence and the PCs (as required by SC-005), one must shuffle the *TE_presence* labels relative to the residuals, or permute the residuals of the *full* model under the null hypothesis that TE has no effect. Simply shuffling the response residuals and refitting with the *original* TE labels does not break the correlation between TE and the outcome if the TE signal is strong, nor does it correctly simulate the null distribution of the TE coefficient. The correct method for preserving covariate structure while testing a specific predictor is to permute the predictor (TE) relative to the residuals of the null model (Freedman-Lane) or permute the residuals of the full model (Kennedy). The current description risks generating a biased null distribution that fails to account for the specific correlation between TE and PCs.
- The plan defines the association model as `gene_expression ~ TE_presence + PC1 + PC2 + PC3`. However, in DGRP, TE insertions are often in linkage disequilibrium (LD) with nearby SNPs. If the PCs are derived from genome-wide SNPs, they may not fully capture the local LD structure around the TE. More critically, if the TE insertion itself is a major driver of population structure (e.g., a TE family fixed in a specific subpopulation), the TE presence variable may be highly collinear with the PCs. The plan mentions VIF > 5 as a flag, but does not address the statistical power loss or the potential for over-correction (removing the true signal) if the TE is a primary component of the population structure. The methodology should explicitly consider whether to include PCs or use a mixed-model approach (e.g., EMMAX/REML) which is standard for DGRP to handle both population structure and relatedness, rather than just fixed-effect PCs.
- The plan excludes TEs with frequency <5% or >95% (monomorphic filter). While this avoids unstable estimates, it introduces a selection bias in the final results. The reported 'significant' associations will only be from TEs with intermediate frequency. The plan does not state how this selection bias affects the interpretation of the 'impact of TE activity' on a genome-wide scale. The success criteria (SC-001) claims to measure the number of significant associations, but this number is conditional on the frequency filter. The methodology should clarify that the results apply only to 'polymorphic TEs with intermediate frequency' and not to the total TE landscape.
