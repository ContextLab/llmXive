# The Influence of Chatbot Politeness on User Trust

**Field**: psychology  

## Research question

Does higher linguistic politeness in text‑based chatbot responses lead to higher user‑reported trust in the chatbot?

## Motivation

Understanding which conversational cues foster trust is crucial for designing safe and effective conversational agents. While prior work has examined trust drivers broadly, the specific role of politeness—a subtle yet controllable linguistic feature—remains under‑explored. Clarifying this relationship would inform design guidelines for trustworthy AI assistants across domains such as health, finance, and education.

## Related work

- [Why do we Trust Chatbots? From Normative Principles to Behavioral Drivers (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.08707) — Reviews theoretical foundations of chatbot trust and identifies linguistic factors as potential drivers, highlighting a gap in empirical tests of specific cues like politeness.  
- [Evaluating Chatbots to Promote Users' Trust -- Practices and Open Problems (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.05680) — Surveys evaluation methods for chatbot trust and notes the need for systematic studies linking dialogue attributes to trust outcomes.  
- [How should my chatbot interact? A survey on human‑chatbot interaction design (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.02743) — Provides a taxonomy of interaction design dimensions, including politeness, but does not quantify its impact on trust.  
- [Trust as a Situated User State in Social LLM‑Based Chatbots: A Longitudinal Study of Snapchat's My AI (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.22417) — Shows that trust evolves over time in real‑world chatbots; offers a methodological template for measuring trust longitudinally.  
- [LunaAI: A Polite and Fair Healthcare Guidance Chatbot (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.18444) — Demonstrates that politeness improves perceived fairness in a healthcare chatbot, suggesting that politeness may also boost trust.

## Expected results

We anticipate a positive association between the politeness score of chatbot utterances and users’ post‑interaction trust ratings. Confirmation will be a statistically significant (p < 0.05) positive coefficient for politeness in mixed‑effects regression after controlling for conversation length and user demographics. A null or negative finding would indicate that politeness alone does not drive trust, guiding future work toward alternative cues.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**  
  - Download Persona‑Chat (`https://github.com/facebookresearch/ParlAI/tree/master/projects/personachat`) and EmpatheticDialogues (`https://github.com/facebookresearch/EmpatheticDialogues`) datasets, which include human‑bot dialogues and Likert‑scale trust/quality ratings.  
- **Politeness annotation**  
  - Apply an open‑source politeness classifier (e.g., the “Politeness‑BERT” model ≤ 100 MB) to each chatbot utterance; compute the mean politeness score per conversation.  
- **Trust extraction**  
  - Parse the provided trust rating fields (typically 1‑7 Likert) for each dialogue.  
- **Pre‑processing**  
  - Filter out incomplete dialogues, extreme outliers, and participants lacking demographic metadata.  
  - Standardize trust scores and politeness scores (z‑score).  
- **Statistical modeling**  
  - Fit a linear mixed‑effects model: `trust ~ politeness + conversation_length + (1|user_id) + (1|dialogue_id)`.  
  - Evaluate fixed‑effect significance via likelihood‑ratio tests.  
- **Robustness checks**  
  - Re‑run analysis using alternative politeness classifiers (lexicon‑based) to ensure results are not model‑specific.  
  - Conduct subgroup analyses by user age and gender to test for moderation effects.  
- **Computational constraints**  
  - All steps are scripted in Python, using `pandas`, `scikit‑learn`, and `statsmodels`; total runtime ≤ 45 minutes on a GitHub Actions runner (2 CPU, 7 GB RAM).  
- **Reproducibility**  
  - Store the full pipeline in a GitHub repository with a `requirements.txt` and a single `run.sh` script that performs download → feature extraction → modeling → results CSV.
