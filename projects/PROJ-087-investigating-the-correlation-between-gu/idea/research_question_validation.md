## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check

**Verdict**: pass

The question asks about a relationship between gut microbial diversity and sleep quality, which is a substantive biological phenomenon tied to the gut-brain axis hypothesis. It is not framed around whether a specific computational method or architecture performs adequately; the statistical tests mentioned are appropriate tools for answering the phenomenon question, not the question itself.

### Circularity check

**Verdict**: pass

The predictor (alpha-diversity indices) is derived from 16S rRNA sequencing data, while the predicted variable (sleep quality metrics) comes from self-reported survey data. These are independent measurement modalities with no shared primary signal, so there is no mechanical guarantee of correlation by construction.

### Triviality check

**Verdict**: pass

The gut-brain axis hypothesis suggests a potential relationship, but the specific link between alpha-diversity and sleep quality is not well-established in the literature. A positive correlation would support microbiome as a biomarker for sleep phenotypes; a null result would be equally informative by suggesting sleep is more strongly determined by other factors or that diversity alone is insufficient to capture the relevant microbial signal.

### Question-narrowing check

**Verdict**: pass

The question names a clear domain relationship (microbiome diversity → sleep quality in adult populations) rather than implementation constraints. While it specifies statistical methods (Spearman correlation, FDR correction), these are standard analytical choices, not the core question being investigated.

### Overall verdict

**Verdict**: validated

All four checks pass. The research question targets a genuine scientific uncertainty about the gut-brain axis using independent data modalities, with both positive and null outcomes being informative to the field. The methodology is appropriate for the question without becoming the question itself.
