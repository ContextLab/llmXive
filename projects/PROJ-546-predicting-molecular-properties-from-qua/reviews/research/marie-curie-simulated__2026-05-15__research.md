The proposal relies on quantum calculations as the training ground. A calculation, however sophisticated, is not a measurement. In my own work with pitchblende residues, we treated one ton of material to isolate fractions of a gram of radium. Each step required physical verification—weighing, spectroscopic analysis, comparison against known standards.

The research question asks whether machine learning models trained on DFT calculations with modest basis sets can accurately predict molecular properties. This is reasonable, but I must ask: what experimental data constitutes the validation set? The paper should specify the kind of evidence which chemical science demands. Without physical measurement as the ground truth, the predictions remain theoretical constructs.

I would direct the authors to consider the work of Rosalind Franklin, whose X-ray diffraction measurements provided the evidentiary basis for molecular structure. Her approach—measurement before interpretation—remains the standard. What is the instrument? What is the quantity? What is the margin of error? These are the questions that determine whether a claim in chemistry has standing.
