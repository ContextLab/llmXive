## Research-question validation

### Phenomenon-vs-method check
**Verdict**: pass
The question asks about the causal or correlational relationship between an environmental stimulus (ambient noise levels) and a cognitive trait (task-switching flexibility), which is a substantive psychological phenomenon. The framing explicitly separates the measurement tools (calibrated decibel logging, standardized task batteries) from the scientific question, ensuring the inquiry is not about the performance of the sensors or the software itself.

### Circularity check
**Verdict**: pass
The predictor variable is derived from objective acoustic recordings of the physical environment, while the predicted variable is derived from behavioral performance on a distinct cognitive task (reaction times and errors). These are independent data streams (physical sound vs. human motor/cognitive response) with no shared primary signal that would mechanically guarantee a relationship.

### Triviality check
**Verdict**: pass
While general noise distraction is known, the specific non-linear relationship (e.g., the "coffee shop effect" where moderate noise aids creativity/flexibility) is a debated and non-trivial hypothesis in environmental psychology. A null result would be informative by suggesting that remote work environments are robust to acoustic variability, while a positive result would refine the "sweet spot" theory for home-office design; neither outcome is predetermined by current consensus.

### Question-narrowing check
**Verdict**: pass
The question names a clear domain relationship (how noise levels affect cognitive flexibility across specific categories) rather than focusing on implementation constraints like budget, specific hardware models, or software runtime limits. The mention of "calibrated decibel logging" describes the *how* of measurement, not the *what* of the scientific inquiry.

### Overall verdict
**Verdict**: validated
All four checks pass: the question targets a genuine psychological mechanism, uses independent data sources for prediction and outcome, poses a non-trivial hypothesis with publishable outcomes in either direction, and avoids implementation-method narrowing. The project is ready to advance to initialization.
