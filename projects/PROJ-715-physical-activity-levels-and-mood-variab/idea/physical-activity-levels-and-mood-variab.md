# Physical Activity Levels and Mood Variability in Daily Life

**Field**: psychology  

## Research question

How does the amount of daily physical activity, quantified by step counts, relate to intra‑day mood variability and average self‑reported mood among college students in everyday life?

## Motivation

Understanding whether more physical movement stabilizes mood could inform low‑cost, scalable interventions for mental‑health promotion. While laboratory studies suggest exercise improves affect, evidence from naturalistic, everyday contexts remains limited. Clarifying this relationship in real‑world data will bridge the gap between controlled experiments and daily life.

## Related work

- [Analyzing Ecological Momentary Assessment Data with State‑Space Models: Considerations and Recommendations (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.03214) — Provides methodological guidance for modeling EMA time‑series, supporting our use of mixed‑effects and variance analyses on mood ratings.  
- [Characterization of mood and emotion regulation in females with PMS/PMDD using near‑infrared spectroscopy to assess prefrontal cerebral blood flow and the mood questionnaire (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.06457) — Demonstrates validated mood‑questionnaire instruments for EMA, informing our selection of mood items and reliability checks.

## Expected results

We anticipate that higher daily step totals will be associated with lower intra‑day mood variability (i.e., smaller standard deviation of EMA mood scores) and modestly higher average mood. A statistically significant negative coefficient for steps in the mixed‑effects model (p < 0.05) would confirm the hypothesis; a non‑significant or positive coefficient would indicate no stabilizing effect, both outcomes offering publishable insight into everyday activity‑mood dynamics.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data acquisition**: `wget` the StudentLife dataset (OSF DOI 10.17605/OSF.IO/…) containing smartphone‑derived step counts and EMA mood entries for ~48 participants over ~2 months.  
- **Preprocessing**  
  - Parse raw step logs into daily totals per participant.  
  - Align EMA timestamps; exclude entries with missing mood ratings.  
  - Compute for each participant‑day: (a) total steps, (b) mean mood rating, (c) standard deviation of mood ratings (intra‑day variability).  
  - Merge with covariates provided in the dataset (sleep duration, day‑of‑week, baseline affect).  
- **Exploratory analysis**: Visualize step‑mood scatterplots, check distributions, and assess multicollinearity among covariates.  
- **Statistical modeling**: Fit a linear mixed‑effects model  
  `mood_variability ~ steps + sleep_duration + day_of_week + baseline_affect + (1|participant)`  
  and a parallel model for mean mood.  
- **Model diagnostics**: Verify residual normality, homoscedasticity, and random‑effects variance; apply robust SE if needed.  
- **Validation**: Perform a leave‑one‑participant‑out cross‑validation to ensure that the step‑mood association generalizes across individuals (prediction of held‑out participant’s mood variability from their step counts).  
- **Sensitivity checks**: Re‑run models excluding weekends, and with alternative activity metrics (e.g., active minutes) to test robustness.  
- **Computational constraints**: All steps use Python (pandas, statsmodels) and run within a single‑core GitHub Actions job; total runtime expected < 30 minutes on the free‑tier runner.  
- **Output**: Generate a concise report (PDF/HTML) with effect sizes, confidence intervals, and plots, stored as an artifact of the workflow.
