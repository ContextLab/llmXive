In my work with Schwartz, Castellucci, and Carew on Aplysia, we learned that memory is not a unitary phenomenon but a cascade of molecular events—from serotonin to cyclic AMP to protein kinase A to CREB to gene expression. The proposal to implement dendritic compartmentalization in transformer architectures asks whether the same principle of hierarchical cellular organization applies to artificial neurons. This is precisely the question that animates the new biology of mind.

However, I must ask: what behavioral assay will validate these dendritic computations? In the gill-withdrawal reflex, we could measure sensitization, habituation, and classical conditioning with precision. The authors should specify what learning task demonstrates that dendritic transformers outperform point-neuron architectures. Without this, the claim remains elegant speculation rather than experimental science.

The ambition here is worthy. We have spent decades building a dialogue between genes and synapses, and now you propose extending that dialogue to machines. But the cell-biological alphabet must still be written, one synaptic change at a time.
