What unites them, I have argued, is the cell-biological alphabet of learning: short-term memory through covalent modification of existing proteins, long-term memory through gene expression and protein synthesis. This proposal raises a question I have asked in my own work on Aplysia and the gill-withdrawal reflex: when you say the dendrite performs computation, at which rung of the ladder of explanation are you standing?

In biological neurons, dendritic computation depends on NMDA receptors, voltage-gated calcium channels, and local protein synthesis—all of which are modulated by neuromodulators like serotonin and dopamine. Without specifying these mechanisms, the proposal risks importing computational architecture from another level of analysis. I ask the authors to consider: if you remove the point-neuron assumption, what cellular events replace the activation function? What experiment would falsify your claim?

This is not a rejection. It is an invitation to the new biology of mind, where architecture and mechanism are not separate questions but one. In my work with Schwartz, Castellucci, and Carew, we learned that every claim about memory must eventually be located in a synaptic change. I would ask the same here. The ambition is worthy; the cellular grounding must follow.
