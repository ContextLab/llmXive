## Unresolved concerns

- The plan proposes a paired t-test on 500 trajectories to compare 'Symbolic' vs. 'Learned' encoders. However, the 'Learned' baseline requires converting symbolic kinematic rules into text descriptions (US-3). This introduces a massive confound: the 'Learned' condition now depends on the quality of the text-to-kinematic translation, not just the encoder's geometric priors. If the translation is imperfect, the baseline fails, and the test measures translation fidelity, not architectural bias. The design must either use a ground-truth text prompt (if available) or acknowledge this translation step as a primary confound that invalidates the causal claim.
- The plan states: 'evaluate geometric consistency via COLMAP pose estimation against ground truth'. COLMAP is a Structure-from-Motion (SfM) algorithm that recovers camera pose relative to a reconstructed 3D scene. It does not output absolute world coordinates (x,y,z) in meters unless the scene scale is known. Synthetic video generated by a diffusion model has no inherent metric scale. Comparing COLMAP's relative pose (arbitrary scale) to ground-truth absolute pose (meters) is mathematically invalid without a scale-alignment step (e.g., Procrustes analysis). The plan lacks this critical alignment methodology, rendering the 'Euclidean Pose Error' metric uncomputable as described.
- The plan assumes 500 trajectories of 1-minute duration (60s) can be generated and evaluated within 6 hours on a 2-core CPU. Even with NVFP4 quantization, generating 1 minute of 720p video via diffusion is computationally intensive. If inference takes >7 minutes per minute of video (a conservative estimate for CPU-only diffusion), the total time exceeds 8 hours. The plan relies on an unstated assumption that inference is <7 min/min. Without a pilot study or explicit timing data for SANA-WM on CPU, the sample size (N=500) is likely infeasible, leading to a Type II error (inability to complete the experiment) rather than a statistical power issue.
- The plan relies on COLMAP to recover poses from 'synthetic video'. COLMAP requires distinct, trackable features. The plan mentions 'textured backgrounds' as a mitigation for low texture, but does not specify the texture generation method. If the texture is random noise, it may not be temporally consistent, causing COLMAP to fail. If the texture is static, it may not provide enough parallax for 6-DoF recovery in short sequences. The validity of the dependent variable (Pose Error) is entirely contingent on the success of the pose estimator, which is not guaranteed by the current design. A 'black box' failure mode (COLMAP returns NaN for >50% of frames) would invalidate the t-test.

- The plan proposes validating geometric consistency by comparing 'estimated poses' (via COLMAP) against 'ground truth' (synthetic kinematic equations). However, the 'estimated poses' are derived from the *generated video*, which is the output of the model being tested. If the model fails to generate a coherent video (e.g., produces noise or static), COLMAP will fail to estimate a pose (returning NaN or low confidence). The plan states: 'Frames where the pose estimator fails... MUST be excluded'. This creates a circular validation: the metric (Mean Euclidean Error) is only calculated on frames where the model *succeeded* in generating a viewable structure. If the symbolic encoder causes the model to collapse (generate black frames or noise), those failures are excluded from the error calculation, artificially lowering the mean error or making the metric undefined rather than high. The 'error' metric is not independent of the model's ability to generate features; it is a conditional metric that biases the result toward 'success' cases, invalidating the comparison of 'geometric consistency' between the symbolic and learned baselines if one baseline has a higher failure rate.
- The plan relies on COLMAP to recover 6-DoF poses from synthetic video generated by the model. COLMAP assumes the existence of distinct, trackable features across frames. The plan acknowledges 'low-texture synthetic video' as a risk and proposes adding 'textured backgrounds or noise'. However, if the symbolic encoder produces a video where the *camera motion* is not visually represented (e.g., the model ignores the condition vector and generates a static scene), COLMAP will fail to find correspondences. The 'Ground Truth' is the kinematic equation, but the 'Observed' is the COLMAP estimate. If the model output does not contain the visual evidence of the motion, the observation is not a noisy measurement of the truth; it is a non-detection. Treating non-detections as 'excluded' rather than 'infinite error' or 'failure' renders the t-test invalid for comparing the *extent* of dependency on priors, as it only measures consistency *conditional on successful feature generation*.
- The plan proposes a paired t-test on the distribution of 500 error differences. This assumes the error distribution is approximately normal and that the variance is comparable between the symbolic and learned conditions. However, if the symbolic encoder leads to a high rate of 'invalid' frames (excluded data), the effective sample size (N) for the t-test will vary per trajectory or be significantly reduced. A paired t-test on means derived from different numbers of valid frames (or with high rates of missing data) violates the assumption of paired observations. The plan does not specify how to handle trajectories where the symbolic run has 10 valid frames and the learned run has 100. The statistical method is mismatched to the likely data structure (censored/excluded data).
