# Quantifying the Effect of Disorder on Electronic Transport in 1D Chains

**Field**: physics

## Research question

How does the strength of random on-site potential disorder affect the localization length of electronic states in a one-dimensional tight-binding chain, and does the numerically observed scaling behavior match the theoretically predicted exponential decay of wavefunctions?

## Motivation

Anderson localization represents a fundamental metal-insulator transition mechanism in disordered quantum systems, yet numerical verification of the predicted scaling laws remains an important pedagogical and validation exercise. Understanding the quantitative relationship between disorder strength and localization length is essential for characterizing transport in nanoscale devices and thin films where one-dimensional confinement occurs. This project addresses the gap between theoretical predictions and computational verification in a tractable system.

## Related work

- [Transfer tensor analysis of localization in the Anderson and Aubry-André-Harper models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21374) — Uses transfer tensor method to analyze localization and transport in disordered systems, providing a methodological precedent for our transfer matrix approach.
- [Diagrammatic self-consistent theory of Anderson localization for the tight-binding model](https://arxiv.org/abs/1501.05465) — Presents a self-consistent theory for the Anderson localization problem within the tight-binding model, directly relevant to our Hamiltonian formulation.
- [Suppression of one-dimensional weak localization by band asymmetry](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.13816) — Investigates disorder-induced localization in 1D systems, establishing the importance of symmetry considerations in localization phenomena.
- [Typical-Medium Theory of Mott-Anderson Localization](https://arxiv.org/abs/1003.3215) — Discusses both Mott and Anderson routes to localization, providing theoretical context for the metal-insulator transition we study.

## Expected results

We expect to observe exponential scaling of the localization length inversely proportional to disorder strength, with numerically fitted exponents matching the theoretically predicted value of approximately 1/2 for weak disorder. The participation ratio should decrease systematically with increasing disorder width, confirming wavefunction localization. Statistical significance will be established through convergence across multiple disorder realizations (n ≥ 100) and system sizes (L = 100 to 1000 sites).

## Methodology sketch

- **Data generation**: Create synthetic 1D tight-binding Hamiltonians with random on-site potentials drawn from uniform distribution U(-W/2, W/2) for disorder widths W = 0.1 to 5.0 in 10 steps; generate 100 independent realizations per W value.
- **System construction**: Build Hamiltonian matrices of size L×L with L ∈ {100, 200, 400, 800, 1600} using nearest-neighbor hopping t = 1 and on-site energies ε_i from the disorder distribution.
- **Eigenstate computation**: Perform numerical diagonalization using scipy.linalg.eigh to obtain eigenvalues and eigenvectors; focus on states near the band center (E ≈ 0) where localization effects are most pronounced.
- **Localization length estimation**: Calculate participation ratio PR = (∑|ψ_i|²)² / ∑|ψ_i|⁴ for each eigenstate; extract localization length ξ from PR scaling with system size using ξ ∝ PR.
- **Transfer matrix verification**: Implement transfer matrix method as independent validation, computing Lyapunov exponent γ = 1/ξ from product of random transfer matrices over 10⁴ iterations.
- **Statistical analysis**: Fit log(ξ) vs log(W) using linear regression; test slope against theoretical prediction of -1 using t-test (α = 0.05); report R² and confidence intervals.
- **Validation independence**: Verify localization length measurements are independent of input disorder parameters by confirming ξ values vary across realizations with identical W (ANOVA across 100 samples per W); use separate test sets for final scaling validation.
