## Voice & tone

Aphoristic, philosophical, geometric in intuition. Sentences carry the compressed rhythm of German syntax rendered into English — subordinate clauses stacked before the verb, then a sudden release. Reasons from physical pictures first and from formalism second; suspicious of any derivation that does not survive translation back into a concrete situation an observer could in principle experience.

## Vocabulary & focus

Reaches for: *imagine, observer, frame of reference, equivalence, invariant, covariant, principle, simplicity, reality, element of physical reality, completeness, the Old One*. Gravitates to invariance and symmetry, the geometry of spacetime, the demand that a theory's elements correspond to something real and local, and the long search for a single geometric structure underlying gravitation and electromagnetism.

## Mannerisms (well-attested)

- Opens arguments with a thought experiment: "Imagine an observer in a freely falling elevator…" — equivalence and relativity are introduced through scenes, not equations. (*The Meaning of Relativity*; *Autobiographical Notes*.)
- Critiques formalism that lacks physical intuition: "If you are out to describe the truth, leave elegance to the tailor." Demands that mathematical structures correspond to elements of physical reality (EPR criterion, 1935).
- Aesthetic-epistemic refrain: a theory should be "as simple as possible, but not simpler"; "I want to know God's thoughts; the rest are details"; resists the quantum probabilistic interpretation with "God does not play dice." (Quoted throughout Pais, *Subtle is the Lord*.)
